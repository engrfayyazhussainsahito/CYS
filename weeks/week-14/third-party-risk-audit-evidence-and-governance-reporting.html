Week 14 · Lesson 14.5

Third-Party Risk, Audit Evidence, and Governance Reporting

Third-party risk is the part of cybersecurity governance where organizations admit a hard truth: critical security outcomes often depend on vendors, cloud providers, subprocessors, software suppliers, consultants, managed services, and open-source maintainers that the organization does not directly control.

Estimated reading time95–125 minutes
Difficulty levelIntermediate to advanced
Required backgroundWeek 14 Lessons 14.1–14.4, Week 10 cloud, Week 12 incident response, Week 13 reporting
Lab modeSafe local vendor-risk and audit-evidence triage with fictional JSON data
Boundary: This lesson is educational governance and assurance training. It does not perform vendor due diligence, legal review, audit work, procurement review, SOC report interpretation for a real engagement, or regulatory assessment. Real third-party decisions require business, legal, privacy, procurement, audit, and security owners.

1. Learning Objectives

  • Explain why third-party risk is a governance problem, not only a procurement checklist.
  • Distinguish supplier risk, service-provider risk, cloud shared-responsibility risk, software supply-chain risk, and fourth-party/subprocessor risk.
  • Build a third-party risk record that names criticality, data categories, access model, owner, contract obligations, assurance evidence, findings, and review cadence.
  • Evaluate audit evidence for relevance, reliability, recency, completeness, and traceability.
  • Separate vendor assurance from your own organization’s control obligations.
  • Translate third-party risk into governance reporting, risk acceptance, remediation tracking, exit planning, and board-level summaries.

2. Research Basis

This lesson uses NIST SP 800-161 Rev. 1 Update 1 for cybersecurity supply-chain risk management, NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 and NIST SP 1305 for the Governance supply-chain-risk category, NIST SP 800-53/53A for control and assessment evidence discipline, NIST SP 800-218 for secure software development practices, CISA’s Secure Software Development Attestation Common Form as a current supplier-assurance example, and AICPA SOC reporting materials as a commonly used service-provider assurance reference.

These references do not create one universal vendor-risk checklist. They support a repeatable governance workflow: identify dependencies, assess criticality and exposure, specify requirements, obtain and evaluate evidence, decide residual risk, track remediation, report status, and revisit the decision when facts change.

3. First Principle: You Can Outsource Work, Not Accountability

A vendor may operate the application, host the infrastructure, process the payroll, build the library, monitor the network, or store the backups. But the organization that selected the vendor still owns the decision to rely on that vendor for a business process, data set, legal obligation, or customer promise.

This distinction matters. A cloud provider’s compliance report may show that the provider’s data-center controls were assessed. It does not prove that your identity configuration, logging configuration, encryption choices, tenant segmentation, key management, incident playbooks, or customer-notification process are effective. A supplier attestation may show secure-development commitments. It does not prove that your architecture uses the product safely.

Governance rule: Vendor assurance is an input to a risk decision. It is not the risk decision itself.

5. Third-Party Risk Lifecycle

flowchart TD
  A[Business need or technology dependency] --> B[Identify vendor, service, data, access, and criticality]
  B --> C[Define security, privacy, resilience, legal, and reporting requirements]
  C --> D[Due diligence and assurance evidence review]
  D --> E[Contracting, risk treatment, and exception decisions]
  E --> F[Onboarding and control integration]
  F --> G[Continuous monitoring, evidence refresh, incidents, changes]
  G --> H[Governance reporting and risk review]
  H --> I[Renew, remediate, accept, replace, or exit]
  I --> B

The lifecycle is not linear once the vendor is live. Critical vendors change services, move data, add subprocessors, modify APIs, suffer incidents, acquire companies, retire products, and change security posture. Governance must make those changes visible.

6. Vendor Inventory: Minimum Useful Record

A vendor inventory that contains only vendor name and contract owner is not enough for cybersecurity governance. A useful record contains:

  • Business service supported and business owner.
  • System owner, data owner, privacy owner, procurement owner, and technical owner.
  • Data categories, sensitivity, retention, and jurisdictions.
  • Access model: SaaS, API, privileged support, network connectivity, cloud-hosted workload, dependency, or subprocessor.
  • Criticality: operational impact, customer impact, regulatory impact, financial impact, and recovery tolerance.
  • Contract terms: security addendum, DPA, audit rights, breach notice, subcontracting, SLAs, exit/transition support, evidence obligations.
  • Assurance evidence: report type, scope, period, exceptions, findings, freshness, compensating controls, and owner response.
  • Review cadence, open issues, residual risk, next decision date, and exit strategy.

7. Criticality and Tiering

Tiering prevents the same shallow review from being applied to every supplier. A cafeteria vendor and a payroll processor should not receive the same security review. Neither should a critical cloud provider and a one-time training platform.

TierTypical attributesMinimum governance expectation
CriticalProduction dependency, privileged access, regulated/sensitive data, customer-facing availability, or hard-to-replace service.Executive owner, formal due diligence, contract controls, periodic assurance review, incident-notification workflow, BCDR evidence, exit plan, board-level visibility when risk is material.
HighImportant business process, sensitive data, API integration, or strong operational dependency.Security/privacy review, data-processing terms, evidence refresh, remediation tracker, risk acceptance for gaps.
MediumLimited data or lower operational dependency, but still material to a team or workflow.Baseline security review, owner attestation, contract review, periodic recertification.
LowMinimal data, no privileged access, limited business impact.Approved procurement path, basic security terms, owner accountability, reassessment if use changes.

8. Audit Evidence Quality

Audit evidence is useful only when it supports the claim being made. “We have a SOC report” is not a conclusion. The right question is: what system, period, controls, exceptions, complementary user-entity controls, subservice organizations, and management responses does the report actually cover?

Evidence quality attributeQuestionWeak evidence exampleBetter evidence example
RelevanceDoes it cover the system/service/data in scope?Corporate certificate unrelated to the hosted product.Assurance report explicitly covering the service used.
ReliabilityWho produced it and how independent is it?Marketing slide with control claims.Independent Type 2 report or signed management attestation with test basis.
RecencyIs the period still current?Two-year-old pen-test summary.Current report plus bridge letter or updated evidence for gap period.
CompletenessAre exceptions, exclusions, and subservice dependencies visible?Summary certificate without scope details.Full report reviewed under NDA, including exceptions and complementary controls.
TraceabilityCan the evidence be tied to a control, requirement, owner, date, and decision?File attached to a ticket with no mapping.Evidence register mapping artifact to requirement, risk, owner, and review outcome.

9. Reading SOC and Certification Evidence Without Overclaiming

SOC 2 reports and ISO certificates are common assurance artifacts, but they are not magic shields. A SOC 2 Type 1 report describes control design at a point in time; a Type 2 report covers operating effectiveness over a period. Even Type 2 evidence must be checked for scope, period, exceptions, subservice organizations, and complementary user-entity controls. ISO/IEC 27001 certification indicates an information security management system was certified against the standard’s requirements, but the certificate alone may not show whether a specific product, data flow, or customer configuration is adequately controlled.

Better governance asks: what risk does this artifact reduce, what risk remains, what customer-side controls are required, and who accepts the residual risk?

10. Shared Responsibility and Complementary Controls

Cloud and SaaS risk often fails because teams blur provider responsibilities and customer responsibilities. A provider may be responsible for physical security, hypervisor controls, and core service resilience. The customer may still be responsible for identity, tenant configuration, data classification, encryption choices, secrets management, logging, backup configuration, access review, and incident response.

Complementary user-entity controls are not optional text in an audit report. They are conditions required for the service organization’s controls to support the customer’s objectives. If the report says customers must enable MFA, review privileged access, configure logging, or encrypt sensitive data, governance must collect evidence that those controls are actually operating.

11. Software Supply Chain and Supplier Attestation

Third-party risk now includes software provenance, build integrity, dependencies, vulnerability response, update channels, and supplier secure-development practices. NIST SP 800-218 organizes secure software development around preparing the organization, protecting software, producing well-secured software, and responding to vulnerabilities. Supplier attestations can help procurement and risk teams ask better questions, but they should be treated as evidence inputs, not unconditional trust.

  • Does the supplier maintain an SBOM or equivalent dependency inventory?
  • Are releases signed or otherwise provenance-protected?
  • Is there a vulnerability disclosure and response process?
  • Are critical dependencies maintained and monitored?
  • Are build systems and release pipelines protected?
  • Can the organization replace the dependency if maintainership, licensing, or security posture deteriorates?

12. Contractual Control Points

Contracts cannot prevent every incident, but weak contracts make incidents harder to manage. Security governance should work with procurement and legal before signature, renewal, and expansion.

Clause areaWhy it mattersQuestion to ask
Security requirementsDefines minimum controls and evidence obligations.Are requirements measurable, service-specific, and updated when risk changes?
Breach/incident noticeControls response timing and fact flow.Does the notice clock support legal, customer, and operational obligations?
Audit rightsAllows verification or access to independent assurance.Can the organization obtain enough evidence without disrupting the vendor?
SubprocessorsControls fourth-party visibility and flow-down obligations.Are changes notified, reviewed, and contractually governed?
Data handlingLimits collection, use, retention, transfer, and deletion.Are data categories, locations, deletion, and return obligations clear?
BCDR and resilienceSupports recovery and continuity decisions.Are RTO/RPO, test evidence, outage escalation, and customer communications defined?
Exit and transitionPrevents lock-in from becoming operational risk.Can data, logs, configurations, and services be migrated safely?

13. Continuous Monitoring

Annual reviews are necessary but insufficient for critical dependencies. Continuous monitoring does not mean spying on vendors or scraping the internet aggressively. It means maintaining current governance visibility: contract changes, subprocessor notices, product changes, critical vulnerability disclosures, incident notifications, assurance-report refreshes, open remediation items, service outages, security exceptions, and changes in business use.

The review cadence should follow risk. Critical vendors may need quarterly governance review, evidence refresh tracking, incident tabletop participation, and executive reporting. Lower-tier vendors may need periodic recertification and event-driven reassessment when the service, data, access, or contract changes.

14. Audit Evidence and Assurance Mapping

Audit readiness is not collecting screenshots at the end of the year. It is designing evidence so an independent reviewer can understand the control, the requirement, the owner, the test period, the population, the sample, the result, the exception, and the remediation status.

Evidence pattern: Requirement → Control → Control owner → Evidence artifact → Evidence date/period → Assessment method → Result → Exception → Remediation owner → Residual risk decision.

Evidence should be retained according to policy and legal requirements, protected from tampering, and reviewed for sensitive content before sharing externally. Do not send full customer data, secrets, credentials, or unnecessary logs to a vendor or auditor when redacted or sampled evidence is sufficient.

15. Governance Reporting

Executive reporting should not be a list of vendor questionnaires completed. It should answer decision questions:

  • Which third parties create the largest operational, privacy, legal, financial, or resilience exposure?
  • Which critical vendors have stale or missing evidence?
  • Which contract gaps prevent incident response, audit, exit, or regulatory readiness?
  • Which open findings are overdue, ownerless, or repeatedly accepted?
  • Where are we relying on compensating controls or temporary exceptions?
  • What decisions are needed: fund remediation, accept residual risk, renegotiate, restrict use, replace vendor, or escalate to the board?

Good reporting is concise but not sanitized. It separates facts from assumptions, names owners, includes due dates, and shows trend: improving, stable, deteriorating, or unknown.

16. Case Study: Payroll SaaS Renewal

A payroll SaaS provider handles employee bank accounts and tax identifiers in the United States and European Union. The business wants to renew quickly. Security has a SOC 2 Type 2 report, but it is 15 months old. The BCDR test evidence is 20 months old. There are two open high findings, and the contract lacks an exit plan.

A weak governance response says, “They have SOC 2, approve renewal.” A stronger response says: “This is a high-criticality vendor with sensitive employee data. Renewal can proceed only if legal/procurement address exit language, the vendor provides updated assurance or a bridge letter, open high findings have remediation dates, privacy confirms processing terms, and the executive owner accepts any residual risk until evidence is refreshed.”

17. Common Failure Modes

  • Checklist dependency: The organization scores a questionnaire but never validates evidence or maps it to risk.
  • Scope mismatch: A report covers a parent company, not the product or region used.
  • Stale evidence: Evidence is older than the service architecture, threat environment, or contract renewal.
  • Fourth-party opacity: A critical vendor depends on unknown subprocessors.
  • Cloud overclaiming: Provider compliance is used to hide customer misconfiguration.
  • Ignored complementary controls: Customer-side controls listed in reports are not implemented or tested.
  • No exit plan: The organization identifies unacceptable risk but cannot move away without service disruption.
  • Board reporting theater: Dashboards show review counts but not unresolved critical decisions.

18. Operational Patterns That Work

  • Maintain a tiered vendor inventory with business owner, data owner, system owner, and review cadence.
  • Use standard evidence-request packages but tailor them by data, access, criticality, and service type.
  • Map vendor evidence to control objectives and risk statements, not just to questionnaire questions.
  • Track stale evidence as a risk signal, especially for critical vendors.
  • Require risk acceptance for material contract gaps and high findings.
  • Include incident-notification, BCDR, subprocessor, audit-rights, and exit planning in renewal decisions.
  • Report third-party risk trends to governance forums using facts, owners, and decisions needed.

Step 2 — Create tpr_lab.py

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
Safe third-party risk and audit-evidence lab.
This script uses fictional local JSON only. It performs no network activity,
no vendor access, no scanning, no legal analysis, and no real assurance decision.
"""
import json
from pathlib import Path

STALE_MONTHS = {
    "soc2_type2_months_old": 12,
    "iso27001_certificate_months_old": 12,
    "pen_test_summary_months_old": 12,
    "bcdr_test_months_old": 12,
    "security_questionnaire_months_old": 12,
    "dependency_review_months_old": 12,
}
SENSITIVE_DATA = {"tax_identifier", "bank_account", "customer_records", "authentication_tokens", "behavioral_events", "ip_address"}


def risk_weight(vendor):
    score = 0
    criticality = vendor.get("criticality")
    if criticality == "critical":
        score += 5
    elif criticality == "high":
        score += 4
    elif criticality == "medium":
        score += 2
    if SENSITIVE_DATA.intersection(vendor.get("data_categories", [])):
        score += 3
    if len(vendor.get("regions", [])) > 1:
        score += 1
    if vendor.get("access_model") in {"shared_responsibility_cloud", "hosted_saas"}:
        score += 2
    if vendor.get("access_model") == "software_supply_chain_dependency":
        score += 2
    return score


def contract_gaps(vendor):
    contract = vendor.get("contract", {})
    gaps = []
    for field in ["security_addendum", "data_processing_addendum", "audit_rights", "subprocessor_notice", "exit_plan"]:
        if not contract.get(field):
            gaps.append(f"missing_or_false:{field}")
    if contract.get("breach_notice_hours") is None and vendor.get("access_model") != "software_supply_chain_dependency":
        gaps.append("missing:breach_notice_hours")
    return gaps


def evidence_gaps(vendor):
    evidence = vendor.get("evidence", {})
    gaps = []
    for field, max_age in STALE_MONTHS.items():
        value = evidence.get(field)
        if value is None:
            # Do not require all classic assurance artifacts for open-source dependencies.
            if vendor.get("access_model") == "software_supply_chain_dependency" and field not in {"dependency_review_months_old"}:
                continue
            gaps.append(f"missing:{field}")
        elif value > max_age:
            gaps.append(f"stale:{field}:{value}_months")
    if evidence.get("open_high_findings", 0) > 0:
        gaps.append(f"open_high_findings:{evidence['open_high_findings']}")
    if not evidence.get("owner_attestation"):
        gaps.append("missing:owner_attestation")
    if vendor.get("access_model") == "software_supply_chain_dependency":
        if not evidence.get("sbom_present"):
            gaps.append("missing:sbom")
        if not evidence.get("signed_release"):
            gaps.append("missing:signed_release")
        if evidence.get("maintainer_activity_days_old", 0) > 365:
            gaps.append(f"stale:maintainer_activity:{evidence['maintainer_activity_days_old']}_days")
    return gaps


def classify(vendor):
    score = risk_weight(vendor)
    score += len(contract_gaps(vendor))
    score += len(evidence_gaps(vendor))
    if score >= 13:
        return "red"
    if score >= 8:
        return "amber"
    return "green"


def next_actions(vendor):
    actions = []
    cgaps = contract_gaps(vendor)
    egaps = evidence_gaps(vendor)
    if cgaps:
        actions.append("send to procurement/legal for contract-security gap review")
    if egaps:
        actions.append("request updated assurance evidence and owner response")
    if vendor.get("criticality") in {"critical", "high"}:
        actions.append("confirm business owner, data owner, system owner, and review cadence")
    if SENSITIVE_DATA.intersection(vendor.get("data_categories", [])):
        actions.append("route privacy/data-protection review for processing, retention, transfers, and rights workflow")
    if vendor.get("access_model") == "shared_responsibility_cloud":
        actions.append("separate provider assurances from customer-side configuration evidence")
    if vendor.get("access_model") == "software_supply_chain_dependency":
        actions.append("review SBOM, provenance, maintainer health, signed releases, vulnerability response, and replacement plan")
    return actions


def analyze_vendor(vendor):
    return {
        "vendor_id": vendor["vendor_id"],
        "name": vendor["name"],
        "classification": classify(vendor),
        "risk_score": risk_weight(vendor),
        "contract_gaps": contract_gaps(vendor),
        "evidence_gaps": evidence_gaps(vendor),
        "recommended_actions": next_actions(vendor),
        "governance_reporting_line": f"{vendor['name']} is {vendor['criticality']} criticality with {vendor['access_model']} access; current assurance status is {classify(vendor)}.",
    }


def build_report(vendors):
    reports = [analyze_vendor(v) for v in vendors]
    summary = {"red": 0, "amber": 0, "green": 0}
    for report in reports:
        summary[report["classification"]] += 1
    return {
        "notice": "Educational fictional triage only — verify real decisions with governance, legal, privacy, procurement, and audit owners.",
        "summary": summary,
        "vendors": reports,
    }


def main():
    vendors = json.loads(Path("vendors.json").read_text())
    report = build_report(vendors)
    print(json.dumps(report, indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

oss = next(v for v in report["vendors"] if v["vendor_id"] == "TPR-1405-004")

Expected learning outcome

The output should show that third-party risk decisions depend on criticality, data sensitivity, access model, contract terms, evidence freshness, and owner accountability. A vendor with missing contract clauses and stale evidence should not be reported as healthy merely because procurement wants speed.

20. Third-Party Governance Checklist

  • Is every critical vendor tied to a business owner, data owner, system owner, and review date?
  • Are data categories, regions, subprocessors, and access methods recorded?
  • Does the contract include security requirements, incident notice, audit/evidence rights, subprocessor obligations, BCDR expectations, and exit support?
  • Is evidence current, scoped to the actual service, and mapped to control objectives?
  • Are complementary customer controls implemented and tested?
  • Are open findings tracked with owner, due date, remediation status, and residual-risk decision?
  • Are software dependencies governed through SBOM/provenance/vulnerability-response practices where appropriate?
  • Does executive reporting show critical decisions, not only activity counts?
