Week 2

Operating Systems, Identity, and Access Control

Week 2 explains how computers enforce security decisions: who you are, what you can access, which programs run, what privileges they have, and what evidence is recorded. Lesson 2.5 completes the week with privilege, services, logs, audit trails, hardening baselines, configuration drift, and safe local hardening review.

Estimated reading time12–18 hours
Difficulty levelBeginner → MS foundation
Required backgroundWeek 0 and Week 1 recommended
Major outputOS hardening and access-control lab report

Overview

This week starts from operating-system fundamentals and gradually builds toward identity, authentication, authorization, file permissions, privilege, logging, and system hardening. Lesson 2.5 connects privilege, running services, exposed ports, logging, auditability, and system hardening into a practical defensive workflow.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain what operating systems do and why they are security-critical.
  • Distinguish users, groups, service accounts, permissions, privileges, and roles.
  • Explain authentication, authorization, access control, and accountability.
  • Inspect local users, processes, files, services, and logs in a safe lab environment.
  • Apply least privilege and basic hardening practices.
  • Produce a documented OS and access-control review for a fictional lab system.

Required Background

No operating-system or identity-management background is required. Week 0 is recommended because students will use Linux commands, simple files, and lab documentation. Week 1 is recommended because OS access control is easier to understand after assets, risk, threat modeling, and ethics.

Lessons

  1. Lesson 2.1: Operating Systems from First Principles ready
  2. Lesson 2.2: Users, Groups, and Identity ready
  3. Lesson 2.3: Authentication and Password Security ready
  4. Lesson 2.4: Authorization, Permissions, and Access Control Models ready
  5. Lesson 2.5: Privilege, Services, Logs, and System Hardening ready

Week 2 Output

By the end of Week 2, students will produce a safe lab report that documents local OS identity, permissions, services, and logs, then recommends least-privilege improvements for a fictional classroom server.
