Week 11 · Lesson 11.4 · SOC Investigation

Alert Triage, Investigation, and Threat Hunting

Triage is the disciplined conversion of a noisy alert into an evidence-based decision: close, monitor, investigate, escalate, or hand off to incident response.

Estimated reading time75 minutes
Difficulty levelIntermediate
Required backgroundLessons 11.1–11.3
Practice time90–120 minutes

1. Introduction

Previous lessons built the pipeline: telemetry enters the SIEM, detection rules create alerts, and analysts receive work. Lesson 11.4 focuses on what happens next. The analyst must decide whether an alert is harmless noise, a weak signal worth monitoring, a case needing investigation, or an incident requiring coordinated response.

The failure mode to avoid is jumping from an alert title directly to a conclusion. An alert is not proof. It is a claim generated by a rule or tool. Triage tests that claim against evidence: who acted, what changed, which system was affected, what other sources corroborate the activity, what business context explains it, and what action is authorized.

Threat hunting extends this mindset. Instead of waiting for an alert, the hunter forms a testable hypothesis about adversary behavior and searches existing telemetry for evidence. A good hunt either finds suspicious activity, disproves the hypothesis in the available data, or exposes a visibility gap that should become an engineering task.

2. Learning Outcomes

  • Differentiate alert triage, investigation, incident response, and threat hunting.
  • Classify alerts using consistent dispositions: true positive, false positive, benign true positive, duplicate, insufficient evidence, and escalation candidate.
  • Build a timeline from identity, endpoint, network, cloud, and change-management evidence.
  • Separate severity, confidence, business impact, and urgency.
  • Write concise analyst notes that distinguish observed facts from inferences.
  • Escalate safely without taking unauthorized containment actions.
  • Create a small hypothesis-driven hunt against fictional local JSONL data.
  • Turn triage outcomes into detection tuning and telemetry-improvement tasks.

3. Research Anchors Used for This Lesson

This lesson is a teaching translation of commonly accepted defensive operations guidance, not a replacement for an organization's incident-response plan. The main references are:

  • NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 3, which frames incident response recommendations as part of cybersecurity risk-management activities and CSF 2.0.
  • CISA Cybersecurity Incident and Vulnerability Response Playbooks, which emphasize standard procedures to identify, coordinate, remediate, recover, and track mitigations.
  • NIST SP 800-92, which remains useful for log-management infrastructure, collection, retention, and operational process design.
  • MITRE ATT&CK, which organizes real-world adversary tactics and techniques and provides a useful vocabulary for detection, triage, and hunting.
  • Microsoft Sentinel hunting documentation and Splunk's PEAK hunting material, used only as examples of structured hunting workflow patterns.
Currency note: Older incident-handling material may still teach useful concepts, but this lesson points students to NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 3 because Rev. 2 has been superseded.

4. Authorization and Privacy Boundary

Defensive scope only: Use this lesson with fictional data or systems where you have explicit authorization. Do not access real logs, user records, cloud audit trails, endpoint telemetry, or network captures unless your role, policy, and legal basis permit it.

Security operations often touches sensitive data: usernames, IP addresses, device names, browser activity, authentication failures, file paths, email metadata, and cloud-resource names. Treat triage notes as evidence records. Write only what is needed, avoid speculation about people, and follow retention and access-control requirements.

5. The Triage-to-Hunt Workflow

flowchart TD
  A[Alert or hunt lead] --> B[Validate alert metadata and data quality]
  B --> C[Identify actor, asset, action, time, source]
  C --> D[Collect corroborating evidence]
  D --> E[Build timeline]
  E --> F[Assess severity, confidence, impact, urgency]
  F --> G{Disposition}
  G -->|False positive / benign true positive| H[Close with rationale and tuning note]
  G -->|Insufficient evidence| I[Request telemetry or monitor]
  G -->|Suspicious but bounded| J[Open investigation case]
  G -->|Likely active compromise| K[Escalate to incident response]
  H --> L[Improve detections and documentation]
  I --> L
  J --> L
  K --> L

The loop matters. Every alert outcome should improve the system: tune a rule, fix a parser, add context, update a playbook, clarify escalation criteria, or document a known benign pattern.

6. Triage, Investigation, Incident Response, and Threat Hunting

ActivityPrimary questionTypical output
Alert triageDoes this alert require action?Disposition, severity/confidence, case note, escalation decision.
InvestigationWhat happened, how far did it go, and what evidence supports that?Timeline, scope, affected entities, evidence package, recommended next actions.
Incident responseHow do we contain, eradicate, recover, communicate, and learn?Coordinated response plan, containment actions, recovery validation, post-incident review.
Threat huntingCould this adversary behavior exist even without an alert?Findings, disproven hypothesis, visibility gap, new detection idea, or tuned rule.

The boundary between these activities is organizational. In a small team, the same person may do all four. In a large enterprise, triage may be Tier 1 SOC work, investigation Tier 2/3 work, incident response a separate function, and threat hunting a specialized team. The method still depends on evidence, scope, and authorized action.

7. Alert Dispositions

A disposition is the analyst's final classification for the alert or case. Consistent dispositions make metrics and tuning meaningful.

DispositionMeaningExample
True positive maliciousThe detection identified malicious or unauthorized activity.Compromised account used to add a new privileged user.
Benign true positiveThe rule correctly detected the behavior, but the behavior was approved or expected.Vulnerability scanner triggered exploit-attempt signatures during an approved test window.
False positiveThe rule fired even though the target behavior did not occur.A parser mapped a normal script name into the wrong field.
DuplicateSame event already exists in another case.EDR and SIEM created separate alerts for one blocked file.
Insufficient evidenceThe available data cannot support a confident decision.Network alert exists, but endpoint and identity logs are missing.
Escalation candidateThe evidence suggests possible compromise or material risk.MFA fatigue followed by privileged action on a high-criticality asset.

8. The Five-Minute Triage Questions

First-pass triage should be fast but not careless. The analyst should answer:

  1. What exactly fired? Read rule name, logic summary, fields, severity, and known false positives.
  2. What entity is involved? Identify user, host, service account, IP, cloud resource, application, or data store.
  3. What changed? Look for new login location, privilege change, process execution, data access, network path, or policy modification.
  4. What corroborates it? Compare identity, endpoint, network, DNS, proxy, cloud audit, ticketing, and asset inventory data.
  5. What is the risk context? Consider asset criticality, account privilege, sensitivity of data, exposure, and whether activity appears ongoing.
Weak triage note: “Looks suspicious.” Better triage note: “Two MFA denials and one approval for a high-risk admin were followed three minutes later by a privileged group change from the same actor and host; no matching change ticket found yet.”

9. Severity, Confidence, Impact, and Urgency

Do not collapse every decision into one severity label. A low-confidence but high-impact signal may deserve investigation. A high-confidence but low-impact alert may be closed quickly with a tuning note.

DimensionMeaningAnalyst question
SeverityPotential harm if the behavior is malicious.How bad could this be?
ConfidenceStrength and reliability of the evidence.How sure are we?
ImpactBusiness, safety, privacy, operational, or compliance effect.What would be affected?
UrgencyHow quickly action is required.Is the activity active or time-sensitive?

Example: a blocked malware file on an isolated test VM may be high confidence but low urgency. A suspicious admin login to production identity infrastructure may be lower confidence at first but high urgency because delay could increase blast radius.

10. Timeline Building

A timeline is the backbone of investigation. It converts scattered telemetry into a defensible sequence of events. Use UTC where possible, preserve original timestamps, and document clock drift or ingestion delay if known.

sequenceDiagram
  participant ID as Identity Provider
  participant EDR as Endpoint
  participant DIR as Directory
  participant SOC as SOC Analyst
  ID->>SOC: MFA denials then approval
  DIR->>SOC: Privileged group membership changed
  EDR->>SOC: Encoded PowerShell observed
  SOC->>SOC: Correlate actor, host, time, and risk
  SOC->>IR: Escalate with timeline and open questions

Good timelines include observed facts, not theories. Instead of writing “attacker compromised the admin,” write “admin account approved MFA at 10:18Z and changed privileged group membership at 10:21Z; the user's confirmation is pending.”

11. Evidence Quality Checks

  • Source reliability: Is the log source authoritative for this event type?
  • Parser accuracy: Are fields mapped correctly, especially actor, host, source IP, action, outcome, and target?
  • Time reliability: Are event time and ingest time close enough for correlation?
  • Entity resolution: Are aliases, service accounts, NAT addresses, shared hosts, and renamed devices handled?
  • Completeness: Are relevant logs missing because of retention, licensing, collection failure, or privacy filtering?
  • Corroboration: Does another independent source support the same conclusion?

When evidence is weak, say so. “Insufficient evidence” is better than inventing certainty. It also gives engineering teams a clear visibility-gap backlog item.

12. Investigation Playbook

A practical investigation can follow this sequence:

  1. Stabilize the case record: Capture alert ID, rule version, time window, source, owner, and current status.
  2. Identify entities: Actor, account type, device, application, cloud resource, data target, and source network.
  3. Establish baseline: Determine whether the action is normal for that actor, host, service, or business process.
  4. Search adjacent time: Look 30–60 minutes before and after for authentication, process, network, cloud, and change-ticket activity.
  5. Scope laterally: Search for the same indicators across other users, hosts, IPs, tokens, and resources.
  6. Document open questions: Do not hide uncertainty; list what must be confirmed.
  7. Decide disposition: Close, tune, monitor, investigate further, or escalate.
  8. Feed back: Update detection logic, analyst guidance, allowlists, telemetry requirements, and playbooks.

13. Analyst Case Notes

A good note should be short, factual, and repeatable. It should allow another analyst to understand the decision without rereading every raw log.

FieldExample content
SummaryOne sentence describing the alert and current conclusion.
Observed factsTimestamped actions, entities, log sources, and outcomes.
ContextAsset criticality, identity privilege, expected business process, change ticket, or user confirmation.
AssessmentInference based on evidence, clearly labeled as an assessment.
DispositionClose, monitor, investigate, duplicate, false positive, benign true positive, or escalate.
Next actionsOwner, deadline, containment request, tuning task, or telemetry gap.
Fact: “The account approved MFA at 10:18Z.” Inference: “This may indicate MFA fatigue because two denials occurred immediately before the approval.” Unsupported claim: “The user was hacked.”

14. Escalation and Containment Boundaries

Escalation is not a failure of triage. It is the correct outcome when evidence exceeds the analyst's authority, available time, or required expertise. Escalate when there is possible active compromise, sensitive data access, privileged identity abuse, lateral movement, malware execution, destructive activity, legal exposure, or executive/public-impact risk.

Containment actions such as disabling accounts, isolating hosts, revoking tokens, blocking domains, or changing firewall rules can protect the organization, but they can also disrupt operations and destroy volatile evidence. Only perform them when your role and playbook authorize them. Otherwise, recommend the action with evidence and urgency.

15. Threat Hunting from First Principles

Threat hunting is proactive investigation. A hunt starts with a hypothesis, not a dashboard. A useful hypothesis is specific enough to test:

  • Weak: “Look for hackers.”
  • Better: “If an administrator was phished through MFA fatigue, then we may see multiple denied MFA prompts, followed by approval, followed by privileged directory changes from the same actor within 30 minutes.”

A simple hunt plan should define:

Plan componentQuestion
HypothesisWhat behavior might exist?
ScopeWhich time window, users, assets, applications, and environments are in scope?
Evidence sourcesWhich logs can prove or disprove the hypothesis?
Query strategyWhich fields, joins, sequence logic, or thresholds will be used?
Success criteriaWhat would count as a finding, no finding, or visibility gap?
Follow-upWill the result create a case, detection, tuning change, or telemetry request?

16. Hunt for Behavior, Not Only Indicators

Indicators such as IP addresses, domains, hashes, and filenames can be useful for short-term matching. They are also easy for adversaries to change. Behavior-focused hunts are usually more durable: suspicious authentication sequences, unusual privilege changes, encoded script execution, abnormal service-account use, unexpected cloud storage access, or cross-host lateral movement patterns.

MITRE ATT&CK is helpful here because it gives teams a shared vocabulary for tactics and techniques. The framework does not prove that an incident occurred. It helps analysts describe observed behavior consistently and identify related evidence sources.

Step 3: Save the triage script as triage_cases.py

import json
from collections import defaultdict
from datetime import datetime, timezone
from pathlib import Path

EVENT_FILE = Path("sample_events.jsonl")
HIGH_RISK_ACTIONS = {
    "privileged_group_add",
    "new_powershell_encoded_command",
    "bulk_object_read",
    "role_policy_change_attempt",
    "token_use_new_geo",
}
BENIGN_CONTEXT_ACTIONS = {
    "password_reset_confirmation",
    "approved_build_window",
}


def parse_time(value):
    return datetime.fromisoformat(value.replace("Z", "+00:00"))


def load_events(path=EVENT_FILE):
    events = []
    for line_number, line in enumerate(path.read_text().splitlines(), start=1):
        if not line.strip():
            continue
        event = json.loads(line)
        event["_line"] = line_number
        event["_time"] = parse_time(event["timestamp"])
        events.append(event)
    return sorted(events, key=lambda e: e["_time"])


def group_by_case(events):
    grouped = defaultdict(list)
    for event in events:
        grouped[event["case_id"]].append(event)
    return dict(grouped)


def summarize_case(events):
    actions = {e.get("action") for e in events}
    sources = sorted({e.get("event_source") for e in events})
    actors = sorted({e.get("actor") for e in events})
    hosts = sorted({e.get("host") for e in events if e.get("host")})
    high_assets = any(e.get("asset_criticality") == "high" for e in events)
    high_identity = any(e.get("identity_risk") == "high" for e in events)
    corroborated = len(sources) >= 3
    high_risk_actions = sorted(actions & HIGH_RISK_ACTIONS)
    benign_context = sorted(actions & BENIGN_CONTEXT_ACTIONS)

    score = 0
    if high_assets:
        score += 2
    if high_identity:
        score += 2
    if high_risk_actions:
        score += 3
    if corroborated:
        score += 2
    if "mfa_denied" in actions and "mfa_approved" in actions:
        score += 2
    if benign_context and not high_risk_actions:
        score -= 3

    if score >= 7:
        disposition = "escalate to incident response"
    elif score >= 4:
        disposition = "investigate further"
    elif score >= 2:
        disposition = "monitor or tune"
    else:
        disposition = "likely benign / close with rationale"

    first = min(e["_time"] for e in events).isoformat().replace("+00:00", "Z")
    last = max(e["_time"] for e in events).isoformat().replace("+00:00", "Z")

    return {
        "case_id": events[0]["case_id"],
        "time_window": f"{first} to {last}",
        "actors": actors,
        "hosts": hosts,
        "sources": sources,
        "high_risk_actions": high_risk_actions,
        "benign_context": benign_context,
        "corroborated": corroborated,
        "score": score,
        "disposition": disposition,
        "analyst_note": build_note(events, disposition),
    }


def build_note(events, disposition):
    lead = next((e for e in events if e.get("event_source") == "siem_alert"), events[0])
    key_events = [
        f"{e['timestamp']} {e.get('event_source')} {e.get('action')} {e.get('outcome')} - {e.get('detail')}"
        for e in events
    ]
    return {
        "alert": lead.get("alert_name", "case review"),
        "recommended_disposition": disposition,
        "why": key_events[:6],
        "open_questions": [
            "Is the actor expected to perform this action at this time?",
            "Is there a matching change ticket, user report, or business process?",
            "Do other telemetry sources show the same actor, host, or token behaving unusually?",
            "What containment action is authorized by the incident-response plan?",
        ],
    }


def hunt_admin_activity_after_mfa(events, minutes=30):
    findings = []
    by_actor = defaultdict(list)
    for event in events:
        by_actor[event.get("actor")].append(event)

    for actor, actor_events in by_actor.items():
        denials = [e for e in actor_events if e.get("action") == "mfa_denied"]
        approvals = [e for e in actor_events if e.get("action") == "mfa_approved"]
        admin_changes = [e for e in actor_events if e.get("action") == "privileged_group_add"]
        for approval in approvals:
            recent_denials = [
                d for d in denials
                if 0 <= (approval["_time"] - d["_time"]).total_seconds() <= minutes * 60
            ]
            later_changes = [
                c for c in admin_changes
                if 0 <= (c["_time"] - approval["_time"]).total_seconds() <= minutes * 60
            ]
            if len(recent_denials) >= 2 and later_changes:
                findings.append({
                    "actor": actor,
                    "hypothesis": "MFA fatigue may have preceded privileged group modification.",
                    "approval_time": approval["timestamp"],
                    "denials": [d["timestamp"] for d in recent_denials],
                    "admin_changes": [c["timestamp"] for c in later_changes],
                    "case_ids": sorted({e["case_id"] for e in recent_denials + [approval] + later_changes}),
                })
    return findings


def main():
    events = load_events()
    cases = [summarize_case(case_events) for case_events in group_by_case(events).values()]
    hunts = hunt_admin_activity_after_mfa(events)
    print(json.dumps({"triage_report": cases, "hunt_findings": hunts}, indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

19. How to Read the Lab Output

The expected highest-risk cases are not determined by the alert title alone. The stronger cases combine multiple factors: privileged identity, high-criticality asset, suspicious sequence, multiple log sources, and potentially risky follow-up action.

  • CASE-1104-001: likely low risk because the pattern is limited and appears related to normal authentication recovery context.
  • CASE-1104-002: escalation candidate because denied MFA prompts, approval, privileged group change, and suspicious endpoint activity are close in time.
  • CASE-1104-003: likely tune or monitor because DNS volume is explained by an approved build window.
  • CASE-1104-004: investigation candidate or escalation candidate depending on service-account ownership, expected deployment geography, and storage-access sensitivity.

The important habit is not memorizing these answers. The habit is writing why a case was handled one way rather than another.

20. Feedback Loop to Detection Engineering

Triage and hunting should create engineering work. Examples:

  • Add a suppression for approved package-cache rebuild windows, with owner and expiration date.
  • Raise severity when MFA fatigue is followed by privileged directory changes.
  • Add asset criticality and identity risk to alerts that currently lack context.
  • Create a detection test case from a confirmed incident timeline.
  • Request missing endpoint telemetry for hosts that appear in cloud or identity alerts.
  • Retire a rule that repeatedly produces unactionable alerts and cannot be tuned safely.

21. Common Failure Points

  • Alert-title bias: Treating the rule name as proof.
  • Single-source certainty: Escalating without independent corroboration when time allows.
  • Context blindness: Ignoring asset criticality, identity privilege, change tickets, or business process.
  • Overcontainment: Disabling accounts or isolating systems without authority or impact awareness.
  • Undocumented closure: Closing alerts without enough rationale for audit or learning.
  • Hunt drift: Starting a hunt with no hypothesis, scope, or success criteria.
  • Privacy creep: Collecting more user data than the investigation needs.

22. Triage Checklist

  • Alert ID, rule name, rule version, and alert time recorded.
  • Actor, asset, source, action, outcome, target, and time window identified.
  • At least one corroborating or contradicting source reviewed when available.
  • Severity, confidence, impact, and urgency considered separately.
  • Observed facts separated from analyst inferences.
  • Disposition chosen from a standard taxonomy.
  • Escalation decision linked to evidence and authorized playbook criteria.
  • Tuning, telemetry, or documentation improvement captured if needed.

23. Self-Assessment

  1. Why is an alert not proof of compromise?
  2. What is the difference between a false positive and a benign true positive?
  3. Why should severity and confidence be tracked separately?
  4. What makes a timeline defensible?
  5. When should an analyst escalate instead of continuing solo investigation?
  6. What makes a threat-hunting hypothesis testable?
  7. Why are TTP-focused hunts often more durable than IOC-only matching?
  8. What should a case note include before closure?

24. Practical Deliverables

After completing this lesson, create:

  • A one-page triage note for one fictional case.
  • A timeline with at least five timestamped events.
  • A disposition decision with facts and inferences separated.
  • A hypothesis-driven hunt plan.
  • A tuning or telemetry-improvement recommendation.

These deliverables are the bridge from raw alert handling to professional security operations.
