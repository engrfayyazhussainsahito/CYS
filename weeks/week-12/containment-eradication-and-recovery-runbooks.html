Week 12 · Lesson 12.4

Containment, Eradication, and Recovery Runbooks

This lesson teaches how to design response runbooks that stop harm, preserve important evidence, remove root cause, restore trusted service, and document business-risk decisions.

Incident Response & ForensicsRunbook designSafe local lab

1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Explain the difference between containment, eradication, recovery, and recovery validation.
  • Choose containment actions based on active harm, business criticality, evidence impact, reversibility, authorization, and blast radius.
  • Build runbooks that preserve evidence before cleanup when preservation is justified.
  • Distinguish temporary containment from root-cause eradication.
  • Define recovery criteria that verify integrity, identity, monitoring, backups, and service behavior before declaring recovery complete.
  • Practice defensive runbook decision-making with a safe fictional local Python lab.

2. Why This Matters

The hardest part of incident response is often not finding the suspicious event. It is deciding what to do next without making the incident worse. A rushed rebuild can destroy evidence. A slow containment decision can allow data theft or encryption. A password reset can fail if active sessions, OAuth grants, service principals, and API tokens remain valid. A system can be restored and still be untrustworthy if persistence, vulnerable entry points, or identity paths were not fixed.

This lesson treats containment, eradication, and recovery as linked decisions. A professional runbook does not simply say “isolate host” or “restore backup.” It records the trigger, owner, approval path, evidence impact, business impact, rollback path, validation tests, and communication points.

Important boundary: The runbooks in this lesson are defensive planning templates for authorized environments. They do not authorize action on systems you do not own or administer. Real containment and recovery can affect availability, evidence, privacy, legal obligations, and customer operations.

3. Research Basis

This lesson is grounded in current defensive guidance and uses practical interpretation for a student course:

  • NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 3 frames incident response as part of cybersecurity risk management aligned to CSF 2.0 and supersedes SP 800-61 Rev. 2.
  • NIST CSF 2.0 defines Respond outcomes for incident management, analysis, communication, and mitigation, and Recover outcomes for restoration and communication.
  • CISA Cybersecurity Incident and Vulnerability Response Playbooks provide standardized procedures to identify, coordinate, remediate, recover, and track mitigations.
  • NIST SP 800-184 provides guidance for recovery planning, playbook development, testing, improvement, and resilience metrics.
  • NIST SP 800-86 supports the forensic principle that collection, examination, analysis, and reporting must be integrated into incident response.
  • NIST SP 800-84 supports testing and exercise programs for validating plans, procedures, and response capability.
  • CISA/MS-ISAC Ransomware Guide supports preparation, response coordination, backup discipline, and recovery-oriented thinking for ransomware scenarios.

4. First Principle: Containment Is a Risk Decision

Containment is the act of limiting current or potential harm. It is not automatically “turn everything off.” Containment may mean disabling an account, revoking sessions, isolating a host, blocking a destination, removing a public policy, pausing a workflow, quarantining email, limiting a service account, or temporarily increasing monitoring while the team scopes the incident.

The decision should be based on a clear statement: What harm are we trying to stop, what evidence might we destroy, what business function might we break, and who has authority to accept that tradeoff?

Decision factorQuestion to askWhy it matters
Active harmIs encryption, exfiltration, privilege abuse, persistence, or lateral movement happening now?Active harm may justify faster and more disruptive containment.
Evidence impactWill the action destroy memory, sessions, process state, logs, temporary files, or cloud state?Some evidence disappears when systems are rebooted, isolated, cleaned, or reconfigured.
Business impactWhich service, workflow, customer, or safety function will be affected?A technically clean action can still be unacceptable if it breaks critical operations.
Blast radiusDoes the action affect one identity, one host, a subnet, a tenant, a production pipeline, or a whole organization?Broad controls can stop harm but also cause outage and noise.
ReversibilityCan the action be rolled back safely?Reversible actions are often safer when uncertainty is high.
AuthorizationWho can approve this action?During serious incidents, authority must be explicit, not improvised.

5. Containment, Eradication, and Recovery Are Different

These terms are often collapsed into “fix the incident,” but they answer different questions.

PhaseQuestion answeredExample actionsCommon failure
ContainmentHow do we stop or limit current harm?Revoke sessions, isolate endpoint, block known destination, remove public storage policy, pause compromised automation.Containing too narrowly and letting alternate access continue.
EradicationHow do we remove the cause and close the path back in?Remove persistence, patch exploited flaw, rotate exposed secrets, remove malicious grants, fix misconfiguration, close phishing path.Cleaning visible symptoms while root cause remains.
RecoveryHow do we restore trusted service?Restore from trusted image or backup, re-enable access, validate service behavior, monitor for recurrence.Restoring quickly without proving the asset is trustworthy.
Recovery validationHow do we know recovery is safe enough to declare?Integrity checks, backup verification, identity review, monitoring confirmation, business owner acceptance.Declaring recovery because systems are online, not because risk is reduced.

6. Anatomy of a Good Runbook

A runbook should be specific enough to guide action under stress but flexible enough to handle uncertainty. It should not be a blind checklist. It should force the responder to record evidence, approval, and tradeoffs.

  1. Trigger: What conditions activate this runbook?
  2. Scope statement: Which systems, accounts, tenants, networks, data sets, and time windows are in scope?
  3. Roles: Incident commander, technical lead, evidence custodian, business owner, communications lead, and legal/privacy contact where needed.
  4. Evidence preservation: What must be exported, hashed, screenshotted, snapshotted, or recorded before action?
  5. Containment options: Ordered options with approval, blast radius, business impact, reversibility, and rollback instructions.
  6. Eradication tasks: Root-cause closure, persistence removal, credential rotation, patching, configuration correction, and control hardening.
  7. Recovery actions: Trusted restore source, access restoration, service validation, monitoring period, rollback plan.
  8. Decision log: Who approved what, when, why, based on which facts?
  9. Exit criteria: What conditions must be true before the incident can move to reporting and lessons learned?

7. Response Flow

The runbook flow below shows why evidence and business authorization are not side tasks. They sit inside the technical response.

9. Identity-Centered Containment

Modern incidents often center on identity rather than malware. Containment must therefore address sessions, refresh tokens, OAuth grants, API keys, service principals, mailbox rules, privileged roles, conditional access gaps, and downstream applications. A password reset alone may be insufficient if long-lived tokens, delegated app grants, or service credentials remain valid.

Identity containment should record:

  • Which account, role, service principal, application, or key is affected.
  • Which active sessions or tokens were revoked.
  • Which privileged assignments changed during the incident window.
  • Which downstream systems trust the identity.
  • Which business process may break if the identity is disabled.
  • Which evidence exports were completed before changes.

10. Host and Network Containment

Host isolation is useful when a device is actively malicious, likely to spread, or communicating with suspicious infrastructure. But isolation is not a substitute for scoping. A single isolated host does not answer whether credentials were reused, lateral movement occurred, or cloud/SaaS access happened from the same account.

Network containment can be precise or broad. Blocking a known command-and-control destination may be low impact. Blocking an entire subnet, country, protocol, or business partner can cause operational damage and may only push the actor to a different path. Record why the rule was created, what it matches, when it expires, who owns review, and what telemetry will prove whether it works.

11. Cloud and SaaS Containment

Cloud containment often means changing control-plane state: role assignments, policies, keys, security groups, routes, public exposure, object policies, workload identities, or deployment pipelines. Before changing these, preserve the prior configuration when possible. The before-state may be needed to explain exposure, root cause, and regulatory or customer impact.

Cloud/SaaS problemContainment ideaPreserve first
Public storage exposureRemove public policy or restrict object access.Policy export, object access logs, affected resource list, audit log window.
Compromised cloud keyDisable or rotate key, review use, replace workload credential.Key usage logs, role permissions, recent API calls, dependent workloads.
Suspicious privileged role assignmentRemove role, disable principal, enforce approval path.Role-change logs, actor identity, target identity, source IP, user agent, time window.
Malicious SaaS sharingRevoke shares, remove external collaborators, reset affected sessions.File access logs, sharing events, affected object IDs, actor account timeline.
Compromised CI/CD pipelinePause deployment, rotate secrets, review artifacts and runners.Pipeline logs, commit history, artifact hashes, deployment records, secret access events.

12. Eradication: Remove Cause, Not Just Symptoms

Eradication begins after enough scoping exists to avoid cleaning one visible artifact while the attacker still has another path. For example, deleting malware does not help if stolen credentials still work. Patching a server does not help if a backdoor account remains. Resetting a user password does not help if a malicious app grant still has access.

Good eradication tasks are traceable to root-cause hypotheses:

  • Credential exposure: rotate affected secrets, revoke sessions, remove unauthorized grants, review downstream trust.
  • Exploited vulnerability: patch or mitigate, validate version, inspect for persistence, review logs for exploitation window.
  • Misconfiguration: fix current state, fix infrastructure-as-code or template, add guardrail, check similar assets.
  • Malicious persistence: remove persistence after preserving evidence, verify startup paths, scheduled jobs, service entries, cloud automation, and identity artifacts.
  • Phishing entry path: remove malicious messages, block campaign infrastructure where justified, review mailbox rules, train targeted users, strengthen email controls.

13. Recovery: Restore Trust, Not Just Availability

Recovery is the controlled restoration of affected assets and operations. The goal is not merely that the service responds. The goal is that the restored service is trustworthy enough for the business to use.

Recovery questionValidation evidence
Is the restore source trusted?Backup integrity check, known-good image, artifact signature, backup date before compromise where needed.
Is the entry path closed?Patch validation, policy test, fixed IAM scope, removed malicious rule, corrected configuration.
Is identity safe?Credential rotation, session revocation, privileged access review, service-account dependency review.
Is data integrity acceptable?Application checks, database consistency checks, object counts, hash comparison where available, business owner validation.
Is monitoring active?Logs enabled, alert rules active, EDR healthy, audit coverage confirmed, post-recovery watch window defined.
Is the business ready?Service owner acceptance, customer impact review, rollback plan, communications update.

14. Backup and Restore Discipline

Backups do not guarantee recovery. A backup may be too old, corrupted, infected, encrypted, misconfigured, inaccessible, or missing critical dependencies. Recovery planning should define recovery time objective, recovery point objective, restore ownership, backup access controls, offline or immutable backup strategy where appropriate, and regular restore testing.

Before restoring, responders should ask whether the backup predates compromise, whether credentials inside the restore are safe, whether vulnerable configuration will return, and whether the recovered system will reconnect to an unsafe network or identity environment.

16. Runbook Metrics

Metrics help improve response only when they measure useful behavior. A team should avoid vanity metrics such as “number of tickets closed” without context.

  • Time to declare: How long from first credible evidence to incident declaration?
  • Time to preserve critical evidence: Did short-retention and volatile sources get preserved before loss?
  • Time to contain active harm: How long from decision to verified containment?
  • Containment effectiveness: Did activity stop, shift, or continue through another path?
  • Eradication completeness: Were root cause, persistence, credentials, and similar assets addressed?
  • Recovery validation pass rate: How often did restored services pass integrity, identity, monitoring, and business checks on the first attempt?
  • Reopen rate: How often did incidents return because containment or eradication was incomplete?

18. Communications During Containment and Recovery

Technical teams often under-communicate during response because they are busy. This creates confusion, duplicate work, and unmanaged business risk. Communications should be short, factual, and decision-oriented.

  • Situation: What is known, what is suspected, and what remains unknown?
  • Impact: Which systems, users, data, and business functions are affected?
  • Action: What containment or recovery action was taken or proposed?
  • Risk: What could break, what evidence could be lost, and what risk remains?
  • Next update: What will be checked next and when?

A good update does not claim “resolved” until recovery validation is complete. It can say “contained,” “eradication in progress,” “service restored under monitoring,” or “recovery criteria met.”

Step 2: Create runbook_decision_aid.py

import json

IMPACT_SCORE = {"low": 1, "medium": 2, "high": 3, "critical": 4}
SPEED_SCORE = {"slow": 1, "medium": 2, "fast": 3}
REVERSIBILITY_SCORE = {"low": 0, "medium": 1, "high": 2}
EVIDENCE_SCORE = {"positive": 3, "low": 2, "medium": 1, "high": -1}
BLAST_RADIUS_PENALTY = {
    "single_identity": 0,
    "single_identity_plus_workflows": 1,
    "single_host": 0,
    "single_storage_resource": 0,
    "network_wide_for_indicator": 1,
    "pipeline_workflow": 2,
    "storage_audit_scope": 0,
}


def load_incidents(path="runbook_scenarios.json"):
    with open(path, "r", encoding="utf-8") as handle:
        return json.load(handle)


def score_containment(option, incident):
    active_bonus = 3 if incident.get("active_harm") else 0
    criticality_bonus = IMPACT_SCORE.get(incident.get("business_criticality"), 1)
    speed = SPEED_SCORE.get(option.get("speed"), 0)
    reversibility = REVERSIBILITY_SCORE.get(option.get("reversibility"), 0)
    evidence = EVIDENCE_SCORE.get(option.get("evidence_impact"), 0)
    business_penalty = IMPACT_SCORE.get(option.get("business_impact"), 1) - 1
    blast_penalty = BLAST_RADIUS_PENALTY.get(option.get("blast_radius"), 1)
    return active_bonus + criticality_bonus + speed + reversibility + evidence - business_penalty - blast_penalty


def rank_containment_options(incident):
    ranked = []
    for option in incident["containment_options"]:
        ranked.append({
            "action": option["action"],
            "score": score_containment(option, incident),
            "approval_required": option["approval_required"],
            "business_impact": option["business_impact"],
            "evidence_impact": option["evidence_impact"],
            "reversibility": option["reversibility"],
            "blast_radius": option["blast_radius"],
        })
    ranked.sort(key=lambda item: (-item["score"], -SPEED_SCORE.get(next(opt for opt in incident["containment_options"] if opt["action"] == item["action"]).get("speed"), 0), item["business_impact"], item["action"]))
    return ranked


def build_runbook(incident):
    ranked = rank_containment_options(incident)
    top_action = ranked[0]
    preservation_warning = []
    if incident.get("evidence_at_risk"):
        preservation_warning.append(
            "Preserve or explicitly waive volatile/short-retention evidence before actions that destroy it: "
            + ", ".join(incident["evidence_at_risk"])
        )
    if incident.get("active_harm"):
        preservation_warning.append("Active harm is present; containment speed may outweigh perfect evidence collection.")
    else:
        preservation_warning.append("No active harm is marked; confirm evidence and business impact before disruptive containment.")
    return {
        "incident_id": incident["incident_id"],
        "scenario": incident["scenario"],
        "recommended_first_containment": top_action,
        "containment_options_ranked": ranked,
        "preservation_warning": preservation_warning,
        "eradication_tasks": incident["eradication_tasks"],
        "recovery_checks": incident["recovery_checks"],
        "decision_record_prompt": [
            "What fact justified containment?",
            "Who approved the action?",
            "What evidence was preserved first?",
            "What business impact was accepted?",
            "How will rollback or recovery be validated?",
        ],
    }


def main():
    incidents = load_incidents()
    report = [build_runbook(incident) for incident in incidents]
    print(json.dumps(report, indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

from runbook_decision_aid import build_runbook, load_incidents, rank_containment_options


def find_incident(incident_id):
    return next(item for item in load_incidents() if item["incident_id"] == incident_id)


def test_active_identity_incident_prefers_session_revocation():
    incident = find_incident("INC-1204-001")
    runbook = build_runbook(incident)
    assert runbook["recommended_first_containment"]["action"] == "revoke_active_sessions"
    assert "active_sessions" in runbook["preservation_warning"][0]


def test_ransomware_precursor_prioritizes_fast_isolation_or_blocking():

    assert "isolate_endpoint_from_network" in top_actions

20. How to Interpret the Lab Output

The decision aid should prioritize fast, reversible, low-blast-radius actions when active harm exists. It should also warn when volatile or short-retention evidence is at risk. The score is intentionally simple. In a real incident, the score should never replace human authorization, legal/privacy input, business ownership, or incident commander judgment.

Use the output to practice asking better questions: Did we preserve enough evidence? Did we stop active harm? Did we avoid unnecessary outage? Did we document approval? Did we define recovery validation before restoring service?
