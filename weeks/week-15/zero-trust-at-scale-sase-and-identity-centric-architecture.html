Week 15 · Lesson 15.3

Zero Trust at Scale, SASE, and Identity-Centric Architecture

Zero Trust is not a product and not a slogan. It is an architecture discipline for making access decisions per resource, with strong identity, device context, least privilege, data awareness, telemetry, and continuous policy enforcement across users, workloads, SaaS, cloud, branches, and legacy systems.

Estimated reading time95–125 minutes
Difficulty levelAdvanced MS foundation
Required backgroundWeeks 2, 7, 10, 11, 13, and 14 recommended
Lab modeSafe local Zero Trust readiness triage using fictional architecture records
Boundary: This lesson teaches defensive architecture planning. It does not teach bypassing identity controls, attacking SASE/ZTNA products, evading device posture checks, credential abuse, scanning, or live access testing. All practical work uses fictional records and local files only.

1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Explain Zero Trust as an architecture model rather than a vendor product.
  • Describe the roles of identity, device posture, application/workload controls, network controls, data controls, visibility, analytics, automation, and governance.
  • Distinguish Zero Trust Architecture, ZTNA, SASE, SSE, VPN replacement, microsegmentation, and identity-centric architecture.
  • Map policy decision points, policy enforcement points, and policy information sources.
  • Design a phased migration for users, devices, workloads, SaaS, branches, legacy applications, and cloud resources.
  • Identify common failure modes such as broad VPN access, standing privilege, unmanaged devices, thin telemetry, product-first adoption, and exception sprawl.
  • Create metrics and governance evidence that show maturity instead of marketing claims.
  • Use a safe local lab to triage fictional systems for Zero Trust readiness.

2. Research Basis

This lesson is grounded in neutral architecture and government guidance rather than vendor marketing. NIST SP 800-207 defines Zero Trust as a shift from static network perimeters toward users, assets, and resources, with no implicit trust based only on network location or asset ownership. NIST SP 1800-35 provides implementation examples and lessons learned from end-to-end Zero Trust architecture demonstrations. OMB M-22-09 gives a federal implementation strategy organized around identity, devices, networks, applications/workloads, and data. CISA’s Zero Trust maturity material is commonly used for maturity thinking across identity, devices, networks, applications/workloads, and data, with visibility/analytics, automation/orchestration, and governance as cross-cutting capabilities.

The practical conclusion is that a mature program cannot be measured by buying a SASE, ZTNA, identity, or endpoint product. It must be measured by whether access decisions become more explicit, resource-specific, least-privilege, observable, reversible, and governed.

3. What Zero Trust Actually Means

Zero Trust starts from a simple architectural assumption: network location is not enough reason to trust a request. A laptop on an office LAN, a service account inside a cloud VPC, a branch device connected by SD-WAN, or an employee connected through VPN may still be compromised, misconfigured, overprivileged, or acting outside policy.

Zero Trust therefore focuses on protecting resources. A resource may be an application, API, dataset, administrative console, workload, file repository, SaaS tenant, database, or operational workflow. Access should be granted only after policy evaluates relevant context: who or what is requesting, from which device or workload, for which resource, to perform which action, under what risk conditions, with what data sensitivity, and with what monitoring.

Key distinction: “Never trust, always verify” is useful shorthand, but it is incomplete. Verification must become enforceable authorization, scoped privilege, continuous telemetry, data protection, and accountable governance.

4. What Zero Trust Is Not

  • Not a single product: Products can enforce parts of the architecture, but no product automatically understands your data, owners, risk appetite, legacy constraints, and business processes.
  • Not only MFA: MFA improves authentication, but Zero Trust also needs authorization, device/workload posture, data controls, telemetry, and lifecycle governance.
  • Not only network segmentation: Segmentation reduces lateral movement, but access still needs identity, resource awareness, and least privilege.
  • Not VPN with a new name: Replacing VPN with ZTNA can reduce broad network access, but poor application authorization can still expose sensitive functions.
  • Not “trust nobody” operationally: The goal is not paranoia. The goal is explicit, evidence-based trust decisions with constrained blast radius.
  • Not a maturity label: “We are Zero Trust” is too vague. Useful claims name the resource, policy, enforcement point, evidence, owner, exception, and measured outcome.

5. Core Architecture Components

Zero Trust architecture separates decision-making from enforcement and feeds both with high-quality context.

ComponentPurposeExamples
SubjectThe actor requesting accessUser, administrator, service account, workload, automation agent, partner identity
ResourceThe protected thingApplication, API, database, file, SaaS workspace, admin console, workload
Policy Decision PointEvaluates context and returns an allow, deny, step-up, or constrained decisionIdentity policy engine, authorization service, risk engine, workload policy controller
Policy Enforcement PointApplies the decision to the access pathApplication gateway, proxy, API gateway, service mesh, SaaS control, firewall, endpoint agent
Policy Information SourcesProvide context and evidenceIdentity directory, device management, EDR, CMDB, data catalog, vulnerability status, risk signals
Telemetry and AnalyticsMeasure behavior, detect anomalies, reconstruct decisions, and improve policySign-in logs, app logs, data access logs, network flow, audit events, DLP events

Weak designs centralize authentication but leave authorization scattered, undocumented, or still based on network reachability. Stronger designs make policy explicit at each important resource and use logs to prove what was decided and enforced.

6. Pillars and Cross-Cutting Capabilities

A practical maturity model usually treats Zero Trust as several interacting domains:

  • Identity: humans, administrators, partners, service accounts, workloads, devices, and automation all need lifecycle, authentication, authorization, and review.
  • Devices: managed endpoints, mobile devices, servers, branch equipment, IoT, and unmanaged devices require posture, compliance, inventory, and compensating controls.
  • Networks: connectivity should support resource-specific access, segmentation, encryption, inspection where appropriate, and reduced lateral movement.
  • Applications and workloads: applications, APIs, SaaS, containers, serverless functions, CI/CD agents, and service meshes need embedded authorization and telemetry.
  • Data: sensitivity, location, sharing, export, retention, encryption, tokenization, DLP, and rights controls determine the real business impact of access.
  • Visibility and analytics: logs and context must be good enough to investigate, measure, and tune decisions.
  • Automation and orchestration: policy updates, access reviews, risk response, ticketing, and remediation need scalable workflows with human oversight.
  • Governance: owners, risk decisions, exception expiry, metrics, funding, and audit evidence prevent the program from becoming a pile of disconnected tools.

7. Identity-Centric Architecture

Identity-centric security means that access decisions are primarily tied to verified subject identity and role, not to being “on the network.” This includes human identity, privileged identity, workload identity, service accounts, device identity, and partner identity.

Identity architecture at scale needs:

  • Authoritative sources for users, groups, roles, attributes, lifecycle status, and employment/contractor changes.
  • Phishing-resistant or risk-appropriate MFA for high-value and privileged access.
  • Least-privilege roles mapped to business functions, not only technical teams.
  • Just-in-time and time-bound privileged access where feasible.
  • Service account and workload identity governance, including ownership, scope, rotation, secrets, and revocation.
  • Access review evidence that shows who approved access, why it is needed, when it expires, and what changed.

At scale, the hard problem is not turning on MFA. It is keeping identity attributes accurate enough that policy decisions are meaningful.

8. Device and Workload Posture

Device posture is context about the requester’s platform. For endpoints, this can include management state, OS version, disk encryption, EDR health, jailbreak/root status, patch state, certificate state, and risk score. For workloads, posture can include image provenance, runtime identity, vulnerability state, signing status, admission policy, secret exposure, and deployment environment.

Device posture has limits. A “compliant” device can still be used by a phished user. An unmanaged device may still need carefully constrained access. Legacy systems may not support modern posture checks. The correct design is not always “deny everything unmanaged”; it may be browser isolation, read-only access, data-loss controls, step-up approval, lower-risk app paths, or explicit exception handling.

9. Application and Resource-Specific Access

Traditional VPN often grants a user network reachability first and relies on downstream application controls later. ZTNA tries to invert that model by giving access to a specific application or resource only after policy evaluation. This can reduce exposed attack surface and lateral movement, but it only works if the application and data controls are also strong.

Resource-specific access should answer:

  • Which exact application, API, admin action, dataset, or function is being requested?
  • Is the subject allowed to perform this action, or only view/read/search/export/approve?
  • Does the device or workload context satisfy policy?
  • Does the data classification require DLP, encryption, watermarking, approval, or logging?
  • Is this access normal for this role, time, geography, behavior, and business process?
  • Can the decision be reconstructed later from logs?

10. Network Controls, Segmentation, and Blast Radius

Zero Trust does not make networks irrelevant. It changes what networks are trusted to prove. Network controls still matter for segmentation, encrypted paths, egress control, service-to-service restrictions, branch connectivity, industrial zones, cloud VPCs/VNETs, and traffic visibility.

Microsegmentation can reduce blast radius by limiting which workloads, users, and services can communicate. But segmentation is not enough if all users share the same broad app role or if administrators have permanent standing access. The stronger pattern combines segmentation with identity, resource authorization, device/workload posture, and telemetry.

11. Data-Centric Controls

Data controls answer the question that many access projects skip: “What harm can this access cause?” Two users may access the same app, but one action views public metadata while another exports regulated customer records. Zero Trust maturity improves when data sensitivity affects policy.

Useful data-centric patterns include:

  • Data classification and ownership tied to applications and repositories.
  • Controls for export, sharing, printing, bulk download, API extraction, and external collaboration.
  • DLP and rights-management controls for high-value documents and SaaS repositories.
  • Encryption and key management aligned to data sensitivity and recovery needs.
  • Retention, deletion, legal hold, and privacy workflows that match governance requirements.
  • Telemetry for sensitive operations, not only sign-in events.

12. SASE, SSE, ZTNA, and Vendor Language

SASE is a market architecture term that combines wide-area networking and cloud-delivered security functions such as SD-WAN, secure web gateway, cloud access security broker, firewall-as-a-service, ZTNA, and data-loss controls. SSE generally refers to the security-service subset without the full SD-WAN component. ZTNA is a narrower access pattern for connecting subjects to specific applications or resources.

These services can support Zero Trust, especially for distributed users, branches, SaaS, and cloud access. But they do not replace architecture work. A SASE provider does not automatically fix bad role design inside SaaS, missing data owners, stale device inventory, excessive service-account privileges, unlogged admin actions, or weak exception governance.

Architecture rule: Evaluate SASE and ZTNA by what policies they enforce, which resources they protect, what context they use, how they integrate with identity/device/data systems, and what evidence they produce. Do not evaluate them only by product category.

13. Visibility, Analytics, and Continuous Evaluation

Zero Trust decisions become safer when telemetry can answer five questions:

  1. Who or what requested access?
  2. What resource and action were requested?
  3. Which policy decision was made and why?
  4. Which enforcement point applied the decision?
  5. What happened after access was granted?

Useful telemetry includes identity sign-ins, MFA events, device posture, EDR alerts, application audit logs, data access and export events, network flows, service mesh events, SaaS admin actions, CI/CD events, secrets access, and policy changes. The goal is not to collect unlimited data. The goal is enough trustworthy evidence to detect abuse, investigate incidents, prove control operation, and tune policy.

14. Automation and Orchestration

Zero Trust at scale requires automation, but automation must be constrained. Useful automation examples include access request routing, time-bound privileged access, risk-based step-up, device quarantine, conditional data-export approval, ticket creation, exception expiry reminders, and policy regression tests.

Unsafe automation creates denial-of-service, business disruption, or silent privilege expansion. Every automated action should have an owner, trigger condition, approval model, rollback path, audit log, and exception process. High-impact actions need simulation or staged rollout before production enforcement.

15. Implementation Roadmap at Scale

A practical Zero Trust program is phased. Trying to transform everything at once usually creates user friction, exception sprawl, and abandoned controls.

  1. Inventory resources: name the applications, APIs, SaaS tenants, data repositories, admin consoles, workloads, service accounts, and branches.
  2. Classify data and criticality: prioritize high-value data, privileged functions, internet-exposed access, partner access, and legacy risk.
  3. Map current access paths: VPN, SSO, direct SaaS, admin portals, service accounts, API keys, branch routing, and workload-to-workload paths.
  4. Pick a pilot: choose a resource with clear ownership, measurable risk, good telemetry, and rollback.
  5. Define target policy: subject, device/workload posture, resource, action, data sensitivity, risk context, and enforcement point.
  6. Improve telemetry: make sure access decisions and post-access behavior can be reconstructed.
  7. Test and stage: validate policy with test users, canaries, exception handling, help-desk support, and rollback.
  8. Measure outcomes: reduced standing access, fewer broad network paths, better access review, shorter exception lifetime, and stronger investigation evidence.
  9. Repeat patterns: convert successful pilots into reusable architecture patterns, templates, and governance standards.

16. Legacy Systems and Exceptions

Legacy systems are where many Zero Trust programs fail. A mainframe, clinical workstation, manufacturing cell, branch appliance, vendor-hosted app, or old claims system may not support SAML, OIDC, modern logging, granular roles, or device posture. Pretending the problem does not exist only creates shadow exceptions.

Better exception handling includes:

  • Name the legacy owner, business owner, risk owner, and technical constraint.
  • Use compensating controls: access proxy, jump host, segmentation, admin session recording, read-only replicas, strict firewall paths, or monitored service accounts.
  • Separate authentication improvements from deeper application authorization changes.
  • Create a migration trigger: vendor upgrade, contract renewal, platform replacement, audit finding, incident, or risk threshold.
  • Review exceptions on a fixed cadence with expiry dates and evidence.

17. Metrics and Maturity Evidence

Zero Trust metrics should show control outcomes, not tool deployment counts. Useful measures include:

  • Percentage of high-value resources with named owner, data classification, and access policy.
  • Percentage of privileged access that is time-bound and approved.
  • Number of broad VPN/network paths retired or constrained.
  • Coverage of managed device posture for sensitive resources.
  • Percentage of service accounts with owner, scope, rotation/expiry, and last-use evidence.
  • Number and age of exceptions by pillar, owner, and expiry date.
  • Access-review completion rate and removal rate.
  • Policy decision/enforcement logs available for critical applications.
  • Mean time to revoke risky access after role change, device compromise, or incident.

Maturity increases when the organization can prove that high-risk access is explicit, constrained, observable, reviewed, and reversible.

18. Common Anti-Patterns

  • Product-first program: buying SASE, ZTNA, identity, or endpoint tooling before defining resources, data, owners, and policies.
  • MFA-only maturity: treating authentication as the whole control story while application roles and data export remain broad.
  • VPN replacement theater: replacing VPN with ZTNA but leaving application authorization and service accounts unchanged.
  • Network-only microsegmentation: building many network zones without role cleanup, data classification, or telemetry.
  • Over-blocking: creating so much user friction that teams create shadow IT, shared accounts, and workarounds.
  • Exception sprawl: approving legacy exceptions without expiry, owner, compensating control, or migration plan.
  • No workload identity: focusing on human users while CI/CD agents, containers, serverless functions, and APIs use long-lived secrets.
  • Thin logs: logging sign-ins but not admin actions, data exports, application decisions, or policy changes.

19. Reference Architecture Pattern

The pattern below is intentionally vendor-neutral. Different organizations will implement it with different identity providers, endpoint platforms, proxies, service meshes, SaaS controls, cloud-native services, network tools, and governance systems.

flowchart TD
  A[Subject: user, device, workload, service account] --> B[Policy Enforcement Point]
  B --> C{Policy Decision Point}
  D[Identity directory and lifecycle] --> C
  E[Device or workload posture] --> C
  F[Resource inventory and data classification] --> C
  G[Threat/risk signals and vulnerability state] --> C
  H[Governance policy and exceptions] --> C
  C -->|allow / deny / step-up / constrain| B
  B --> I[Protected resource: app, API, SaaS, dataset, admin console]
  B --> J[Telemetry]
  I --> J
  C --> J
  J --> K[Analytics, detection, access review, metrics, and improvement]
  K --> H

20. Case Study: Branch Staff, SaaS, and a Legacy App

A company wants to “do Zero Trust” by buying a SASE service. Branch staff use SaaS apps, a legacy claims system through VPN, a document repository, and shared branch devices. The first architecture diagram shows traffic moving through a cloud security service, but the claims system still has broad subnet access, contractor access is not time-bound, document exports are not approved, and service-desk accounts have permanent admin rights.

A weak program claims success because traffic now passes through a modern access service. A stronger program says: “The access path improved, but the actual control gaps remain. We need resource-specific policy for the claims system, time-bound contractor access, document export controls, privileged-access cleanup, device posture rules for shared devices, and logs that reconstruct policy decisions.”

Step 2 — Create zero_trust_readiness_lab.py

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""Safe local Zero Trust readiness triage lab.

This script analyzes fictional architecture records only. It performs no network
activity, authentication, scanning, vendor access, or real security assessment.
"""
import json
from pathlib import Path

REQUIRED_PILLARS = {
    "identity": "identity_controls",
    "device": "device_posture_controls",
    "application": "application_controls",
    "network": "network_controls",
    "data": "data_controls",
}

HIGH_VALUE_DATA = {
    "regulated_customer_data",
    "confidential_financial",
    "source_code_and_secrets",
    "highly_sensitive_governance",
}


def score_system(system):
    score = 0
    reasons = []

    if system.get("data_classification") in HIGH_VALUE_DATA:
        score += 3
        reasons.append("High-value or regulated data needs stronger resource-specific controls.")

    missing_pillars = [pillar for pillar, field in REQUIRED_PILLARS.items() if not system.get(field)]
    score += len(missing_pillars) * 2
    if missing_pillars:
        reasons.append("Missing or weak zero-trust pillar coverage: " + ", ".join(missing_pillars) + ".")

    gaps = system.get("policy_gaps", [])
    score += len(gaps)
    if gaps:
        reasons.append("Policy/evidence gaps exist: " + "; ".join(gaps) + ".")

    telemetry = system.get("telemetry", [])
    if len(telemetry) < 3:
        score += 2
        reasons.append("Telemetry is too thin for continuous evaluation and investigation.")

    if any("broad" in gap.lower() or "shared" in gap.lower() for gap in gaps):
        score += 2
        reasons.append("Broad or shared access conflicts with least-privilege and accountability goals.")

    if system.get("legacy_constraints"):
        score += 2
        reasons.append("Legacy constraints require phased migration and compensating controls.")

    if system.get("automation_level") == "manual":
        score += 2
        reasons.append("Manual operation limits scalable policy enforcement and review.")
    elif system.get("automation_level") == "partial":
        score += 1
        reasons.append("Partial automation can support transition but needs measurable improvement.")

    return score, reasons, missing_pillars


def band(score):
    if score >= 12:
        return "red"
    if score >= 7:
        return "amber"
    return "green"


def recommendations(system, band_name, missing_pillars):
    actions = []
    if band_name == "red":
        actions.append("Create a phased remediation plan before expanding access or migrating users.")
    elif band_name == "amber":
        actions.append("Improve specific pillar gaps and evidence before claiming zero-trust maturity.")
    else:
        actions.append("Maintain controls, validate exceptions, and use this system as a candidate for repeatable patterns.")

    if "identity" in missing_pillars:
        actions.append("Define strong identity, MFA, session, and lifecycle controls for humans and service accounts.")
    if "device" in missing_pillars:
        actions.append("Add device posture or compensating controls for unmanaged and legacy access paths.")
    if "application" in missing_pillars:
        actions.append("Move authorization closer to the application/resource instead of relying on network location.")
    if "network" in missing_pillars:
        actions.append("Segment access paths and make network policy support resource-specific authorization.")
    if "data" in missing_pillars:
        actions.append("Classify data and add controls for export, sharing, retention, and sensitive operations.")

    if system.get("legacy_constraints"):
        actions.append("Document legacy exceptions with owner, expiry, compensating controls, and migration trigger.")
    if any("long-lived" in gap.lower() for gap in system.get("policy_gaps", [])):
        actions.append("Replace long-lived credentials with short-lived, scoped, auditable credentials.")
    if len(system.get("telemetry", [])) < 3:
        actions.append("Add logs needed to reconstruct identity, device, resource, policy, and data-access decisions.")

    actions.append("Do not treat a SASE, VPN, ZTNA, or identity product purchase as proof of architecture maturity.")
    return actions


def analyze(systems):
    results = []
    summary = {"red": 0, "amber": 0, "green": 0}
    for system in systems:
        score, reasons, missing = score_system(system)
        b = band(score)
        summary[b] += 1
        results.append({
            "system_id": system["system_id"],
            "name": system["name"],
            "score": score,
            "risk_band": b,
            "missing_pillars": missing,
            "reasons": reasons,
            "recommended_actions": recommendations(system, b, missing),
            "safe_boundary": "Fictional architecture triage only; no network, authentication, scanning, or real access testing."
        })
    results.sort(key=lambda item: (item["score"], item["system_id"]), reverse=True)
    return {"summary": summary, "systems": results}


def main():
    systems = json.loads(Path("zt_systems.json").read_text())
    print(json.dumps(analyze(systems), indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

assert any("long-lived credentials" in action.lower() for action in build["recommended_actions"])
assert exec_docs["risk_band"] in {"green", "amber"}
assert report["summary"]["red"] >= 1
print("Zero Trust readiness lab tests passed")

Expected learning outcome

The output should show that the legacy claims system is higher risk than a better-governed high-value repository because it has broad network access, weak identity controls, missing device/data controls, limited telemetry, and legacy constraints. The exercise reinforces that Zero Trust maturity is based on policy coverage, evidence, and migration discipline, not product category.

22. Zero Trust Architecture Checklist

  • Do we have an inventory of resources, owners, data classes, access paths, and current controls?
  • Are access decisions based on subject, resource, action, device/workload posture, data sensitivity, and risk context?
  • Do high-value resources have application-level authorization and data controls, not only network access control?
  • Are service accounts, workload identities, CI/CD agents, APIs, and automation included?
  • Can we reconstruct policy decisions and post-access actions from logs?
  • Are exceptions owned, time-bound, reviewed, and tied to migration triggers?
  • Have legacy systems been given compensating controls and a funded migration plan?
  • Do metrics show reduced standing access, reduced lateral movement, stronger access review, and better incident evidence?
  • Have users, help desk, operations, and application owners been prepared for staged rollout?
