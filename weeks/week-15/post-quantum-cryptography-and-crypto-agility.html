Week 15 · Lesson 15.2

Post-Quantum Cryptography and Crypto-Agility

Post-quantum migration is not a simple algorithm swap. It is a multi-year engineering and governance program: discover where cryptography exists, classify business impact, understand long-lived data risk, coordinate vendors, test interoperability, and build systems that can change cryptography without emergency redesign.

Estimated reading time95–125 minutes
Difficulty levelAdvanced MS foundation
Required backgroundWeeks 3, 4, 10, 13, and 14 recommended
Lab modeSafe local PQC readiness triage using fictional JSON data
Boundary: This lesson teaches defensive cryptographic inventory, migration planning, and governance. It does not teach custom cryptographic algorithm design, cryptanalysis, production key handling, live scanning, certificate harvesting, or changes to real systems. Do not implement your own cryptography; use approved standards, vetted libraries, vendor-supported protocols, formal change control, and qualified cryptographic review.

1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Explain why sufficiently capable quantum computers threaten widely deployed public-key cryptography.
  • Distinguish post-quantum cryptography from quantum key distribution, proprietary “quantum-safe” claims, and ordinary key rotation.
  • Describe the practical role of NIST FIPS 203, FIPS 204, and FIPS 205 in PQC migration.
  • Build a cryptographic inventory that supports PQC prioritization and audit evidence.
  • Explain crypto-agility as a software, protocol, vendor, and governance property.
  • Prioritize migration using data lifetime, system exposure, public-key use, certificates, firmware signatures, vendors, and change complexity.
  • Identify where hybrid transition, interoperability testing, certificate lifecycle, and rollback planning matter.
  • Create a safe migration roadmap without changing production cryptography prematurely.

2. Research Basis

This lesson is grounded in current post-quantum guidance and standards. NIST released the first three finalized post-quantum encryption and signature standards in August 2024: FIPS 203 for ML-KEM, FIPS 204 for ML-DSA, and FIPS 205 for SLH-DSA. FIPS 203 defines ML-KEM for key establishment over public channels, and FIPS 205 defines SLH-DSA as a stateless hash-based digital signature standard. NIST’s NCCoE Migration to Post-Quantum Cryptography project emphasizes cryptographic discovery, inventory, prioritization, and interoperability testing. NIST IR 8547 is an initial public draft describing NIST’s expected approach to transition from quantum-vulnerable cryptographic standards to post-quantum standards.

The practical conclusion is that organizations should not wait for a “quantum emergency.” They should inventory cryptography now, prioritize systems with long-lived confidentiality or signature requirements, improve crypto-agility, coordinate suppliers, and test transition patterns in non-production environments.

3. What Quantum Changes — and What It Does Not

Most enterprise cryptography uses a combination of primitives:

  • Symmetric encryption: AES or similar algorithms used after parties have a shared secret.
  • Hashing: SHA-2/SHA-3-style functions used for integrity, identifiers, signatures, and key derivation.
  • Public-key key establishment: RSA key exchange, Diffie-Hellman, or elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman used to agree on shared secrets.
  • Public-key signatures: RSA, DSA, ECDSA, or EdDSA-style signatures used for certificates, software signing, firmware signing, documents, and authentication.

The main migration pressure is on public-key algorithms based on factoring and discrete logarithm problems. Symmetric cryptography and hashing still require review, but most migration programs start by discovering public-key key establishment and public-key signatures because those are the systems directly targeted by the current PQC standards.

4. The Threat Model: CRQC and Harvest-Now-Decrypt-Later

A cryptographically relevant quantum computer, or CRQC, means a quantum computer capable of breaking currently deployed public-key cryptography at operationally meaningful scale. Nobody should present the exact arrival date as known. The planning problem is a risk equation:

  • How long must the data remain confidential? Medical records, state secrets, trade secrets, legal records, and research data may have long value.
  • How long will migration take? Large enterprises have embedded cryptography in applications, protocols, libraries, certificates, appliances, hardware, firmware, vendors, and partner channels.
  • When might a CRQC exist? The date is uncertain, but uncertainty does not remove the risk for long-lived information.

The “harvest-now-decrypt-later” risk is that an adversary records encrypted traffic or obtains encrypted archives now, then decrypts them later if quantum capability becomes available. This matters most where confidentiality lifetime exceeds the expected migration and threat horizon.

Do not read this table as “replace everything today.” Read it as a standards baseline for planning, vendor questions, test labs, procurement language, and migration roadmaps.

6. Crypto-Agility

Crypto-agility is the ability to identify, configure, replace, validate, and roll back cryptographic algorithms, keys, parameters, libraries, providers, and protocols without uncontrolled disruption. It is not only a library feature. It requires design and governance:

  • Algorithms and modes are not hard-coded across application logic.
  • Key formats, certificate profiles, protocol versions, and message formats can evolve.
  • Policy can define allowed and disallowed cryptography.
  • Inventories identify where cryptography is used and who owns it.
  • Tests detect interoperability, performance, certificate-chain, handshake, storage, and signature-verification issues.
  • Change control supports canary deployment, rollback, monitoring, and exception handling.

A crypto-agile system can respond to PQC migration, algorithm deprecation, library vulnerability, certificate-policy change, or compliance requirement faster than a system where cryptography is scattered and undocumented.

7. Cryptographic Inventory and CBOM

A post-quantum readiness program starts with inventory. A useful cryptographic inventory should include:

  • System, owner, business process, data owner, and risk owner.
  • Cryptographic purpose: confidentiality, integrity, authentication, signing, key establishment, non-repudiation, or storage protection.
  • Algorithms, parameter sizes, modes, libraries, providers, protocols, certificates, keys, and hardware modules.
  • Data protected, data lifetime, business criticality, regulatory constraints, and external exposure.
  • Certificate authorities, trust stores, HSMs, KMS services, firmware signing workflows, and software signing pipelines.
  • Supplier, partner, SaaS, cloud, appliance, identity, and client dependencies.
  • Evidence quality: how the record was discovered, when it was checked, and what remains unknown.

A CBOM, or cryptographic bill of materials, is a structured form of this inventory. It is especially useful where software and product teams need machine-readable evidence of cryptographic dependencies.

8. Where Cryptography Hides

PQC migration fails when teams only inspect obvious TLS certificates. Cryptography may be embedded in:

  • Browsers, mobile apps, APIs, service meshes, VPNs, SSH, SFTP, email security, and federated identity.
  • Databases, backups, archives, object storage, tokenization, secrets management, and KMS/HSM integrations.
  • Container registries, package signing, CI/CD release signing, firmware signing, secure boot, and update mechanisms.
  • IoT, OT, medical devices, network appliances, printers, payment terminals, and embedded systems.
  • Third-party SaaS, managed service providers, partner file-transfer channels, and customer integrations.
  • Custom protocols, old Java/.NET libraries, embedded OpenSSL builds, hardware accelerators, smart cards, and certificate pinning.

The question is not “Where did we intentionally deploy cryptography?” It is “Where does any system depend on public-key cryptography to protect confidentiality, identity, integrity, or update trust?”

9. Prioritization Model

Not every cryptographic use receives the same migration priority. Start with risk drivers:

DriverHigher priority when...Why it matters
Long-lived confidentialityData must remain secret for years or decades.Harvest-now-decrypt-later risk can exist before a CRQC exists.
Long-lived signaturesFirmware, legal, identity, archive, or safety signatures must verify far into the future.Signature migration may require dual signing, timestamping, trust-store updates, or new verification paths.
ExposureInternet-facing, partner-facing, or widely distributed client systems are involved.Interoperability and phased rollout are harder.
Embedded dependencyHardware, firmware, secure boot, HSMs, smart cards, or appliances are involved.Replacement may require product lifecycle planning, vendor cooperation, or physical rollout.
Low crypto-agilityAlgorithms are hard-coded, undocumented, or vendor-controlled.Technical migration may be blocked until architecture changes.
Supplier dependencyThe organization cannot update the component alone.Procurement and contract controls become part of the migration path.

10. Migration Architecture

A practical PQC program uses a pipeline: discover, classify, prioritize, test, pilot, deploy, monitor, and retire legacy dependencies.

flowchart TD
  A[Cryptographic discovery] --> B[Inventory and CBOM]
  B --> C[Classify purpose, data lifetime, exposure, owner]
  C --> D[Prioritize migration queue]
  D --> E[Non-production interoperability and performance tests]
  E --> F{Pilot ready?}
  F -->|no| G[Fix architecture, vendor, protocol, or evidence gaps]
  G --> B
  F -->|yes| H[Controlled pilot with rollback]
  H --> I[Production rollout by risk tier]
  I --> J[Monitor errors, latency, certificate/signature validation, exceptions]
  J --> K[Retire legacy cryptography where safe]
  K --> B

11. Hybrid Transition and Interoperability

In many protocol ecosystems, transition may involve hybrid mechanisms that combine classical and post-quantum approaches during a migration period. The purpose is to reduce transition risk while standards, libraries, clients, servers, certificates, hardware, and policies mature. Hybrid does not mean “automatically secure.” It must be tested for protocol correctness, downgrade resistance, performance, message size, certificate-chain behavior, logging, monitoring, and rollback.

The safest place to start is a non-production interoperability lab that mirrors representative clients, servers, libraries, load balancers, proxies, certificates, HSM/KMS integrations, and monitoring. Production changes should follow formal change control and vendor support statements.

12. Certificates, PKI, and Trust Stores

PKI migration is harder than replacing one certificate. It involves certificate authorities, certificate profiles, intermediates, roots, trust stores, browsers, mobile clients, service meshes, mTLS, certificate pinning, revocation, issuance automation, observability, and rollback. PQC signatures may also change certificate and message sizes, validation performance, and compatibility assumptions.

For readiness, record which systems issue, validate, pin, rotate, monitor, and revoke certificates. Map which clients must trust which roots and which vendors control trust-store updates. A system with strong certificate automation is usually easier to pilot than one with manual certificates hidden in appliances.

13. Long-Lived Data and Re-Encryption

Systems that protect short-lived public website traffic may not be the first migration priority. Systems that protect archives, legal records, health data, trade secrets, national-security material, long-lived customer records, backups, and research data may require earlier action because confidentiality must survive for years. For stored data, determine whether quantum-vulnerable public-key cryptography protects data keys, transport keys, wrapping keys, archive packages, or recovery keys.

Do not assume that rotating current keys solves the problem. If encrypted material has already been captured or copied, future safety depends on what algorithm protected it, how keys were established or wrapped, and whether re-encryption or re-wrapping is possible and auditable.

14. Code Signing, Firmware, and Secure Boot

Signature migration is often more complex than transport encryption migration. Firmware and secure-boot chains may be constrained by boot ROMs, hardware roots of trust, field devices, update package formats, size limits, verification code, regulatory approval, and safety testing. Some products may need dual-signature periods or new verification logic before legacy signatures can be retired.

For each signing workflow, identify the signer, verifier, algorithm, key storage, signing service, release process, timestamping, field-device population, update channel, rollback model, and end-of-life plan. A signature that must be trusted for years should not be treated like a short-lived session key.

15. Vendor and Supply-Chain Dependencies

Most organizations will not migrate alone. Ask vendors and partners specific questions:

  • Which NIST PQC algorithms are on the roadmap and in which product versions?
  • Which protocols, libraries, appliances, APIs, agents, and certificate workflows are affected?
  • Will the product support hybrid transition, rollback, and telemetry?
  • What are the performance, size, compatibility, and operational constraints?
  • How will SBOM/CBOM evidence be provided?
  • What dates apply to test environments, preview features, production support, and legacy retirement?
  • What contractual commitments, support boundaries, and incident-notification terms apply?

“Quantum-safe” in marketing language is not enough. Readiness requires evidence, supported configurations, compatibility statements, and a tested migration path.

16. Dangerous Anti-Patterns

  • Algorithm shopping: selecting a PQC algorithm without understanding protocol support or system constraints.
  • Production-first testing: trying new cryptographic modes in production before non-production interoperability testing.
  • Inventory theater: listing only web certificates while ignoring libraries, APIs, HSMs, firmware, backups, SaaS, and partners.
  • Vendor trust without evidence: accepting roadmap statements without versions, dates, supported configurations, or test artifacts.
  • Custom cryptography: designing proprietary algorithms or glue code instead of using vetted standards and libraries.
  • No rollback: deploying new cryptography without a tested recovery path for client failures, performance regression, or validation errors.
  • Ignoring signatures: focusing on transport encryption while code signing, firmware, document signing, and identity signatures remain unmanaged.

17. Governance and Risk Ownership

PQC readiness crosses security architecture, engineering, product, legal, procurement, compliance, risk, operations, and vendor management. A defensible program needs:

  • A named executive sponsor and accountable migration owner.
  • Cryptographic inventory standards and evidence quality rules.
  • Risk acceptance criteria for legacy public-key cryptography.
  • Procurement language requiring PQC roadmap and crypto-agility evidence.
  • Architecture review for new systems that use public-key cryptography.
  • Exception process with expiry date and compensating controls.
  • Metrics: inventory coverage, high-risk assets identified, vendor roadmap coverage, pilot readiness, and legacy retirement progress.

18. Practical Roadmap

  1. Establish ownership: define sponsor, architecture owner, risk owner, and working group.
  2. Define inventory fields: algorithms, protocols, keys, certificates, libraries, providers, data lifetime, owners, vendors, and evidence source.
  3. Start discovery: combine CMDB, certificate inventory, code review, SBOM/CBOM, KMS/HSM records, architecture diagrams, and vendor questionnaires.
  4. Prioritize: identify long-lived data, signatures, internet-facing services, firmware, hardware, critical business processes, and low-agility systems.
  5. Build non-production tests: evaluate interoperability, performance, observability, certificates, rollback, and client compatibility.
  6. Update procurement: require vendor PQC roadmaps, supported standards, CBOM evidence, and transition support.
  7. Pilot safe candidates: choose lower-risk environments with strong automation and rollback.
  8. Report progress: use risk-reduction metrics instead of vague “quantum readiness” claims.

19. Case Study: Two Systems, Different Priorities

A public marketing website uses TLS with automated certificates and protects mostly public content. A research archive uses RSA-wrapped keys to protect confidential research files that must remain private for 25 years. The website is visible and important, but its data lifetime may be short and its certificate automation may make it a good pilot. The archive is less visible but more urgent from a harvest-now-decrypt-later perspective because confidentiality must last beyond any plausible migration window.

A weak program migrates the most visible TLS endpoint first and reports success. A stronger program pilots safely on manageable infrastructure while prioritizing long-lived confidentiality, key wrapping, backups, archives, and vendor-constrained systems for funded remediation.

Step 2 — Create pqc_readiness_lab.py

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
Safe local lab: post-quantum readiness triage for fictional cryptographic inventory records.
This script performs no scanning, DNS lookup, web request, cryptographic operation, or system inspection.
"""
import json
from pathlib import Path

REQUIRED_EVIDENCE = {"owner_record", "certificate_inventory", "CMDB_record", "CBOM", "key_inventory", "data_classification_record"}


def normalize(value):
    return str(value or "").strip().lower()


def score_asset(asset):
    score = 0
    reasons = []
    data_years = int(asset.get("data_lifetime_years", 0))

    if asset.get("quantum_vulnerable_public_key"):
        score += 25
        reasons.append("uses quantum-vulnerable public-key cryptography")

    if data_years >= 10:
        score += 25
        reasons.append("protects or signs data/devices with long-lived value")
    elif data_years >= 5:
        score += 15
        reasons.append("data lifetime creates harvest-now-decrypt-later concern")
    elif data_years >= 1:
        score += 5
        reasons.append("moderate data lifetime")

    if asset.get("hardware_or_firmware_dependency"):
        score += 20
        reasons.append("hardware or firmware dependency may make migration slow")

    if asset.get("internet_facing"):
        score += 10
        reasons.append("internet-facing or partner-facing exposure")

    agility = normalize(asset.get("crypto_agility"))
    if agility == "low":
        score += 20
        reasons.append("low crypto-agility")
    elif agility == "medium":
        score += 10
        reasons.append("medium crypto-agility")

    gaps = asset.get("known_gaps", [])
    score += min(20, 5 * len(gaps))
    if gaps:
        reasons.append("known migration evidence gaps exist")

    evidence = set(asset.get("inventory_evidence", []))
    if not evidence:
        score += 15
        reasons.append("no inventory evidence")
    elif len(evidence) < 3:
        score += 8
        reasons.append("inventory evidence is thin")

    return score, reasons


def risk_band(score):
    if score >= 75:
        return "red"
    if score >= 45:
        return "amber"
    return "green"


def recommendations(asset, band, reasons):
    actions = []
    evidence = set(asset.get("inventory_evidence", []))
    crypto_uses = [normalize(x) for x in asset.get("crypto_uses", [])]

    if asset.get("quantum_vulnerable_public_key"):
        actions.append("Confirm where public-key cryptography is used and whether it provides confidentiality, authentication, signing, or key establishment.")
    if "rsa_wrapped_data_keys" in crypto_uses or "file_encryption" in crypto_uses:
        actions.append("Prioritize long-lived confidentiality review and plan re-encryption or key-wrap redesign with approved owners.")
    if "firmware_signature" in crypto_uses or asset.get("hardware_or_firmware_dependency"):
        actions.append("Coordinate firmware/signature migration with hardware lifecycle, boot-chain constraints, vendor roadmap, and rollback testing.")
    if asset.get("internet_facing"):
        actions.append("Place this asset on the protocol and interoperability testing roadmap before production changes.")
    if asset.get("vendor_dependency") not in {"internal_platform", "standard_browser_tls_stack"}:
        actions.append("Request vendor or partner PQC roadmap, supported algorithms, testing windows, and contractual commitments.")
    if normalize(asset.get("crypto_agility")) == "low":
        actions.append("Create a crypto-agility improvement story: externalize algorithm policy, version formats, test rollback, and reduce hard-coded dependencies.")
    if "CBOM" not in evidence and "key_inventory" not in evidence:
        actions.append("Improve the cryptographic inventory or CBOM before selecting technical migration steps.")
    if band == "red":
        actions.append("Escalate to architecture/risk governance for a funded migration plan and named residual-risk owner.")
    elif band == "amber":
        actions.append("Assign a pilot or discovery task with success criteria and review date.")
    else:
        actions.append("Keep in inventory and use as a low-risk pilot candidate if it has strong automation and test isolation.")
    return actions


def analyze_assets(assets):
    results = []
    summary = {"red": 0, "amber": 0, "green": 0}
    for asset in assets:
        score, reasons = score_asset(asset)
        band = risk_band(score)
        summary[band] += 1
        results.append({
            "asset_id": asset.get("asset_id"),
            "name": asset.get("name"),
            "score": score,
            "risk_band": band,
            "drivers": reasons,
            "recommended_actions": recommendations(asset, band, reasons),
            "do_not_do": [
                "Do not replace cryptographic algorithms in production without compatibility testing and rollback.",
                "Do not invent proprietary cryptography.",
                "Do not treat vendor roadmap claims as proof of readiness without evidence."
            ]
        })
    results.sort(key=lambda item: (item["score"], item["asset_id"]), reverse=True)
    return {"summary": summary, "assets": results}


def main():
    assets = json.loads(Path("crypto_inventory.json").read_text())
    print(json.dumps(analyze_assets(assets), indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

Expected learning outcome

The output should show that migration priority is not based only on whether an asset is internet-facing. Long-lived data, firmware signatures, low crypto-agility, vendor dependency, and weak inventory evidence can make a quiet internal system more urgent than a visible web endpoint.

21. PQC Readiness Checklist

  • Do we know where public-key cryptography is used for key establishment, signatures, certificates, identity, software signing, firmware, archives, and partners?
  • Do inventory records have owners, evidence sources, data lifetime, business criticality, and vendor dependencies?
  • Have we identified high-value data that may be exposed to harvest-now-decrypt-later risk?
  • Do new procurement and architecture reviews require PQC roadmap and crypto-agility evidence?
  • Can systems change algorithms, key formats, certificate profiles, and libraries without redesign?
  • Have we created a non-production interoperability lab before production changes?
  • Are firmware, secure boot, code signing, HSM/KMS, PKI, and trust-store dependencies included?
  • Do rollback and monitoring plans exist for pilot changes?
  • Are residual risks owned and time-bound?
