Midterm Practical: Attack Surface and Defense Review

This practical checks whether you can reason like a defender. You will analyze a fictional university services environment, identify attack surface, prioritize risks, recommend controls, map detections, and write a professional report. You do not attack real systems, scan public targets, use stolen credentials, or run malware.

Estimated reading time180–240 minutes for the packet; 8–14 hours total work
Difficulty levelMS foundation integration
Required backgroundWeeks 0–7: Linux, networking, Python, Git/evidence, security goals, OS/IAM, crypto, secure software, memory safety, web/API, and network security
Practice timeOne week; recommended 2–3 focused sessions

Week Overview

Week 8 is the midpoint checkpoint. It is deliberately practical because cybersecurity knowledge is weak if it cannot be applied to a messy system with incomplete information. You will not receive a perfectly labeled vulnerability list. You will receive a fictional organization, a simple architecture, logs, code snippets, configuration fragments, and stakeholder constraints.

Your job is to produce a defensible security review. Defensible means every claim is tied to evidence, assumptions are stated, uncertainty is visible, and recommendations reduce measurable risk.

Learning Objectives

After completing this practical, you should be able to:

  • Identify critical assets, business services, trust boundaries, and security objectives.
  • Explain attack surface without relying on fear-based or vague language.
  • Distinguish threat, vulnerability, exploit path, exposure, impact, likelihood, and risk.
  • Assess identity, access control, cryptographic, software, web/API, memory-safety, and network weaknesses.
  • Prioritize findings using asset criticality, exposure, exploitability, compensating controls, and evidence quality.
  • Recommend practical controls mapped to prevention, detection, response, and recovery.
  • Write a professional report with an executive summary, technical findings, evidence appendix, and residual-risk statement.

1. Introduction

This practical is a controlled security assessment of a fictional environment named Northbridge University Services. Northbridge runs a student portal, a staff administration portal, a small research-lab network, public websites, remote access for staff, and a cloud-hosted API gateway.

The practical exists because real defenders rarely solve isolated textbook problems. A real security review must combine architecture, identity, software, logs, policies, user behavior, and business priorities. A vulnerability is not automatically the highest risk just because it has a scary name. A weak control may be high risk if it protects a critical asset and has broad exposure.

Who uses this type of review? Security analysts, application security engineers, penetration testers, cloud security engineers, network defenders, incident responders, auditors, risk managers, system administrators, and security researchers.

Authorization rule: all analysis must use only the fictional artifacts in this page. Do not scan, test, probe, fuzz, or intercept any real system. Do not run tools against university, public, personal, company, or cloud targets unless a separate written authorization exists.

2. Learning Outcomes

Your final submission should prove that you can:

  1. Draw or refine a system diagram with assets, zones, trust boundaries, and data flows.
  2. List attack surface by entry point, exposed service, identity path, software path, and network path.
  3. Write at least eight findings, each with evidence, affected asset, risk explanation, recommendation, and verification method.
  4. Explain why your top three risks matter more than lower-ranked issues.
  5. Map at least five detection opportunities to logs or telemetry.
  6. Produce a concise executive summary that a non-specialist can understand.

3. Prerequisites

3.1 Asset

An asset is anything valuable that needs protection. Examples include student records, credentials, payment data, research data, uptime, reputation, safety, and staff productivity.

3.2 Attack surface

Attack surface is the set of places where an attacker or accidental misuse can interact with a system. It includes public interfaces, APIs, login forms, VPN gateways, Wi-Fi networks, cloud storage, dependencies, admin panels, service accounts, and human processes.

3.3 Risk

Risk is the possibility of loss or harm. A simple classroom formula is:

risk = likelihood × impact

This formula is useful for thinking, but it is incomplete. Real prioritization must also consider exposure, exploitability, asset value, compensating controls, detection, recovery, and uncertainty.

3.4 Evidence

Evidence is information that supports a claim. In this practical, evidence may be a line from a fictional log, a code snippet, a config fragment, a diagram flow, a permission table, or a stakeholder requirement.

4. Core Concepts

4.1 The security review workflow

  1. Scope: define what is included and excluded.
  2. Assets: identify what matters and why.
  3. Architecture: map systems, users, zones, trust boundaries, and flows.
  4. Attack surface: identify where interaction can happen.
  5. Weaknesses: identify missing, weak, or misconfigured controls.
  6. Risk: estimate likelihood and impact with clear assumptions.
  7. Controls: recommend prevention, detection, response, and recovery improvements.
  8. Report: write evidence-backed findings and an executive summary.

4.2 Severity is not the same as risk

A vulnerability scoring system may estimate technical severity, but organizational risk still depends on context. A low-severity issue on a payroll system may matter more than a high-severity issue on an isolated lab VM with no sensitive data and strong compensating controls.

4.3 Attack path thinking

An attack path is a chain of conditions that could lead to harm. Example: weak VPN MFA → compromised staff account → broad internal network access → admin portal reachable → insufficient object authorization → student records exposed. Breaking any link may reduce risk.

4.4 Defense-in-depth thinking

Defense in depth means several controls work together. A single control can fail. Good designs combine strong identity, least privilege, segmentation, input validation, logging, monitoring, backups, patching, and response procedures.

4.5 Evidence quality

Evidence quality ranges from weak to strong:

Evidence levelExampleProblem
Weak“This looks insecure.”No proof.
Moderate“The diagram shows staff VPN can reach the database zone.”Needs confirmation of firewall rules and auth.
Strong“Firewall rule F-17 allows staff_vpn → database_subnet:5432; staff group includes 812 users; database contains student records.”Specific and verifiable.

7. Mathematical Foundations

7.1 Simple risk matrix

Use a 1–5 scale for likelihood and impact. This is a learning tool, not a universal truth.

risk_score = likelihood × impact

Example: if broad VPN access to a sensitive database has likelihood 4 and impact 5, then:

risk_score = 4 × 5 = 20

7.2 Adjusted risk score

For the practical, use an adjusted score when evidence supports it:

adjusted_risk = asset_value × exposure × exploitability × impact ÷ control_strength

Worked example:

asset_value = 5        # student records
exposure = 4           # reachable by many staff VPN users
exploitability = 3     # needs credentials but no special device restriction
impact = 5             # large privacy and operational impact
control_strength = 2   # MFA exists but segmentation is weak

adjusted_risk = 5 × 4 × 3 × 5 ÷ 2 = 150

Do not pretend the number is exact. Use it to make your assumptions visible and to compare findings consistently.

7.3 Precision and recall for detection

Detection quality can be discussed using two ideas:

precision = true_positives / (true_positives + false_positives)
recall = true_positives / (true_positives + false_negatives)

A noisy detection may have low precision. A quiet detection that misses many real incidents may have low recall. Good detection engineering balances both.

8. Practical Examples

8.1 Linux example: suspicious permissions

Artifact: linux_file_inventory.txt

-rw-r--r-- 1 root root     3120 /etc/ssh/sshd_config
-rw-r----- 1 app  app      1940 /opt/student-portal/config.yml
-rw-r--r-- 1 app  app       980 /opt/student-portal/.env
-rwxr-xr-x 1 root root    21800 /usr/local/bin/backup-students.sh

Reasoning: a `.env` file may contain secrets. World-readable permissions are suspicious if the file contains credentials or tokens. The finding should ask for evidence of file contents, owner, access need, and secret-management process.

8.2 Windows example: local administrator sprawl

Artifact: windows_admin_review.csv

8.3 Python example: risk scoring helper

# Safe local calculation only. This does not scan or test anything.
findings = [
    {"id": "F-01", "asset": 5, "exposure": 4, "exploitability": 3, "impact": 5, "controls": 2},
    {"id": "F-02", "asset": 3, "exposure": 2, "exploitability": 2, "impact": 3, "controls": 3},
]

for f in findings:
    score = f["asset"] * f["exposure"] * f["exploitability"] * f["impact"] / f["controls"]
    print(f["id"], score)

8.5 Web/API example: object-level authorization issue

# Fictional unsafe example for review.
@app.get("/api/transcripts/<student_id>")
def get_transcript(student_id):
    # Missing check: is the current user allowed to access this student_id?
    return db.get_transcript(student_id)

Reasoning: authentication alone is not enough. The server must verify that the authenticated user is authorized for the specific object being requested.

8.6 Cloud example: overly broad storage access

{
  "principal": "student-portal-service",
  "action": "storage:*",
  "resource": "*",
  "condition": "none"
}

Reasoning: wildcard permissions make blast radius larger. Recommend least privilege, environment separation, key rotation, logging, and policy review.

9. Code Examples

9.1 Safe finding template generator

# This script creates a local Markdown template for findings.
# It does not connect to a network or test any system.

findings = [
    "F-01: Broad VPN access to database zone",
    "F-02: Missing object-level authorization in transcript API",
    "F-03: World-readable environment file",
]

for item in findings:
    finding_id, title = item.split(": ", 1)
    print(f"## {finding_id} — {title}")
    print("Asset affected: ")
    print("Evidence: ")
    print("Risk: ")
    print("Recommendation: ")
    print("Verification: ")
    print()

Line-by-line explanation

  • findings stores fictional finding titles.
  • The loop processes each finding.
  • split separates the finding ID from the title.
  • The script prints a consistent report structure.
  • Consistency matters because security reports must be reviewable and auditable.

Common mistakes

  • Writing recommendations without evidence.
  • Calling something “critical” without explaining asset value and exposure.
  • Reporting tool output without human analysis.
  • Confusing authentication with object-level authorization.
  • Ignoring compensating controls or uncertainty.

10. Security Perspective

Common attacks and weaknesses in the scenario

  • Credential stuffing against the student portal.
  • Password spraying against staff VPN accounts.
  • Broken object-level authorization in transcript APIs.
  • Over-broad VPN network reach.
  • Weak secrets management in application configuration files.
  • Insufficient logging for privileged admin actions.
  • Flat internal routing between guest, lab, and staff zones.
  • Missing dependency and release-integrity evidence.

Defensive strategies

  • Strong MFA and phishing-resistant authentication for privileged users.
  • Per-object authorization checks on sensitive APIs.
  • Network segmentation and deny-by-default firewall policy.
  • Centralized logs for authentication, API access, administrative actions, and network flows.
  • Secret storage outside source code and local files where possible.
  • Security testing gates before production release.
  • Incident response playbooks for credential compromise and data-access anomalies.

Detection methods

  • Repeated failed logins by username, source, or password-spray pattern.
  • Transcript access volume unusual for a user role.
  • VPN sessions reaching database ports directly.
  • New administrator membership or privilege changes.
  • Unexpected API 403/404/200 sequences across many object IDs.
  • Configuration-file reads by unusual processes.

12. Research Perspective

Current research relevant to integrated security reviews includes attack-graph generation, automated threat modeling, policy verification, software supply-chain risk scoring, detection engineering evaluation, cyber-risk quantification, adversary emulation, security metrics, and human-centered security reporting.

Open problems include:

  • How to quantify cyber risk without pretending uncertain estimates are exact.
  • How to automatically find missing authorization logic in large applications.
  • How to verify firewall, cloud, and identity policies across hybrid environments.
  • How to measure detection coverage against realistic attacker behavior.
  • How to prioritize vulnerabilities based on actual exploitation, not only theoretical severity.
  • How to present risk to executives without hiding technical uncertainty.

13. Case Studies

13.1 Equifax 2017

Public reporting and official analysis describe how an unpatched internet-facing application vulnerability contributed to major data exposure. Lessons for this practical: asset inventory, patch governance, segmentation, monitoring, and evidence quality matter together.

13.2 SolarWinds supply-chain compromise

The compromise demonstrated that trusted software update paths can become high-value attack surfaces. Lessons: release integrity, build-system security, logging, detection, and supplier-risk review are part of attack-surface analysis.

13.3 Colonial Pipeline ransomware event

Public reporting described a compromised VPN account as part of the incident context. Lessons: remote access, account lifecycle, MFA, business continuity, and incident response planning can be critical to operational resilience.

13.4 MOVEit Transfer exploitation

Mass exploitation of managed file-transfer software showed how internet-facing enterprise applications can become broad third-party risk. Lessons: exposure management, vendor patching, monitoring, data minimization, and incident notification processes are important.

14. Common Misconceptions

MisconceptionCorrection
“A VPN means the network is safe.”A VPN protects a path. It does not prove device health, limit access automatically, or fix weak authorization.
“High CVSS always means highest business risk.”Severity helps, but risk needs asset context, exposure, likelihood, controls, and impact.
“Authentication solves authorization.”Authentication identifies a subject. Authorization decides what that subject may access.
“If there is a WAF, web vulnerabilities do not matter.”A WAF is defense in depth, not a substitute for secure design and code.
“No alert means no incident.”Missing telemetry or weak detection can produce silence during compromise.
“A long report is a good report.”A good report is clear, evidence-backed, prioritized, and actionable.

15. Summary

The midterm practical integrates the first half of the course. You analyze a fictional environment, identify attack surface, prioritize risks, recommend defenses, and write a professional report. The expected skill is not tool memorization. The expected skill is evidence-based security reasoning.

A strong submission states scope, identifies critical assets, explains trust boundaries, prioritizes findings with transparent assumptions, recommends practical controls, maps detection opportunities, and communicates residual risk clearly.

16. Glossary

Attack surface
All places where someone or something can interact with a system.
Attack path
A chain of conditions that could lead to harm.
Compensating control
A control that reduces risk when the ideal control is absent or incomplete.
Evidence appendix
A report section containing artifacts that support findings.
Finding
A documented security issue with evidence, risk, recommendation, and verification guidance.
Likelihood
An estimate of how probable a threat event is under stated assumptions.
Residual risk
Risk that remains after controls are applied.
Risk acceptance
A decision to tolerate residual risk, usually by an accountable business owner.
Trust boundary
A point where data or control crosses between different trust levels.
Verification
Checking that a remediation or control actually works.
