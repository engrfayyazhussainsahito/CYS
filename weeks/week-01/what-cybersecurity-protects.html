Lesson 1.1: What Cybersecurity Protects

Cybersecurity does not begin with tools, hacking, malware, or firewalls. It begins with a simple question: what could be harmed, and why would that harm matter? This lesson teaches you to identify what cybersecurity protects before you decide how to protect it.

Estimated reading time120–180 minutes
Difficulty levelBeginner → Graduate foundation
Required backgroundWeek 0 recommended, but not required
Practice time4–7 hours

Week 1 Context

Overview: Week 1 builds the security mindset. Instead of memorizing attacks first, you learn how to identify protected assets, security objectives, stakeholders, harms, threats, assumptions, and ethical boundaries.

Learning objectives: after this lesson you should be able to explain what cybersecurity protects, classify assets, connect assets to confidentiality, integrity, availability, safety, privacy, trust, and resilience, and build a basic protection map for a small organization.

Required background: no prior security background is required. Linux, networking, Python, and documentation lessons from Week 0 make the examples easier, but this lesson starts from first principles.

Estimated reading time: 120–180 minutes. Difficulty: begins at zero knowledge and ends with graduate-level security reasoning.

1. Introduction

Plain-English idea: cybersecurity protects valuable things that depend on computers, networks, software, data, and people. These valuable things are called assets.

An asset is anything worth protecting. A database is an asset. A hospital appointment system is an asset. A student record is an asset. A password is an asset. A power grid control system is an asset. A person’s privacy is an asset. A company’s ability to keep operating is also an asset.

Cybersecurity exists because digital systems can be misused, broken, tricked, overloaded, modified, copied, or observed without permission. A cyber incident can expose private data, stop a service, corrupt records, mislead decision makers, damage physical equipment, cause financial loss, or reduce public trust.

This topic matters because beginners often start with the wrong question: “Which tool should I use?” A stronger first question is: “What am I protecting, from whom, under what assumptions, and what happens if protection fails?”

Cybersecurity is used anywhere digital systems support important work: banks protect money and fraud systems; hospitals protect patient records and care delivery; governments protect public services and national functions; cloud providers protect shared infrastructure; universities protect research and student systems; defense organizations protect missions; large enterprises protect operations, customers, intellectual property, and reputation.

Do not confuse cybersecurity with hacking

Offensive techniques are one part of cybersecurity education, but cybersecurity as a discipline is broader. It includes risk management, secure design, defensive monitoring, incident response, governance, privacy, safety, resilience, law, ethics, and human factors.

2. Learning Outcomes

  • Define cybersecurity, information security, asset, stakeholder, harm, impact, threat, vulnerability, control, and security objective.
  • Explain why cybersecurity protects more than computers.
  • Classify assets into data, identity, software, hardware, network, service, process, financial, human, physical, legal, and reputational assets.
  • Explain the basic meaning of confidentiality, integrity, availability, privacy, safety, authenticity, accountability, resilience, and trust.
  • Map a real-world situation to protected assets and possible harms.
  • Distinguish observation, inference, and assumption in security analysis.
  • Explain why the same asset can have different value for different stakeholders.
  • Use a simple risk formula: risk ≈ likelihood × impact.
  • Create a basic asset inventory for a fictional system.
  • Describe how banks, governments, cloud providers, healthcare, defense, universities, and enterprises think about protection.
  • Identify common beginner misconceptions about cybersecurity.
  • Perform a safe, non-invasive protection-mapping lab.

3. Prerequisites

3.1 What is a computer system?

A computer system is a combination of hardware, software, data, users, and processes. A laptop is a computer system. A cloud web application is a computer system. A hospital imaging machine connected to a network is also part of a computer system.

3.2 What is data?

Data is information represented in a form that a computer can store, process, or transmit. Examples include names, grades, bank balances, GPS coordinates, source code, medical records, log files, and sensor readings.

3.3 What is a network?

A network connects devices so they can communicate. The internet is a network of networks. Cybersecurity often protects communication across networks.

3.4 What is software?

Software is instructions that tell a computer what to do. Software includes operating systems, mobile apps, web applications, cloud services, scripts, firmware, and security tools.

3.5 What is authorization?

Authorization means permission. A person may be allowed to read one file but not another. A program may be allowed to access one database table but not delete records.

3.6 What is harm?

Harm means a negative consequence. In cybersecurity, harm may be technical, financial, legal, physical, social, psychological, operational, or reputational.

3.7 What is evidence?

Evidence is information used to support a conclusion. A security conclusion should be supported by logs, configurations, observations, measurements, screenshots, packet captures, code review, or documented reasoning.

4. Core Concepts

4.1 Cybersecurity in one sentence

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting digital systems, data, services, and people from harm caused by unauthorized access, misuse, disruption, modification, disclosure, or destruction.

Intuition: if a system matters and computers are involved, cybersecurity asks how that system can fail, how people can abuse it, and how to reduce the harm.

4.2 Information security versus cybersecurity

Information security focuses on protecting information and information systems. Cybersecurity often includes information security but also emphasizes networked systems, adversaries, cyber-physical systems, operational technology, online services, and the broader social impact of digital harm.

Example: protecting a spreadsheet of grades is information security. Protecting the university portal, login system, cloud database, student privacy, audit logs, and ability to publish grades reliably is cybersecurity.

4.3 Assets: the things worth protecting

Definition: an asset is anything valuable enough that loss, misuse, exposure, corruption, or disruption would matter.

Analogy: a house has assets: people, documents, keys, doors, electricity, water, money, memories, and reputation. A digital organization also has many kinds of assets, not only servers.

4.4 Stakeholders: who cares about protection?

A stakeholder is a person or group affected by security decisions. Stakeholders may include users, customers, patients, students, employees, system administrators, executives, regulators, auditors, law enforcement, researchers, suppliers, and the public.

Different stakeholders value the same asset differently. A hospital database matters to doctors because it supports care, to patients because it contains private information, to administrators because it supports operations, and to regulators because it must comply with law and policy.

4.5 Security objectives: what kind of protection is needed?

A security objective states the kind of protection required. The most famous objectives are confidentiality, integrity, and availability. They are often called the CIA triad. This lesson introduces them; Lesson 1.2 studies them deeply.

  • Confidentiality: prevent unauthorized disclosure. Example: only authorized staff can see salary data.
  • Integrity: prevent unauthorized or incorrect modification. Example: a bank balance should not change without valid transaction logic.
  • Availability: keep systems usable when needed. Example: emergency communication systems should remain available during crisis.
  • Privacy: protect people’s personal information and control inappropriate use.
  • Safety: prevent cyber failures from harming people or physical environments.
  • Authenticity: confirm that something is genuine. Example: a software update really comes from the vendor.
  • Accountability: actions can be traced to responsible identities or systems.
  • Resilience: continue operating or recover quickly after failure or attack.
  • Trust: maintain justified confidence in systems, organizations, and records.

4.6 Threats, vulnerabilities, and controls

A threat is a possible cause of harm. Examples: criminal group, careless employee, malicious insider, natural disaster, software bug, power failure, lost laptop, or misconfigured cloud storage.

A vulnerability is a weakness. Examples: weak password, missing software patch, excessive permissions, exposed database, no backups, confusing user interface, or poor logging.

A control is a safeguard. Examples: multi-factor authentication, encryption, backups, monitoring, access review, firewalls, security training, input validation, and incident response planning.

Simple relationship: a threat uses or triggers a vulnerability to cause harm to an asset. A control reduces likelihood, reduces impact, improves detection, or supports recovery.

4.7 Impact: why loss matters

Impact is the consequence if protection fails. Beginners often think impact means “computer broken.” In real organizations, impact is broader.

  • Financial impact: fraud, ransom, recovery cost, lost sales.
  • Operational impact: systems unavailable, work delayed, services interrupted.
  • Privacy impact: personal data disclosed or misused.
  • Safety impact: physical harm or unsafe conditions.
  • Legal impact: regulatory investigation, contractual breach, litigation.
  • Strategic impact: stolen intellectual property, competitive disadvantage.
  • Trust impact: users stop trusting the system or organization.

4.8 Security is contextual

The same control may be reasonable in one place and insufficient in another. A personal blog, a bank payment system, and a hospital emergency system should not have the same risk tolerance.

Risk tolerance means how much risk an organization is willing to accept. High-risk systems require stronger controls, more monitoring, better testing, and stronger governance.

4.9 The asset-protection map

A useful beginner method is to build an asset-protection map:

  1. List assets.
  2. List stakeholders.
  3. List security objectives for each asset.
  4. List possible harms if protection fails.
  5. List likely threats and weaknesses.
  6. List existing and missing controls.
  7. Record assumptions and uncertainty.

4.10 Observation, inference, and assumption

Good security analysis separates what you know from what you guess.

  • Observation: “The server log contains 500 failed login attempts from one IP address.”
  • Inference: “This may indicate password guessing.”
  • Assumption: “The log clock is accurate.”

This distinction prevents overconfident reports and supports better investigation.

4.11 What cybersecurity protects in human terms

Cybersecurity protects people’s ability to live, work, learn, transact, communicate, receive care, and make decisions without digital systems being abused against them.

This is why technical controls are not the final goal. The goal is reducing meaningful harm.

7. Mathematical Foundations

Security is not purely mathematical, but simple quantitative thinking helps organize decisions. The numbers below are approximations, not proof.

7.1 Basic risk formula

A common simplified model is:

Risk ≈ Likelihood × Impact

Likelihood estimates how probable an unwanted event is. Impact estimates how bad the consequence would be.

7.2 Worked example: student portal

Assume a university estimates the following on a 1–5 scale:

ScenarioLikelihoodImpactRisk score
Student password guessing4312
Grade database corruption2510
Portal outage during registration3412
Minor website typo313

The score does not decide automatically. It helps compare concerns. A lower-likelihood, high-impact safety issue may deserve more attention than a frequent but minor nuisance.

7.3 Expected loss

Another simple model is:

Expected Loss = Probability of Event × Loss if Event Happens

Example: if a small service has a 10% yearly probability of an outage and the estimated outage cost is $20,000, then:

Expected Loss = 0.10 × 20,000 = 2,000 dollars per year

A control that costs $500 per year and significantly reduces outage probability may be reasonable. A control that costs $100,000 per year for a low-impact system may not be reasonable.

7.4 Why the math is limited

  • Attackers adapt.
  • Historical data may be incomplete.
  • Impact can include human harm that is hard to price.
  • Organizations may underestimate rare but severe incidents.
  • Risk scores can create false precision.

Graduate-level security reasoning uses numbers carefully and records uncertainty.

8. Practical Examples

8.1 Linux example: identify local assets safely

# Show the current user.
whoami

# Show the current directory.
pwd

# List files in the current folder with permissions and sizes.
ls -lah

# Show recent local login records on your own lab VM.
last -n 5

# Show disk usage summary for the current folder only.
du -sh .

Security perspective: users, files, permissions, logs, and storage are all assets or asset metadata.

8.2 Windows example: identify local assets safely

# Show the current Windows username.
whoami

# List files in the current directory.
Get-ChildItem

# Show local IP configuration.
Get-NetIPConfiguration

# Show recent security-related event log entries on your own machine.
Get-WinEvent -LogName Security -MaxEvents 5

Security perspective: accounts, network configuration, files, and event logs help identify what exists and what might need protection.

8.3 Python example: create a simple asset inventory

# This script creates a small fictional asset inventory.
# It does not scan a network and does not contact any external system.

assets = [
    {
        "name": "Student Records Database",
        "type": "data store",
        "owner": "Registrar",
        "security_objectives": ["confidentiality", "integrity", "availability"],
        "impact_if_lost": "privacy harm and incorrect academic records"
    },
    {
        "name": "Login Service",
        "type": "identity service",
        "owner": "IT Department",
        "security_objectives": ["authenticity", "availability", "accountability"],
        "impact_if_lost": "unauthorized access or blocked users"
    }
]

for asset in assets:
    print(f"Asset: {asset['name']}")
    print(f"Type: {asset['type']}")
    print(f"Owner: {asset['owner']}")
    print("Objectives:", ", ".join(asset["security_objectives"]))
    print("Impact:", asset["impact_if_lost"])
    print("-" * 40)

8.5 Web example: what a login page protects

  • User identity.
  • Password or authentication secret.
  • Session cookie after login.
  • Personal account data.
  • Audit trail of access.
  • Service availability.

A login page is not “just a form.” It is a control point for identity, authorization, privacy, monitoring, and trust.

9. Code Examples

9.1 Safe asset inventory in Python

"""
Safe asset inventory example.
Purpose: teach asset classification without scanning real systems.
This script uses only hard-coded fictional data.
"""

# A list stores multiple assets.
# Each asset is represented as a dictionary with named fields.
assets = [
    {
        "asset_id": "A001",
        "name": "University Portal",
        "category": "web service",
        "owner": "IT Department",
        "confidentiality": 3,
        "integrity": 4,
        "availability": 5
    },
    {
        "asset_id": "A002",
        "name": "Student Grade Records",
        "category": "sensitive data",
        "owner": "Registrar",
        "confidentiality": 5,
        "integrity": 5,
        "availability": 3
    }
]

# This function computes a simple priority score.
# The formula is intentionally simple for learning.
def priority_score(asset):
    return asset["confidentiality"] + asset["integrity"] + asset["availability"]

# Print a readable report.
for asset in assets:
    score = priority_score(asset)
    print(f"{asset['asset_id']} - {asset['name']}")
    print(f"  Category: {asset['category']}")
    print(f"  Owner: {asset['owner']}")
    print(f"  CIA priority score: {score}/15")

    # Simple interpretation.
    if score >= 13:
        print("  Priority: high")
    elif score >= 9:
        print("  Priority: medium")
    else:
        print("  Priority: low")

    print()

Line-by-line explanation

  • The triple-quoted text at the top explains the script purpose.
  • assets is a list of fictional asset records.
  • Each record uses a dictionary so fields have names.
  • Confidentiality, integrity, and availability values are learning scores from 1 to 5.
  • priority_score adds the three values.
  • The loop prints a short report for every asset.
  • The if statements turn the score into a rough priority label.

Common mistakes

  • Mistake: treating the score as objective truth. Fix: record assumptions and ask stakeholders.
  • Mistake: storing real secrets in example data. Fix: use fictional values only.
  • Mistake: scanning networks to build an inventory without permission. Fix: use authorized sources only.
  • Mistake: ignoring asset owners. Fix: every important asset needs an accountable owner.

9.2 Why secure coding matters here

Even simple inventory scripts can become risky if they process sensitive data, write reports to public folders, or connect to systems without authorization. Secure coding begins with respecting scope, validating inputs, avoiding secrets in code, and writing clear logs.

10. Security Perspective

Common attacks

  • Phishing: tricking users into revealing credentials or approving harmful actions.
  • Ransomware: disrupting operations by encrypting or threatening to leak data.
  • Data theft: stealing sensitive records, intellectual property, or credentials.
  • Account takeover: using stolen or guessed credentials to act as a legitimate user.
  • Service disruption: making systems unavailable through overload, sabotage, or misconfiguration.
  • Data manipulation: changing records so decisions become wrong.
  • Supply-chain compromise: attacking trusted software, vendors, or updates.

Common vulnerabilities

  • Unknown assets.
  • Weak authentication.
  • Excessive permissions.
  • Missing patches.
  • Publicly exposed services.
  • Unencrypted sensitive data.
  • No backups or untested backups.
  • Poor logging and monitoring.
  • Unclear ownership.
  • No incident response plan.

Defensive strategies

  • Maintain an asset inventory.
  • Classify data by sensitivity and business importance.
  • Use least privilege.
  • Patch and harden systems.
  • Use multi-factor authentication for important accounts.
  • Back up critical data and test recovery.
  • Monitor important logs.
  • Train users without blaming them for system design failures.
  • Practice incident response.

Detection methods

  • Failed login monitoring.
  • Unusual account behavior.
  • Unexpected data exports.
  • Configuration drift detection.
  • Endpoint alerts.
  • Network anomaly detection.
  • Cloud audit log review.
  • Integrity checks for important files.

Mitigation and best practices

  • Protect the most important assets first.
  • Document assumptions.
  • Use layered controls.
  • Separate public, internal, sensitive, and critical systems.
  • Design for failure and recovery.
  • Make security measurable through inventories, tests, reviews, and incident exercises.

11. Industry Perspective

Banks

Banks protect money movement, customer identity, transaction integrity, fraud-detection models, payment systems, regulatory records, and public trust. Integrity is especially important because a small unauthorized change in balances can have large consequences.

Governments

Governments protect citizen services, identity systems, tax records, public safety systems, election-related infrastructure, national records, and critical public functions. Availability and trust are often central concerns.

Cloud providers

Cloud providers protect shared infrastructure, customer isolation, identity platforms, storage services, APIs, hypervisors, physical data centers, and management planes. A cloud failure can affect many organizations at once.

Healthcare

Healthcare organizations protect patient privacy, clinical records, medical devices, appointment systems, lab systems, billing, and care delivery. Cybersecurity can become a patient-safety issue when systems affect treatment.

Defense

Defense organizations protect missions, operational plans, communications, weapons-support systems, supply chains, intelligence, personnel safety, and command decisions. Confidentiality, integrity, availability, resilience, and safety can all be mission-critical.

Universities

Universities protect student records, research data, grant information, learning systems, identity platforms, labs, intellectual property, and academic trust. They also support open collaboration, which makes security governance harder.

Large enterprises

Large enterprises protect customer data, employee data, financial systems, enterprise resource planning systems, manufacturing operations, legal records, brand reputation, cloud services, and supplier connections.

12. Research Perspective

Researchers study what should be protected, how to measure harm, and how to design systems that remain trustworthy under adversarial pressure.

Current research directions

  • Security economics: how incentives, costs, markets, and insurance affect protection decisions.
  • Human-centered security: how real users understand warnings, passwords, privacy choices, and security workflows.
  • Cyber-physical safety: how digital compromise can affect vehicles, medical devices, factories, grids, and infrastructure.
  • Privacy engineering: how to design systems that collect less data and limit misuse.
  • Security metrics: how to measure risk, control effectiveness, resilience, and incident impact without false precision.
  • Asset discovery at scale: how large organizations identify unknown systems, shadow IT, cloud assets, and dependencies.
  • Supply-chain risk: how to understand what software depends on and who can influence it.
  • AI security: how models, training data, prompts, outputs, and AI-enabled workflows become protected assets.

Open problems

  • How can organizations quantify human and societal harm without reducing everything to money?
  • How can defenders maintain accurate asset inventories in fast-changing cloud environments?
  • How should security teams prioritize rare but catastrophic events?
  • How can privacy, transparency, and security monitoring be balanced?
  • How can researchers evaluate real-world security controls without exposing sensitive data?

Notable research directions to explore

  • Security engineering and secure system design.
  • Usable security and privacy.
  • Risk modeling and cyber insurance.
  • Formal methods for high-assurance systems.
  • Resilience engineering for critical infrastructure.
  • Empirical studies of breaches and vulnerability management.

13. Case Studies

13.1 Equifax breach: sensitive data and patching

What happened: in 2017, Equifax suffered a major breach involving sensitive personal information. Public investigations connected the incident to a known Apache Struts vulnerability and failures around vulnerability management and remediation.

Root cause theme: sensitive personal data was a critical asset, and the organization failed to reduce exposure after a serious known software weakness became public.

Impact: the incident affected public trust, consumer privacy, regulatory scrutiny, and organizational reputation.

Lessons learned: know where sensitive data exists, know which systems expose it, patch critical systems promptly, verify that remediation occurred, and monitor for suspicious access.

13.2 WannaCry: availability, patching, and operational disruption

What happened: WannaCry ransomware spread globally in 2017 and abused a Microsoft SMB vulnerability for which Microsoft had released a security update before the outbreak.

Root cause theme: unpatched systems, exposed network services, and weak resilience increased the impact.

Impact: organizations faced disrupted operations, unavailable systems, and expensive recovery efforts.

Lessons learned: availability is a security objective, patching is a protection activity, and backups plus segmentation can reduce ransomware impact.

13.3 Colonial Pipeline: cyber incident as physical-world disruption

What happened: in May 2021, Colonial Pipeline proactively shut down pipeline operations in response to a ransomware attack, causing visible fuel-supply disruption concerns.

Root cause theme: a cyber incident affecting business systems can force operational decisions with physical-world consequences.

Impact: the incident highlighted critical infrastructure risk, ransomware response challenges, and the importance of resilience planning.

Lessons learned: cybersecurity protects operations, not just files. Incident response, backups, identity controls, segmentation, and executive decision-making all matter.

14. Common Misconceptions

  • Misconception: cybersecurity means hacking. Correction: cybersecurity includes prevention, design, monitoring, response, governance, law, privacy, and resilience.
  • Misconception: only data matters. Correction: services, identities, processes, safety, decisions, and trust also matter.
  • Misconception: firewalls solve cybersecurity. Correction: firewalls are one control, not a full security program.
  • Misconception: small organizations have nothing worth protecting. Correction: even small organizations have accounts, payments, private records, operations, and reputation.
  • Misconception: if data is public, integrity does not matter. Correction: public data can still cause harm if modified.
  • Misconception: availability is an IT operations issue, not security. Correction: denial-of-service, ransomware, sabotage, and destructive attacks make availability a security objective.
  • Misconception: risk scores are exact science. Correction: risk scores are decision aids with assumptions and uncertainty.
  • Misconception: users are the weakest link. Correction: users often operate inside poorly designed systems. Good security reduces unreasonable burden on users.
  • Misconception: compliance means security. Correction: compliance can help but does not guarantee effective protection.
  • Misconception: security is finished after deployment. Correction: assets, threats, vulnerabilities, and controls change over time.

15. Summary

Cybersecurity protects valuable assets from meaningful harm. Those assets include data, systems, services, software, identities, people, operations, safety, privacy, money, reputation, and trust.

The first job of a security professional is not to choose a tool. It is to understand what matters, who depends on it, what could go wrong, how bad the impact would be, and what evidence supports that judgment.

A strong security mindset separates observation from inference, documents assumptions, and connects technical controls to real-world consequences.

A weakness that can contribute to security harm.

18. Practice Lab

Lab title

Build a Protection Map for a Fictional University Portal

Safety scope

  • Do not scan, test, or access any real university system.
  • Use only the fictional scenario below.
  • Do not collect real personal data.
  • Do not attempt phishing, password guessing, exploitation, or network probing.

Scenario

A fictional university has an online portal called CampusOne. Students use it to view grades, register for courses, download fee invoices, and update contact information. Teachers use it to upload grades. Administrators use it to manage student records. The portal uses a login page, a web application server, a student database, a file storage system, email notifications, and audit logs.

Prerequisites

  • Basic reading of this lesson.
  • Text editor or spreadsheet.
  • Optional Python from Lesson 0.4.

Objectives

  • Identify protected assets.
  • Identify stakeholders.
  • Assign security objectives.
  • Describe harms.
  • Suggest basic controls.
  • Record assumptions and uncertainty.

Step 1: Create an asset table

| Asset | Type | Owner | Stakeholders | Security objectives | Harm if protection fails |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Student grades | Data | Registrar | Students, teachers, university | Confidentiality, integrity, availability | Privacy loss, wrong academic decisions |
| Login service | Identity service | IT | All users | Authenticity, availability, accountability | Account takeover, blocked access |

Step 2: Add threats and vulnerabilities

| Asset | Threat | Possible vulnerability | Existing control | Missing control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Student grades | Unauthorized user | Weak access control | Login required | Role-based access review |
| Login service | Password guessing | No MFA | Passwords | MFA and failed-login monitoring |

Step 3: Estimate simple risk

Use a 1–5 scale for likelihood and impact.

| Scenario | Likelihood | Impact | Score |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Password guessing against student accounts | 4 | 3 | 12 |
| Unauthorized grade modification | 2 | 5 | 10 |

Step 4: Write assumptions

  • Assumption: CampusOne stores grades in one central database.
  • Assumption: teachers should modify only their own course grades.
  • Assumption: students should read only their own records.
  • Uncertainty: the scenario does not specify whether MFA exists.

Step 5: Optional Python table generator

assets = [
    ["Student grades", "Data", "Registrar", "Confidentiality, integrity, availability"],
    ["Login service", "Identity", "IT", "Authenticity, availability, accountability"],
    ["Audit logs", "Evidence", "Security team", "Integrity, availability, accountability"],
]

print("| Asset | Type | Owner | Objectives |")
print("|---|---|---|---|")
for row in assets:
    print("| " + " | ".join(row) + " |")

Expected outcomes

  • A completed asset-protection table.
  • A threat/vulnerability/control table.
  • A simple risk table.
  • A list of assumptions and uncertainties.
  • A one-page summary explaining the top three protection priorities.

Lab report checklist

  • At least 10 assets.
  • At least 5 stakeholder groups.
  • At least 3 security objectives per critical asset.
  • At least 5 harms.
  • At least 5 controls.
  • Clear assumptions.
  • No real target systems, names, credentials, or private data.
