Lesson 1.4: Threat Modeling from First Principles

Threat modeling is the practice of looking at a system before or during design and asking: what are we protecting, from whom, where can things go wrong, how bad would it be, and what should we change? This lesson teaches threat modeling from zero knowledge, then builds toward graduate-level reasoning about data flows, trust boundaries, attacker goals, abuse cases, STRIDE, mitigations, assumptions, and uncertainty.

Difficulty levelBeginner → Graduate foundation

Week 1 Context

Overview: Lesson 1.1 explained what cybersecurity protects. Lesson 1.2 introduced security goals. Lesson 1.3 separated threats, vulnerabilities, exploits, and risk. Lesson 1.4 turns those ideas into a repeatable design method: threat modeling.

Learning objectives: after this lesson you should be able to create a simple threat model, draw a data-flow diagram, identify trust boundaries, ask STRIDE-style threat questions, write abuse cases, propose mitigations, and document assumptions.

Required background: no prior threat-modeling knowledge is required. Helpful ideas: asset, confidentiality, integrity, availability, threat, vulnerability, risk, and control.

Estimated reading time: 180–260 minutes. Difficulty: starts at beginner level and ends with structured design review and research-level limitations.

1. Introduction

Plain-English idea: threat modeling is security planning before damage happens. Instead of waiting for a breach, you inspect the design, imagine realistic ways it could fail, and improve it before attackers or accidents expose the weakness.

Think about designing a house. Before building, an architect asks where doors go, which walls need locks, where fire exits belong, and how people will move safely. Threat modeling does the same for software, networks, data, cloud systems, hospitals, banks, and universities.

Why the topic exists: many serious security problems are design problems. A scanner may find missing patches, but it may not find that the wrong employee role can approve payments, that audit logs can be edited by administrators, or that password-reset messages leak sensitive information. Threat modeling exists to find these problems while the design is still changeable.

Why it matters: fixing security late is usually more expensive and disruptive than fixing it during design. Threat modeling helps teams spend limited effort on meaningful risks instead of random fear or tool output.

Where it is used: software development, cloud architecture, banking platforms, identity systems, healthcare workflows, defense systems, industrial control systems, university portals, research platforms, and incident-preparedness exercises.

Who uses it: developers, security engineers, architects, product managers, privacy engineers, compliance teams, incident responders, red teams, blue teams, auditors, researchers, and executives.

Safety boundary

This lesson is about modeling and defending systems. Do not use it to plan unauthorized activity against real systems. All examples use fictional systems, local files, and authorized classroom scenarios.

2. Learning Outcomes

  • Define threat modeling in simple and technical language.
  • Explain why threat modeling belongs early in design and throughout system life.
  • Identify assets, users, components, external dependencies, data stores, data flows, entry points, exits, and trust boundaries.
  • Create a beginner-level data-flow diagram for a small system.
  • Explain STRIDE: spoofing, tampering, repudiation, information disclosure, denial of service, and elevation of privilege.
  • Use STRIDE as a question set, not as a magic checklist.
  • Write abuse cases and misuse cases safely.
  • Build a simple attack tree without giving real-world attack instructions.
  • Connect threats to security goals, controls, risk, and requirements.
  • Prioritize threats using impact, likelihood, exploitability, exposure, asset value, and uncertainty.
  • Document assumptions, non-goals, unresolved questions, and accepted risks.
  • Explain how threat modeling changes across web, network, cloud, identity, healthcare, banking, defense, and research systems.
  • Discuss open research problems in scalable, automated, AI-assisted, and socio-technical threat modeling.

3. Prerequisites

3.1 System

A system is a group of parts that work together. In cybersecurity, a system may include people, software, servers, networks, cloud services, databases, mobile apps, documents, business processes, and third-party providers.

3.2 Asset

An asset is something valuable. Examples include student records, bank balances, identity tokens, source code, patient history, electricity service, research data, and human safety.

3.3 Data

Data is information a system stores, processes, or transmits. Threat modeling often starts with data because data usually explains why attackers, insiders, accidents, and failures matter.

3.4 Component

A component is one part of a system. Examples: browser, web server, application server, database, identity provider, payment gateway, logging service, and administrator console.

3.5 Control

A control is a safeguard that prevents, detects, corrects, deters, or helps recover from unwanted events. Examples: multi-factor authentication, input validation, encryption, rate limiting, monitoring, backups, segregation of duties, and incident response procedures.

3.6 Assumption

An assumption is something you believe is true but have not fully proven. Example: “Only employees can reach the admin portal.” Good threat models label assumptions because hidden assumptions often become real vulnerabilities.

4. Core Concepts

4.1 What is a threat model?

A threat model is a structured explanation of what could go wrong with a system and what should be done about it. It is not just a diagram. It includes assets, architecture, assumptions, trust boundaries, threats, risks, mitigations, owners, and open questions.

Intuition: a threat model is like a map for defensive thinking. Without a map, a team may install tools randomly. With a map, the team can see where the valuable assets are, how data moves, where attackers might interact, and where controls are needed.

4.2 The four basic questions

  1. What are we building or protecting? Define scope, assets, users, components, and data flows.
  2. What can go wrong? Identify threats, abuse cases, trust-boundary violations, and failure modes.
  3. What are we doing about it? Choose mitigations, controls, requirements, design changes, monitoring, and response plans.
  4. Did we do a good enough job? Review coverage, test assumptions, track residual risk, and update the model when the system changes.

4.3 Scope

Scope defines what the threat model covers and what it does not cover. A threat model for “the whole internet” is useless. A threat model for “the password-reset flow of the fictional CampusOne portal” is manageable.

Good scope example: “Student login, password reset, session management, and grade viewing in CampusOne.”

Bad scope example: “Make CampusOne secure.” This is too vague.

4.4 Assets and security objectives

Assets answer “what matters?” Security objectives answer “what kind of protection matters?”

AssetSecurity objectiveExample harm
Student gradesIntegrityUnauthorized grade changes harm fairness and trust.
Password-reset tokenConfidentiality and integrityToken theft can lead to account takeover.
Registration serviceAvailabilityStudents may fail to enroll on time.
Audit logsIntegrity and accountabilityAttackers or insiders may hide actions.

4.5 Data-flow diagram

A data-flow diagram, often shortened to DFD, is a simple picture of how data moves through a system. It usually shows users, processes, data stores, external systems, and flows between them.

Analogy: a DFD is like a plumbing diagram. It does not show every screw in the building. It shows where important material flows and where leaks could matter.

4.6 Trust boundary

A trust boundary is a line where the level of trust changes. Crossing a trust boundary should make you ask stronger security questions.

Examples:

  • Internet user → web application.
  • Student role → administrator role.
  • Application server → database.
  • University network → cloud provider.
  • Unauthenticated request → authenticated session.

Intuition: when data crosses a trust boundary, do not assume it is safe. Validate it, authenticate it, authorize it, log it, and protect it as needed.

4.7 Entry points and exit points

An entry point is where something enters the system: login form, API endpoint, file upload, email, USB device, admin panel, support request, or network connection. An exit point is where data leaves: report export, email notification, API response, log stream, backup, dashboard, or third-party integration.

4.8 Threat source, threat event, and abuse case

A threat source is the origin of possible harm: external attacker, malicious insider, careless user, compromised vendor, software bug, natural disaster, or system failure.

A threat event is something that happens: a stolen credential is used, data is modified, service is overwhelmed, a backup fails, or a user is tricked into approving a request.

An abuse case describes how a system could be misused. Safe abuse case example: “A person with stolen credentials tries to view another student’s grade record.” It describes the security concern without giving operational steps for attacking a real target.

4.9 STRIDE

STRIDE is a threat-category model commonly associated with Microsoft threat modeling. It helps teams ask structured questions instead of relying on random brainstorming.

STRIDE categoryPlain-English meaningSecurity goal affectedExample question
SpoofingPretending to be someone or something else.AuthenticityCan someone impersonate a student, admin, server, or service?
TamperingUnauthorized modification.IntegrityCan someone change grades, requests, logs, tokens, or configuration?
RepudiationDenying an action without enough evidence.AccountabilityCan a user deny approving a transaction because logging is weak?
Information disclosureUnauthorized reading or exposure.Confidentiality and privacyCan sensitive records leak through responses, logs, backups, or analytics?
Denial of serviceMaking something unavailable.AvailabilityCan a service be overwhelmed or disabled?
Elevation of privilegeGaining more access than allowed.AuthorizationCan a student perform instructor or administrator actions?

Important: STRIDE is a thinking aid, not proof of security. A complete threat model also needs context, likelihood, impact, assumptions, controls, and review.

4.10 Attack trees

An attack tree is a diagram that starts with an unwanted goal and breaks it into possible paths. In this course, attack trees must stay conceptual and defensive. They should help defenders see what controls are needed, not provide real-world instructions.

4.11 Mitigation

A mitigation is an action that reduces risk. A mitigation can change design, add a control, remove an unsafe feature, add monitoring, improve recovery, or transfer responsibility through a contract.

4.12 Security requirement

A security requirement is a clear rule the system must satisfy. Example: “All administrator actions that modify grades must require multi-factor authentication and must create tamper-resistant audit records.”

4.13 Residual risk and decision ownership

Residual risk is the risk left after controls are applied. A technical team can describe residual risk, but business or institutional leadership usually owns the decision to accept it.

4.14 Threat modeling lifecycle

Threat models become stale. They should be updated when architecture changes, new data is collected, authentication changes, third-party services are added, cloud permissions change, incidents occur, or new threat intelligence becomes relevant.

7. Mathematical Foundations

7.1 Risk as an expected loss idea

At a basic level, risk can be approximated as:

Risk ≈ Likelihood × Impact

If likelihood is 0.20 per year and estimated impact is $100,000, then:

Expected annual loss ≈ 0.20 × 100,000 = 20,000

This does not mean exactly $20,000 will be lost. It means that under the assumptions, the average loss over many comparable periods would be about $20,000 per year.

7.2 Ordinal scoring

Beginner risk matrices often use 1–5 scales:

Risk score = Likelihood score × Impact score

Example:

Likelihood = 4
Impact = 5
Risk score = 4 × 5 = 20

Warning: 1–5 scores are ordinal approximations. A score of 4 is not necessarily exactly twice a score of 2. Use them for discussion and prioritization, not false precision.

7.3 Control effectiveness

A simple teaching model for residual risk is:

Residual risk = Inherent risk × (1 - Control effectiveness)

If inherent risk is 20 and controls reduce estimated risk by 60%:

Residual risk = 20 × (1 - 0.60) = 8

This is a simplification. Real control effectiveness is uncertain and may fail under stress, misconfiguration, or human error.

7.4 Coverage ratio

Threat model coverage can be roughly tracked as:

Coverage = Reviewed components / Total known components

If a design has 12 known components and the team reviewed 9:

Coverage = 9 / 12 = 0.75 = 75%

This does not prove security. It only shows how much of the known design has been reviewed.

8. Practical Examples

8.1 Linux example: identify local components

This safe command lists listening services on your own machine only. It helps you understand what components expose entry points.

ss -tulpen

Questions for the threat model:

  • Which services are listening?
  • Which users own those services?
  • Which services are necessary?
  • Which logs show access attempts?

8.2 Windows example: identify local services

Get-Service | Sort-Object Status, Name | Select-Object -First 20

Threat-modeling questions:

  • Which services are running?
  • Which services start automatically?
  • Which services are security-critical?
  • Who can change service configuration?

8.3 Python example: safe risk-register helper

# Safe local script: it does not connect to any network.
# It helps students rank fictional threat-model items.

risks = [
    {"id": "T1", "name": "Admin account misuse", "likelihood": 4, "impact": 5},
    {"id": "T2", "name": "Announcement page outage", "likelihood": 3, "impact": 2},
    {"id": "T3", "name": "Editable audit logs", "likelihood": 3, "impact": 5},
]

for risk in risks:
    # Multiply likelihood and impact for a simple teaching score.
    risk["score"] = risk["likelihood"] * risk["impact"]

# Sort highest score first so the most serious teaching examples appear first.
ranked = sorted(risks, key=lambda item: item["score"], reverse=True)

for risk in ranked:
    print(f"{risk['id']}: {risk['name']} -> score {risk['score']}")

Line-by-line explanation:

  • risks = [...] creates a list of fictional risk items.
  • Each item is a dictionary with an identifier, name, likelihood, and impact.
  • The loop calculates a simple teaching score.
  • sorted(..., reverse=True) orders the results from highest score to lowest score.
  • The final loop prints a readable ranking.

Common mistake: treating the score as truth. The score is a conversation starter. Evidence, exposure, controls, and uncertainty still matter.

8.4 Network example: trust boundary review

For a fictional web application, draw three zones:

  • Public internet.
  • Application network.
  • Database network.

Then ask: what data crosses each boundary, which identity is used, what is authenticated, what is authorized, what is logged, and what happens if the request is malformed?

8.5 Web example: password-reset flow

Threat-model the password-reset flow without attacking anything:

  1. User requests reset.
  2. System creates reset token.
  3. System sends email.
  4. User clicks link.
  5. System validates token.
  6. User sets new password.

Questions:

  • Can the token be guessed?
  • Can the token be reused?
  • Can the token leak into logs or browser history?
  • Can a support employee reset accounts without oversight?
  • Is the user notified after a password change?

8.6 Cloud example: storage bucket review

For a fictional cloud bucket storing course files:

  • Asset: course submissions and feedback files.
  • Threat: unauthorized reading or deletion.
  • Trust boundary: university identity provider to cloud storage permission.
  • Control: least-privilege access, logging, versioning, encryption, backup, and review of public access settings.

9. Code Examples

9.1 Commented Python data-flow model

# This script models a tiny fictional system for learning purposes.
# It does not scan, attack, or connect to anything.

components = [
    "Student Browser",
    "Web Application",
    "Identity Provider",
    "Grade Database",
    "Audit Log Store",
]

flows = [
    ("Student Browser", "Web Application", "Login request"),
    ("Web Application", "Identity Provider", "Credential validation"),
    ("Web Application", "Grade Database", "Read grade records"),
    ("Web Application", "Audit Log Store", "Write audit event"),
]

trust_boundaries = [
    ("Student Browser", "Web Application", "Internet to application boundary"),
    ("Web Application", "Grade Database", "Application to data boundary"),
]

print("Components:")
for component in components:
    print("-", component)

print("\nData flows:")
for source, destination, description in flows:
    print(f"- {source} -> {destination}: {description}")

print("\nTrust boundaries:")
for source, destination, boundary in trust_boundaries:
    print(f"- {boundary}: {source} -> {destination}")

9.2 Why this code is safe

  • It uses hardcoded fictional data.
  • It does not perform network requests.
  • It does not read sensitive files.
  • It does not test real systems.

9.3 Secure coding connection

Threat modeling improves secure coding because it tells developers which code paths are sensitive. For example, if the model says “grade updates must be tamper-resistant,” developers know to enforce server-side authorization, validate inputs, log events, protect logs, and test unauthorized role attempts.

9.4 Common coding mistakes discovered by threat modeling

  • Authorization checked only in the browser instead of on the server.
  • Tokens stored in logs or URLs.
  • Administrator actions missing audit logs.
  • Service accounts given broad permissions.
  • File uploads trusted because they came from logged-in users.
  • Error messages leaking system details.

10. Security Perspective

10.1 Common attacks considered during threat modeling

  • Credential theft and account takeover.
  • Unauthorized data access.
  • Privilege escalation.
  • Data tampering.
  • Business-logic abuse.
  • Denial of service.
  • Supply-chain compromise.
  • Insider misuse.
  • Logging and monitoring bypass.
  • Data leakage through backups, exports, analytics, or support tools.

10.2 Common vulnerabilities discovered

  • Missing authorization checks.
  • Weak authentication flows.
  • Unclear ownership of sensitive data.
  • Over-permissive cloud roles.
  • No rate limiting on sensitive actions.
  • Secrets stored in code or configuration files.
  • Unencrypted sensitive data at rest or in transit.
  • Logs that are missing, incomplete, or editable.
  • No backup restore testing.
  • Third-party dependencies trusted without review.

10.3 Defensive strategies

  • Use least privilege for users, services, databases, and cloud roles.
  • Require multi-factor authentication for privileged actions.
  • Enforce server-side authorization.
  • Validate inputs at trust boundaries.
  • Use secure session management.
  • Protect sensitive data with encryption and access controls.
  • Make audit logs tamper-resistant.
  • Monitor high-risk actions.
  • Test recovery procedures.
  • Review architecture after meaningful changes.

10.4 Detection methods

  • Authentication anomaly alerts.
  • Administrative action logging.
  • Unexpected role-change alerts.
  • Unusual data export volume alerts.
  • Database access monitoring.
  • Configuration drift detection.
  • Cloud permission-change alerts.
  • Integrity checks for logs and critical files.

10.5 Mitigation and best practices

  • Start small. Threat-model one flow before modeling the whole organization.
  • Use diagrams but do not stop at diagrams.
  • Write threats as complete sentences.
  • Record assumptions and unresolved questions.
  • Assign owners and due dates to mitigations.
  • Review the model after design changes and incidents.
  • Connect threat-model outputs to tickets, tests, monitoring, and documentation.

11. Industry Perspective

11.1 Banks

Banks threat-model payment flows, customer authentication, fraud workflows, transaction approval, mobile banking, insider misuse, and third-party integrations. They care strongly about integrity, non-repudiation, availability, fraud detection, and regulatory evidence.

11.2 Governments

Government systems threat-model citizen data, identity services, public-service availability, election-support systems, records integrity, insider risk, and nation-state threats. They also need strong auditability and procurement controls.

11.3 Cloud providers

Cloud providers threat-model isolation between tenants, identity and access management, control planes, data-plane permissions, hardware trust boundaries, logging, abuse prevention, and customer misconfiguration patterns.

11.4 Healthcare

Healthcare threat modeling includes patient confidentiality, clinical workflow availability, medical-device safety, ransomware resilience, emergency access, audit logs, and third-party billing or laboratory systems.

11.5 Defense

Defense systems threat-model mission impact, communications integrity, supply-chain trust, insider threats, hardware assurance, operational security, and cyber-physical effects.

11.6 Universities

Universities threat-model student records, research data, learning platforms, identity systems, open networks, public websites, laboratories, grants, and collaboration with external researchers.

11.7 Large enterprises

Large enterprises use threat modeling for new products, mergers, cloud migration, software development, data pipelines, internal applications, AI systems, and vendor integrations.

12. Research Perspective

Threat modeling remains an active research area because modern systems are too large, dynamic, and interconnected for manual diagrams alone.

12.1 Current research directions

  • Scalable threat modeling: methods for large cloud-native and microservice systems.
  • Automated model extraction: deriving partial models from code, infrastructure-as-code, logs, cloud configuration, and API descriptions.
  • AI-assisted threat modeling: using language models to suggest threats while controlling hallucination and unsupported claims.
  • Security of AI systems: adapting threat modeling to prompt injection, data poisoning, model extraction, unsafe tool use, and autonomous agents.
  • Socio-technical modeling: including humans, incentives, procedures, organizations, and usability failures.
  • Risk quantification: connecting threat models to measurable loss, uncertainty, and decision theory.
  • Continuous threat modeling: updating models as systems and dependencies change.

12.2 Open problems

  • How can teams know whether a threat model is complete enough?
  • How can models remain current in fast-moving DevOps environments?
  • How can tools avoid overwhelming engineers with low-value threats?
  • How can security teams measure mitigation effectiveness?
  • How should threat models represent privacy, safety, and human harm?
  • How can AI-generated threat suggestions be verified?

12.3 Notable reading directions

Students should explore STRIDE, attack trees, PASTA, LINDDUN for privacy threat modeling, data-centric threat modeling, adversarial machine learning threat models, secure software design, and empirical studies of how teams actually perform threat modeling.

13. Case Studies

13.1 SolarWinds supply-chain compromise

What happened: attackers compromised a software build/update pathway and distributed maliciously modified software to many downstream organizations.

Threat-modeling lesson: a system boundary cannot stop at the application runtime. Build systems, signing infrastructure, update channels, vendor dependencies, and administrative access are also part of the security model.

Root cause pattern: trusted software supply-chain paths can become high-impact attack paths.

Impact: widespread compromise risk across organizations using the affected software.

Lessons learned: threat models should include build pipelines, update mechanisms, vendor trust, monitoring, and blast-radius limits.

13.2 Equifax breach

What happened: attackers exploited a known vulnerability in a public-facing application component, leading to exposure of sensitive personal information.

Threat-modeling lesson: public-facing components connected to high-value personal data deserve strong inventory, patching, segmentation, monitoring, and escalation processes.

Root cause pattern: asset sensitivity, internet exposure, patch management, and detection gaps combined into high risk.

Impact: large-scale exposure of personal data and major organizational, legal, and reputational consequences.

13.3 Capital One cloud breach

What happened: a cloud-related misconfiguration and access path enabled unauthorized access to sensitive data.

Threat-modeling lesson: cloud identity, metadata services, network paths, and storage permissions must be part of architecture threat modeling.

Root cause pattern: trust boundaries and cloud permissions were not sufficiently constrained for the risk.

13.4 Colonial Pipeline ransomware incident

What happened: ransomware disruption affected business operations connected to fuel distribution.

Threat-modeling lesson: threat models should include availability, business continuity, identity security, incident response, and operational impact, not only data confidentiality.

14. Common Misconceptions

Misconception 1: Threat modeling is only for experts.

Wrong. Beginners can start by asking what is valuable, who can interact with it, what can go wrong, and what controls exist. Expert review improves depth, but basic modeling is accessible.

Misconception 2: A diagram is the threat model.

Incomplete. The diagram is only one artifact. The model also needs threats, assumptions, mitigations, owners, and residual risk.

Misconception 3: STRIDE finds everything.

Wrong. STRIDE is useful, but no checklist finds every threat. Business logic, privacy harm, safety, insider misuse, and supply-chain risk may need additional methods.

Misconception 4: Threat modeling happens once.

Wrong. Systems change. New features, cloud roles, integrations, incidents, and attacker behavior require updates.

Misconception 5: Threat modeling requires exploit details.

Wrong. Defensive threat modeling can describe abuse and controls without operational attack instructions.

Misconception 6: Low likelihood means ignore it.

Not always. A low-likelihood event with catastrophic safety, legal, or mission impact may still require strong controls.

Misconception 7: Security team owns every mitigation.

Wrong. Product, engineering, operations, legal, privacy, compliance, and leadership often own different parts of mitigation and risk acceptance.

Misconception 8: Tool-generated threats are automatically correct.

Wrong. Tools can help, but human review is needed to remove irrelevant items, add missing context, and validate assumptions.

15. Summary

Threat modeling is a structured way to reason about what could go wrong before harm occurs. It starts with scope, assets, components, data flows, and trust boundaries. It uses methods such as STRIDE, abuse cases, and attack trees to identify threats. It then connects threats to risks, controls, requirements, owners, and residual risk decisions.

The most important lesson is not memorizing a framework. The most important lesson is learning to ask disciplined questions: what matters, who can interact with it, where trust changes, what can go wrong, how would we know, and what should we change?

16. Glossary

Abuse Case
A description of how a system could be misused to cause harm.
Attack Tree
A diagram that starts with an unwanted goal and breaks it into possible paths.
Component
A part of a system, such as a browser, server, database, API, or identity provider.
Data Flow
Movement of data from one component, user, or system to another.
Data-Flow Diagram
A diagram showing components, data stores, external entities, and data movement.
Entry Point
A place where input, requests, people, devices, or data enter a system.
Exit Point
A place where data or output leaves a system.
Mitigation
An action or control that reduces risk.
Non-goal
Something intentionally outside the current scope.
Security Requirement
A clear rule that a system must satisfy to protect security objectives.
Spoofing
Pretending to be another identity, system, service, or user.
STRIDE
A threat-category model: spoofing, tampering, repudiation, information disclosure, denial of service, and elevation of privilege.
Threat Model
A structured representation of threats, assumptions, architecture, risks, and mitigations for a system.
Trust Boundary
A point where the level of trust changes between users, components, networks, services, or roles.

18. Practice Lab

Lab title

Create a Threat Model for a Fictional University Password-Reset System

Safety rules

  • Use only the fictional CampusOne scenario.
  • Do not test, scan, probe, or log into any real system.
  • Do not use real student, employee, customer, or patient data.
  • Do not search for or use real exploit instructions.

Prerequisites

  • Lessons 1.1–1.3.
  • Basic drawing tool, text editor, or Markdown.
  • Optional: Mermaid preview support.

Objectives

  • Define scope and non-goals.
  • Identify assets and security objectives.
  • Draw a data-flow diagram.
  • Mark trust boundaries.
  • Use STRIDE to identify threats.
  • Write mitigations and residual-risk notes.

Scenario

CampusOne is a fictional university portal. Students can request a password reset by entering their university email address. The system sends a reset link. The link expires after a period of time. The student opens the link, sets a new password, and receives a confirmation email.

Step 1: Define scope

In scope:
- Password-reset request page
- Reset-token generation
- Reset email delivery
- Reset-link validation
- Password change
- Audit logging

Out of scope:
- Real university systems
- Real email accounts
- Public scanning
- Exploit testing

Step 2: Identify assets

| Asset | Main goal | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Reset token | Confidentiality, integrity | Token misuse can lead to account takeover. |
| Student account | Confidentiality, integrity | Account access exposes personal and academic data. |
| Audit log | Integrity, accountability | Investigation depends on trustworthy records. |
| Email delivery | Availability | Users need reset messages to arrive. |

Step 3: Draw a data-flow diagram

flowchart LR
    User[Student Browser] -->|Reset request| Web[CampusOne Web App]
    Web -->|Create token| TokenDB[(Reset Token Store)]
    Web -->|Send reset link| Email[Email Service]
    User -->|Open reset link| Web
    Web -->|Validate token| TokenDB
    Web -->|Update password| AccountDB[(Account Database)]
    Web -->|Write event| Logs[(Audit Logs)]

Step 4: Mark trust boundaries

  • Student browser to web app: untrusted internet input enters the application.
  • Web app to token store: application logic accesses sensitive reset state.
  • Web app to email service: data leaves to a third-party or separate service.
  • Web app to audit logs: security evidence is created.

Step 5: Use STRIDE prompts

| STRIDE | Example question | Possible control |
|---|---|---|
| Spoofing | Can someone pretend to be the account owner? | MFA or secondary verification for high-risk changes. |
| Tampering | Can a token be modified or reused? | Signed tokens, server-side token state, one-time use. |
| Repudiation | Can a user or admin deny reset activity? | Tamper-resistant audit logs. |
| Information disclosure | Can reset tokens leak in logs or URLs? | Avoid logging tokens; limit token lifetime. |
| Denial of service | Can repeated requests flood email or lock accounts? | Rate limiting and abuse monitoring. |
| Elevation of privilege | Can reset flow grant access to another role? | Server-side authorization and role checks. |

Step 6: Create mitigation tickets

| ID | Threat | Mitigation | Owner | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TM-1 | Reset tokens may leak into logs | Redact reset-token values from logs | App team | Open |
| TM-2 | Repeated reset requests may flood users | Add rate limiting and alerting | Platform team | Open |
| TM-3 | Weak audit logging may prevent investigation | Log reset requested, token used, password changed | Security team | Open |

Step 7: Document assumptions and open questions

Assumptions:
- Email service supports secure transport.
- Reset links expire.
- Password changes are logged.

Open questions:
- Are reset tokens stored hashed?
- Who can view reset logs?
- Are support staff allowed to trigger resets?
- How are suspicious reset spikes detected?

Expected outcome

You should submit a short threat-model report with scope, diagram, trust boundaries, STRIDE table, mitigations, assumptions, and open questions. The report should be defensive and should not include operational attack instructions against real systems.
