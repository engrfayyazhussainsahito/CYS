Week 13 · Lesson 13.5 · Offensive Security Methodology

Reporting, Remediation, Retesting, and Purple-Team Handoff

A professional assessment does not end when testing stops. It ends when findings are clear, owners can act, fixes are validated, residual risk is explicit, and defenders receive useful detection and response lessons.

Estimated reading time100 minutes
Difficulty levelIntermediate
Required backgroundLessons 13.1–13.4, Week 11 SOC, Week 12 IR and evidence handling
Practice time90–120 minutes

1. Introduction

Offensive-security reporting is often weaker than the testing that produced it. A tester may discover a real issue, but if the report is vague, exaggerated, missing evidence, missing an owner, or missing retest criteria, the organization still cannot reduce risk reliably.

This lesson treats reporting as an operational control. A good report converts authorized assessment observations into decisions: what must be fixed, by whom, by when, how closure will be verified, what risk remains, and what defenders should improve.

The lesson also introduces purple-team handoff. The handoff is not a ceremonial meeting after a penetration test. It is the process of converting validated offensive observations into stronger logging, detections, response playbooks, threat models, control requirements, and engineering tests.

2. Learning Outcomes

  • Write findings that separate fact, inference, uncertainty, and business impact.
  • Convert validated findings into remediation tickets with owners, due dates, dependencies, and acceptance criteria.
  • Define safe retest criteria that verify the fix without repeating unnecessary or unsafe testing.
  • Explain how severity, likelihood, exploit activity, asset criticality, compensating controls, and business context affect prioritization.
  • Create a purple-team handoff that maps findings to telemetry, detections, playbooks, and ATT&CK-style behavior only when evidence supports the mapping.
  • Use a safe local Python lab to generate fictional report artifacts, remediation tickets, retest cases, and defensive handoff notes.

3. Research Basis

This lesson is grounded in assessment and vulnerability-management guidance, not tool folklore. NIST SP 800-115 frames testing as a process that includes planning, execution, analysis, reporting, and mitigation strategy development. NIST SP 800-40 Rev. 4 treats patching as enterprise preventive maintenance that must account for technology diversity, prioritization, risk, and operational constraints.

CVSS v4.0 is used here as a severity communication framework, not as a complete business-risk answer. EPSS adds exploitation-likelihood context. CISA KEV adds evidence that a vulnerability is known to be exploited. SSVC-style thinking helps stakeholders decide response actions based on mission impact, exposure, exploitation status, and organizational priorities. MITRE ATT&CK is used as a vocabulary for observed adversary behavior and detection opportunities; unsupported mappings are explicitly discouraged.

Important: none of these frameworks replaces judgment. A low CVSS issue on a critical identity path can matter. A high CVSS issue on a decommissioned asset may need asset cleanup rather than emergency patching. The report must explain the local context.

4. Reporting Is Risk Transfer

When the assessment team sends a finding to an engineering, operations, product, vendor, or risk owner, it transfers a decision problem. The owner needs enough evidence to believe the issue, enough context to prioritize it, enough detail to fix it, and enough retest criteria to know when the risk has changed.

A weak report says, “Critical vulnerability found; fix ASAP.” A stronger report says which asset is affected, how it was validated within scope, what was not done, why the condition matters, who owns remediation, what exact change is expected, what compensating controls exist, and what evidence will close the issue.

5. Anatomy of a Strong Finding

6. Separate Fact, Inference, and Uncertainty

Many reports overstate confidence. The easiest way to improve quality is to label facts, inferences, and unknowns.

CategoryExampleHow to write it
FactOwner-provided export shows version 9.1.2 on the appliance.“Observed: owner export dated 2026-06-18 shows version 9.1.2.”
InferenceThat version appears affected by a vendor advisory.“Inference: this version appears to match vendor advisory conditions.”
UncertaintyExploit reproduction was prohibited.“Not tested: exploit execution was out of scope; validation used version and exposure evidence.”
DecisionRemediate urgently.“Recommendation: remediate urgently because the asset is internet-facing and the weakness is known exploited.”

This separation protects both the organization and the tester. It prevents unsupported claims while still allowing action when safer evidence is sufficient.

7. Executive Summary Without Hype

Executives do not need payload details. They need risk, business impact, trend, required decisions, and blockers. A good executive summary should answer:

  • What was assessed and under what authorization?
  • Which risks require urgent decisions?
  • Which owners must act?
  • Which fixes are blocked by architecture, vendors, change windows, or business constraints?
  • Which risks indicate systemic problems, not just one-off bugs?
  • What detection, response, or governance improvements are recommended?
Bad executive reporting: counting findings without context. Ten low-risk issues on retired assets are not automatically worse than one high-risk issue on an identity provider.

8. Technical Detail for Builders and Defenders

The technical section should be precise enough for remediation and retesting, but it should not include dangerous excess. For example, a report can show that a configuration permits a risky condition without including weaponized exploit steps or data-exfiltration instructions.

Useful technical detail includes affected route, component, version, configuration key, identity condition, security-control gap, log reference, timestamp, test account, expected behavior, observed behavior, and exact retest condition.

Unnecessary detail includes payloads that enable unauthorized reuse, credentials, personal data, customer records, persistent access methods, stealth steps, or instructions for attacking unrelated systems.

9. Severity Is Not Priority

Severity describes technical characteristics. Priority describes what the organization should do next. They overlap, but they are not identical.

InputQuestion it answersLimitation
CVSSHow severe are the technical characteristics?May not reflect local asset criticality or business context.
EPSSHow likely is exploitation in the wild over a short horizon?Applies to CVEs and is probabilistic, not proof of compromise.
CISA KEVIs there evidence this vulnerability is known exploited?Not every risky issue appears in KEV; custom app flaws may not have CVEs.
SSVC-style decisioningWhat response action fits stakeholder context?Requires local decisions and agreed criteria.
Business criticalityHow important is the affected process or data?Often missing or stale in inventories.
Compensating controlsWhat reduces likelihood or impact until the fix lands?Controls must be verified, not assumed.

10. Remediation Planning

A remediation recommendation must be specific enough to become work. It should name the owner, affected assets, expected change, dependencies, risk of change, rollback needs, and due date logic.

Finding typeBetter remediation language
Known vulnerable softwareUpgrade to fixed vendor version X or apply supported mitigation Y, then confirm the affected component is no longer exposed.
Authorization flawEnforce object-level authorization server-side for every access path and add negative tests for cross-object access.
Weak configurationDisable deprecated configuration after compatibility testing; document remaining exceptions with owners and expiration dates.
Exposed admin surfaceRestrict management access to approved networks, require MFA, verify logs, and confirm provider responsibility.
Detection gapEnable required telemetry, create detection logic, test alert routing, and add triage steps to the playbook.

11. Remediation Tracking and Ownership

Findings should not disappear into a PDF. Each material issue should become a trackable item in the organization’s normal work system. At minimum, it needs an owner, asset, severity, priority, evidence package, remediation action, due date, exception path, retest method, and closure evidence.

Ownership must match the actual ability to fix. Assigning a cloud IAM finding to the SOC may create visibility but not remediation. Assigning a vendor-managed exposure to an internal engineer may create frustration but not authority. The report should distinguish technical owner, business risk owner, vendor owner, and accountable approver when needed.

12. Retesting Is Narrow Validation

Retesting verifies whether the specific reported condition changed. It is not a second full penetration test unless explicitly scoped that way.

  • Retest scope: the exact assets and conditions being rechecked.
  • Retest method: the least-invasive method that confirms closure.
  • Pass condition: what evidence proves remediation is effective.
  • Fail condition: what evidence shows the issue remains or the fix is incomplete.
  • Regression concern: what related control should prevent recurrence.
  • Residual risk: what still needs risk-owner acceptance.
Retest rule: do not repeat unsafe validation just because it happened before. A retest can often use configuration export, version evidence, synthetic records, logs, or owner-supervised proof.

13. Exceptions, Risk Acceptance, and Residual Risk

Not every finding is fixed immediately. Some require vendor timelines, legacy-system migration, architecture redesign, contract changes, or business acceptance. That does not mean the finding is closed. It means the risk needs explicit treatment.

A defensible exception records the reason, affected assets, compensating controls, expiration date, approver, monitoring requirements, and trigger conditions that reopen the decision. “Accepted forever” is usually a governance failure, not mature risk management.

14. Purple-Team Handoff

Purple-team handoff asks: what should defenders learn from the assessment? Every validated finding should be reviewed for prevention, detection, response, and recovery implications.

flowchart LR
  A[Validated finding] --> B[Remediation ticket]
  A --> C[Detection opportunity]
  A --> D[Telemetry gap]
  A --> E[Playbook update]
  A --> F[Threat model update]
  B --> G[Retest]
  C --> H[Purple-team validation]
  D --> H
  E --> H
  F --> H
  H --> I[Control improvement evidence]

A handoff should include only supported mappings. If the assessment proved an exposed vulnerable service, it may justify detections for exposed-service inventory drift and exploit attempts. It does not prove a full adversary kill chain unless that evidence exists.

15. ATT&CK Mapping Without Overclaiming

ATT&CK is valuable because it provides a shared vocabulary for tactics and techniques based on observed adversary behavior. It is also easy to misuse. Mapping a finding to a technique should require evidence of behavior, not just imagination.

EvidenceReasonable mapping useOverclaim
Public remote-access appliance exposureInitial-access hypothesis and monitoring requirement.Claiming a specific threat group used it.
Repeated denied object-access attempts in logsApplication authorization abuse detection opportunity.Claiming data theft without evidence.
Weak backup admin exposureImpact and recovery-risk discussion.Claiming ransomware staging occurred.
Legacy protocol usage logsVisibility into downgrade or policy exceptions.Claiming active interception.

18. Quality Review Before Delivery

Before delivery, review the report for unsupported claims, sensitive data, unsafe detail, missing ownership, missing retest criteria, and inconsistent severity. A second reviewer should be able to trace every major claim to evidence.

  • Does every finding have a named asset and owner?
  • Does every material claim have evidence?
  • Are uncertainty and testing limits explicit?
  • Are screenshots and artifacts redacted?
  • Are remediation steps specific enough to become tickets?
  • Are retest criteria safe and precise?
  • Are ATT&CK or control mappings supported by evidence?
  • Are urgent items communicated before the final report if they need immediate action?

19. Case Study: The Useful Report

A fictional clinic receives a penetration-test report. The old version of the report lists twenty findings by scanner severity. Engineers argue about false positives, executives ask which systems affect patient operations, and the SOC cannot tell what to monitor.

The improved report groups findings by business process, identifies owners, separates validated issues from suspected issues, states what testing was prohibited, maps urgent items to known exploitation and exposure, converts fixes into tickets, and gives the SOC a short list of detection and telemetry improvements.

The difference is not cosmetic. The improved report reduces ambiguity, shortens remediation time, improves retest confidence, and gives defenders new ways to see similar weaknesses earlier.

Save this as reporting_handoff_builder.py.

import json
from dataclasses import dataclass
from pathlib import Path
from typing import Any

DATA_FILE = Path("assessment_findings.json")


@dataclass
class ReportArtifact:
    finding_id: str
    executive_line: str
    action_priority: str
    remediation_ticket: dict[str, Any]
    retest_case: dict[str, Any]
    purple_team_handoff: dict[str, Any]
    quality_warnings: list[str]


def load_data(path: Path = DATA_FILE) -> dict[str, Any]:
    return json.loads(path.read_text())


def action_priority(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> str:
    severity_order = {"critical": 4, "high": 3, "medium": 2, "low": 1}
    severity = severity_order.get(finding.get("severity", "low"), 1)
    target_days = finding.get("target_days", 60)
    status = finding.get("status", "needs_more_evidence")

    if status == "owner_coordination_required":
        return "coordinate_owner_before_testing"
    if severity >= 4 or target_days <= 3:
        return "urgent_remediation"
    if severity >= 3 or target_days <= 14:
        return "high_priority_remediation"
    if status == "needs_more_evidence":
        return "evidence_improvement_first"
    if severity == 2:
        return "scheduled_remediation"
    return "track_or_decommission"


def evidence_quality_warnings(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> list[str]:
    warnings: list[str] = []
    evidence = finding.get("evidence", [])
    if len(evidence) < 3:
        warnings.append("evidence package has fewer than three supporting artifacts")
    if finding.get("evidence_strength") == "weak":
        warnings.append("do not present weak evidence as validated impact")
    if "production customer data access" in finding.get("unsafe_actions_avoided", []):
        warnings.append("state explicitly that production customer data was not accessed")
    if finding.get("status") == "owner_coordination_required":
        warnings.append("third-party or owner coordination is required before direct validation")
    if not finding.get("retest_method"):
        warnings.append("finding lacks retest criteria")
    if not finding.get("remediation_owner"):
        warnings.append("finding lacks a named remediation owner")
    return warnings


def build_executive_line(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> str:
    return (
        f"{finding['severity'].title()} risk on {finding['asset']}: {finding['title']}. "
        f"Business impact: {finding['business_impact']}"
    )


def build_remediation_ticket(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> dict[str, Any]:
    return {
        "owner": finding["remediation_owner"],
        "asset": finding["asset"],
        "due_in_days": finding["target_days"],
        "required_action": finding["remediation"],
        "risk_owner_question": finding["residual_risk_question"],
        "closure_requires_retest": True,
    }


def build_retest_case(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> dict[str, Any]:
    return {
        "asset": finding["asset"],
        "allowed_method": finding["retest_method"],
        "must_not_repeat": finding.get("unsafe_actions_avoided", []),
        "pass_condition": "remediation evidence matches the stated retest method and no prohibited action is needed",
        "fail_condition": "affected condition remains, evidence is missing, or retest would require unauthorized activity",
    }


def build_purple_team_handoff(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> dict[str, Any]:
    return {
        "soc_questions": [
            "Would existing telemetry show this condition or attempted abuse?",
            "Which log source, rule, dashboard, or playbook should change?",
            "What signal would prove the remediation reduced detection or response risk?",
        ],
        "detection_opportunities": finding.get("detection_handoff", []),
        "attack_or_behavior_mapping": finding.get("attack_mapping", []),
        "notes": "Map only what evidence supports; do not over-map unsupported attacker behavior.",
    }


def build_artifact(finding: dict[str, Any]) -> ReportArtifact:
    return ReportArtifact(
        finding_id=finding["id"],
        executive_line=build_executive_line(finding),
        action_priority=action_priority(finding),
        remediation_ticket=build_remediation_ticket(finding),
        retest_case=build_retest_case(finding),
        purple_team_handoff=build_purple_team_handoff(finding),
        quality_warnings=evidence_quality_warnings(finding),
    )


def main() -> None:
    data = load_data()
    artifacts = [build_artifact(f).__dict__ for f in data["findings"]]
    print(json.dumps({"engagement": data["engagement"], "artifacts": artifacts}, indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

from reporting_handoff_builder import action_priority, build_artifact, load_data


def by_id(finding_id):
    return next(item for item in load_data()["findings"] if item["id"] == finding_id)


def test_critical_item_is_urgent():
    artifact = build_artifact(by_id("RPT-1305-001"))
    assert artifact.action_priority == "urgent_remediation"
    assert "public exploit execution" in artifact.retest_case["must_not_repeat"]


def test_third_party_item_requires_coordination():
    artifact = build_artifact(by_id("RPT-1305-003"))
    assert artifact.action_priority == "coordinate_owner_before_testing"
    assert any("coordination" in warning for warning in artifact.quality_warnings)


def test_low_confidence_item_is_not_overstated():
    artifact = build_artifact(by_id("RPT-1305-005"))
    assert artifact.action_priority == "evidence_improvement_first"
    assert any("weak evidence" in warning for warning in artifact.quality_warnings)


def test_every_artifact_has_owner_and_retest():
    for finding in load_data()["findings"]:
        artifact = build_artifact(finding)
        assert artifact.remediation_ticket["owner"]
        assert artifact.retest_case["allowed_method"]
        assert artifact.purple_team_handoff["detection_opportunities"]

The output is not a perfect report. It is a decision aid. Your job is to review whether each artifact has enough evidence, a responsible owner, a safe retest method, and a useful defensive handoff.

21. Self-Assessment Questions

  1. Why is severity different from remediation priority?
  2. What belongs in a finding’s evidence package?
  3. Why should a report state what was not tested?
  4. What makes a retest pass condition precise?
  5. When should a finding become a risk exception rather than a closed issue?
  6. Why can a scanner result be useful but insufficient?
  7. What makes a purple-team handoff actionable?
  8. When is ATT&CK mapping unsupported?
  9. Why should production data be avoided in report evidence?
  10. How can a report improve SOC monitoring and not only engineering fixes?
  11. What is the difference between remediation owner and risk owner?
  12. Why are due dates without compensating controls weak?
  13. What should happen if retesting requires an unauthorized method?
  14. How can residual risk be documented without hiding it?
  15. What is one sign that a report overclaims impact?
