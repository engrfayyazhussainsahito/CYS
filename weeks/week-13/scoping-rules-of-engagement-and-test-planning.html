Week 13 · Lesson 13.2 · Offensive Security Methodology

Scoping, Rules of Engagement, and Test Planning

A security assessment is only professional when the boundaries are explicit. Scope and rules of engagement protect systems, people, evidence, privacy, testers, defenders, and the business decision the test is supposed to support.

Estimated reading time95 minutes
Difficulty levelIntermediate
Required backgroundLesson 13.1 and Weeks 0–12
Practice time90–120 minutes

1. Introduction

Weak scoping is one of the easiest ways for an authorized test to become unsafe, useless, or unauthorized. A vague request such as “pentest our app” does not identify the owner, the permitted assets, the testing window, the prohibited actions, the data rules, or the emergency stop process.

This lesson treats scope and rules of engagement as operational security controls. They are not paperwork added after the real work. They are the controls that make the work lawful, repeatable, defensible, and useful.

The practical outcome is a test plan that says exactly what will be tested, why it will be tested, how far the tester may go, what must not be touched, how evidence will be handled, and who can pause or stop the work.

2. Learning Outcomes

  • Explain why authorization, scope, and rules of engagement are required before any offensive-security activity.
  • Write a precise scope statement with in-scope assets, out-of-scope assets, owner approval, business objectives, and deliverables.
  • Define rules of engagement for methods, windows, prohibited actions, credentials, data handling, communication, and stop conditions.
  • Identify common scoping traps involving cloud, SaaS, third parties, shared services, production systems, and sensitive data.
  • Convert a vague assessment request into a controlled test plan.
  • Use a safe local Python lab to review fictional plans for missing controls and conflicts.

3. Research Anchors Used for This Lesson

This lesson is grounded in formal assessment and testing references:

  • NIST SP 800-115 supports planning and conducting technical information security tests and examinations, analyzing findings, and developing mitigation strategies.
  • NIST SP 800-53A Rev. 5 provides assessment procedures that organizations can tailor to support risk management and stated risk tolerance.
  • NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 provides control context for authorization, monitoring, access control, audit, incident response, configuration management, and risk management.
  • OWASP Web Security Testing Guide provides a structured web and web-service testing reference.
  • OWASP's testing-methodology references summarize common testing phases, including pre-engagement interaction, intelligence gathering, vulnerability analysis, reporting, and NIST SP 800-115's planning/execution/post-testing framing.
  • MITRE ATT&CK provides a common vocabulary for adversary behavior when scoping emulation, purple-team, and detection-validation objectives.
  • CISA Cyber Hygiene Services illustrate the idea that even scanning internet-accessible assets must be treated as an authorized, bounded defensive service.
Research limitation: Methodology references do not replace written authorization, contract terms, cloud-provider rules, local law, privacy obligations, or organization-specific risk decisions.

4. Safety and Authorization Boundary

Authorized environments only: Do not scan, enumerate, test, exploit, bypass controls, collect data, phish, stress systems, or interact with accounts unless the asset owner has explicitly authorized the activity in writing. This lesson uses only fictional examples and local validation scripts.

A test plan must be specific enough that a reasonable reviewer can answer: who approved this, which assets are covered, which actions are permitted, which actions are prohibited, when testing may happen, what evidence may be collected, and how to stop the activity if risk increases.

This lesson does not provide legal advice. Real engagements require review by the correct business, legal, privacy, compliance, risk, security, engineering, and service-owner stakeholders.

5. First Principle: Scope Is a Security Control

Scope is not just a list of URLs or IP ranges. It is the permission boundary for the assessment. It limits blast radius, protects third parties, prevents privacy violations, gives defenders context, and makes evidence easier to interpret.

Weak assumptionFailure modeBetter control
“We own the company, so everything is in scope.”Shared cloud accounts, subsidiaries, vendors, customer tenants, and personal accounts may have different ownership and authorization.Name exact legal entities, owners, systems, accounts, domains, environments, and exclusions.
“The tool only scans, so it is harmless.”Scanning can trigger alarms, rate limits, account lockouts, unstable services, or provider abuse systems.Define methods, rate limits, windows, contacts, and stop conditions.
“Production is fine because we need real results.”Testing can disrupt users, expose data, or collide with deployments.Prefer staging/lab validation unless production testing is explicitly justified, approved, and controlled.
“The vendor hosts it, but it is our app.”Vendor terms may forbid specific tests or require prior notice.Confirm third-party approval, provider rules, and escalation paths.

6. Anatomy of a Scope Statement

A useful scope statement should be concrete enough to guide a tester and clear enough for a non-technical owner to approve.

ElementQuestions it must answerExample
Owner and authorityWho owns the asset and who authorizes the test?Application Security Steering Group authorizes testing of the training portal.
Business objectiveWhat decision will the test support?Validate remediation before public release.
In-scope assetsWhich domains, APIs, applications, accounts, repositories, networks, cloud accounts, or roles are included?training-portal.example.test and api.training-portal.example.test.
Out-of-scope assetsWhich connected systems, vendors, tenants, environments, or data stores are excluded?Payment provider, production email, customer export service.
MethodsWhich test activities are permitted, limited, or prohibited?Authenticated web testing with test accounts; no denial-of-service or credential harvesting.
TimingWhen may testing start and stop? Which timezone? Which change freezes?July 6–7, 09:00–18:00 Asia/Karachi.
EvidenceWhat may be captured, where will it be stored, and how long will it be retained?Redacted screenshots, request IDs, timestamps, synthetic data, encrypted repository.
DeliverablesWhat output will the owner receive?Validated findings, impact explanation, remediation, retest criteria, detection opportunities.

7. Rules of Engagement

Rules of engagement turn the scope statement into operating instructions. They should be written before testing begins and should be visible to testers, defenders, service owners, incident responders, and the engagement sponsor.

RoE areaWhat to defineWhy it matters
Allowed methodsPermitted techniques, accounts, tools, test types, and validation depth.Prevents uncontrolled escalation and ambiguous expectations.
Prohibited actionsDenial-of-service, destructive testing, persistence, credential theft, sensitive-data extraction, phishing, physical access, or social engineering unless explicitly approved.Reduces avoidable harm and legal/privacy exposure.
Rate and load limitsRequest rates, concurrency limits, scanning windows, and production throttles.Protects service availability and reduces false incidents.
Credential rulesApproved test accounts, roles, MFA expectations, password handling, and account cleanup.Prevents misuse of real user credentials and unclear authorization.
Evidence rulesRedaction, storage, retention, access, ticket IDs, chain of custody when needed.Improves report quality and protects sensitive data.
CommunicationsKickoff, daily status, emergency bridge, escalation path, deconfliction with SOC.Keeps owners and defenders aligned.
Stop conditionsWho can stop the test, what triggers a pause, and how testers acknowledge the stop.Provides a safety brake when risk changes.

8. Test-Planning Workflow

A professional plan moves from business question to bounded evidence collection. The exact method changes by assessment type, but the planning logic is stable.

flowchart TD
  A[Business objective] --> B[Written authorization]
  B --> C[Scope and exclusions]
  C --> D[Rules of engagement]
  D --> E[Evidence and data-handling plan]
  E --> F[Communication and stop procedure]
  F --> G[Test cases and schedule]
  G --> H[Readiness review]
  H --> I[Authorized testing]
  I --> J[Findings, remediation, and retest criteria]

Do not begin technical testing until the readiness review resolves missing authorization, unclear assets, vague methods, absent emergency contacts, and data-handling gaps.

9. From Objective to Test Cases

The test plan should not be a tool list. It should connect each test case to a business objective, control question, evidence requirement, and safety limit.

ObjectiveTest case framingEvidence targetSafety limit
Validate access-control remediationCan approved test role A access object owned by role B?Redacted request/response metadata, role names, object IDs using synthetic records.No real customer records; stop if unexpected data appears.
Confirm cloud logging coverageAre administrative actions visible in the approved logging path?Event IDs, timestamps, source account, log destination, detection mapping.Read-only review; no privilege changes unless separately approved.
Review exposure of staging serviceWhich approved staging endpoints are reachable from the internet?Asset list, owner mapping, service banners only where permitted.No third-party scanning; no stress testing.
Check remediation effectivenessDoes the previously reported weakness still reproduce under approved conditions?Before/after observation, exact test account, retest conclusion.No expansion beyond the original finding without approval.

10. Asset and Environment Boundaries

Scope must distinguish environment, owner, tenant, and provider boundary. A single product may involve a public domain, CDN, WAF, application servers, APIs, cloud accounts, identity providers, SaaS integrations, CI/CD systems, logging systems, and third-party services. Ownership is not always obvious from the URL.

For each asset, record the environment, owner, asset identifier, approval source, dependency status, data sensitivity, allowed methods, and out-of-scope neighbors. Do not infer that adjacent domains, IP ranges, repositories, tenants, or accounts are included merely because they look related.

11. Identity, Credential, and Role Scope

Many meaningful findings involve identity and authorization. That makes credential boundaries critical. A test plan should specify approved test accounts, roles, permissions, MFA behavior, password handling, service accounts, tokens, session handling, account cleanup, and whether account lockout testing is permitted.

Practical rule: Use approved test accounts and synthetic data whenever possible. Real employee credentials, customer accounts, production tokens, and privileged service accounts require explicit written approval and stronger handling rules.

12. Data Handling and Evidence Rules

Evidence should prove the claim without collecting unnecessary sensitive data. The plan should define approved storage, retention, redaction, access control, naming conventions, timestamps, screenshots, request logs, ticket IDs, and escalation steps if sensitive data appears unexpectedly.

Evidence questionGood plan answer
Can screenshots include personal data?No, unless explicitly approved; prefer redacted synthetic records.
Where is evidence stored?Encrypted project repository with named access list.
How long is evidence retained?Defined retention period tied to report, remediation, and legal requirements.
What if sensitive data is exposed?Stop or pause, preserve minimal metadata, notify authorized contacts, follow privacy/legal process.
What makes evidence reproducible?Timestamp, tester, role, asset, test case ID, input summary, observed result, limitation note.

13. Communication and Deconfliction

Communication prevents safe testing from becoming an avoidable incident. The plan should specify kickoff participants, daily status cadence, SOC awareness, incident-response deconfliction, emergency contacts, after-hours rules, approved channels, and how testers acknowledge stop instructions.

Covert exercises require stricter governance, not less governance. If defenders are not fully informed, someone else must own deconfliction, safety monitoring, legal approval, and emergency authority.

14. Stop Conditions

Stop conditions are not signs of failure. They are part of professional risk control. A test should pause when conditions change in a way the rules did not authorize.

Stop triggerReasonImmediate action
Unexpected sensitive data appearsPrivacy and legal exposure.Stop, minimize capture, notify authorized contacts.
Service instability or user impactAvailability risk.Pause activity, preserve logs, coordinate with service owner.
Tester reaches an out-of-scope systemAuthorization boundary risk.Do not continue; document boundary contact and escalate.
SOC opens an incidentOperational disruption risk.Deconflict through approved contact or stop procedure.
New high-risk finding appears outside planScope expansion risk.Request explicit approval before additional validation.

15. Production, Cloud, SaaS, and Third-Party Traps

Production and cloud testing require extra care because the blast radius is often larger than it appears. A cloud account may contain shared networking, centralized logging, IAM trust relationships, backup services, customer data, and provider-managed services. SaaS systems may contain tenant boundaries and contractual restrictions. Third-party integrations may require separate notice or approval.

  • Confirm provider terms and customer responsibilities before testing hosted systems.
  • Confirm whether infrastructure belongs to your organization, a vendor, a customer, or a shared provider.
  • Prefer read-only review for configuration and logging assessments unless change testing is explicitly approved.
  • Define rollback and emergency contacts before any change-producing test.
  • Exclude customer data and unrelated tenants unless explicit authorization exists.

16. Planning Matrix

A planning matrix helps reviewers see whether the plan is complete before activity begins.

Plan fieldMinimum acceptable detailRed flag
ObjectiveSpecific risk, release, control, or remediation question.“Find anything interesting.”
AssetsNamed assets, owners, environments, identifiers, and exclusions.“All company systems.”
MethodsAllowed and prohibited methods with depth limits.“Use any tools.”
WindowStart/end time, timezone, blackout dates, change freezes.“Whenever.”
CredentialsApproved test accounts and roles.“Use whatever credentials you can get.”
EvidenceRedaction, storage, retention, access control, naming scheme.No data-handling rule.
ContactsOwner, SOC, incident response, emergency stop authority.No one available during testing.
DeliverablesFindings, evidence, impact, remediation, retest criteria.Only a scanner export.

17. Weak vs. Strong Test Requests

Weak requestProblemStronger version
“Pentest our website.”No asset list, owner, methods, or window.“Test these two staging domains for access-control and session findings from July 6–7 using approved test accounts.”
“Scan anything with our company name.”May include third parties, abandoned assets, or unrelated domains.“Review the attached approved external asset inventory; do not test domains outside the inventory.”
“Try to get admin.”Encourages uncontrolled escalation.“Validate whether role separation prevents approved test users from accessing admin-only functions.”
“Use real customer data to prove impact.”Privacy and legal risk.“Use synthetic records; stop and escalate if real customer data appears.”
“Do it quietly.”Can cause confusion with incident response.“Define who is informed, who is blind, and who owns deconfliction.”

18. Fictional Scenario: Release Retest Plan

A fictional product team asks for a release retest of a training portal. The initial request is: “Can security check the portal before launch?” That is not enough. A usable plan adds:

  • Objective: validate remediation of prior access-control and session-management findings.
  • In scope: training portal, training API, approved staging tenant, approved test accounts.
  • Out of scope: payment provider, production mailboxes, customer export service, third-party analytics tenant.
  • Allowed: authenticated web testing, configuration review, evidence review, and role-based access checks using synthetic data.
  • Prohibited: denial-of-service, credential harvesting, persistence, destructive testing, real customer data access, and social engineering.
  • Evidence: redacted screenshots, request IDs, timestamps, affected test roles, synthetic object IDs, and reproducible retest steps.
  • Stop: any sensitive-data exposure, service degradation, out-of-scope redirect, or incident-response escalation pauses testing.

The improved request is still not an exploit procedure. It is a controlled plan that lets useful testing happen safely.

Step 2: Create the validator

Save this as roe_plan_validator.py.

import json
from dataclasses import dataclass
from datetime import datetime
from typing import Any

BLOCKING_FLAGS = {
    "no written authorization",
    "unbounded internet scope",
    "undefined owner",
    "third-party target without permission",
}

HIGH_RISK_FLAGS = {
    "change freeze conflict",
    "production safety concern",
    "sensitive data exposure risk",
}

REQUIRED_DELIVERABLES = {
    "validated findings",
    "risk explanation",
    "retest criteria",
}

REQUIRED_PROHIBITED_METHODS = {
    "denial-of-service",
    "credential harvesting",
    "destructive testing",
}

@dataclass
class PlanDecision:
    plan_id: str
    title: str
    status: str
    completeness_score: int
    missing_items: list[str]
    conflicts: list[str]
    required_next_actions: list[str]


def load_plans(path: str = "roe_test_plans.json") -> list[dict[str, Any]]:
    with open(path, "r", encoding="utf-8") as handle:
        return json.load(handle)


def has_text(value: Any) -> bool:
    return isinstance(value, str) and bool(value.strip())


def has_list(value: Any) -> bool:
    return isinstance(value, list) and len(value) > 0


def valid_window(window: dict[str, Any]) -> bool:
    try:
        start = datetime.fromisoformat(window.get("start", ""))
        end = datetime.fromisoformat(window.get("end", ""))
    except ValueError:
        return False
    return start < end


def evaluate_plan(plan: dict[str, Any]) -> PlanDecision:
    missing: list[str] = []
    conflicts: list[str] = []

    if plan.get("written_authorization") is not True:
        missing.append("written_authorization")
    if not has_text(plan.get("asset_owner")):
        missing.append("asset_owner")
    if not has_text(plan.get("business_objective")):
        missing.append("business_objective")
    if not has_list(plan.get("in_scope_assets")):
        missing.append("in_scope_assets")
    if not has_list(plan.get("out_of_scope_assets")):
        missing.append("out_of_scope_assets")
    if not has_list(plan.get("allowed_methods")):
        missing.append("allowed_methods")
    if not has_list(plan.get("prohibited_methods")):
        missing.append("prohibited_methods")
    if not valid_window(plan.get("test_window", {})):
        missing.append("valid_test_window")
    if not has_list(plan.get("emergency_contacts")):
        missing.append("emergency_contacts")

    data = plan.get("data_handling", {})
    if not data.get("synthetic_data_only"):
        missing.append("synthetic_or_minimized_data_rule")
    if not data.get("redaction_required"):
        missing.append("redaction_required")
    if not data.get("approved_storage"):
        missing.append("approved_storage")

    comms = plan.get("communications", {})
    if not comms.get("kickoff_required"):
        missing.append("kickoff_required")
    if not comms.get("stop_signal"):
        missing.append("stop_signal")

    deliverables = set(plan.get("deliverables", []))
    missing_deliverables = sorted(REQUIRED_DELIVERABLES - deliverables)
    for item in missing_deliverables:
        missing.append(f"deliverable:{item}")

    prohibited = set(plan.get("prohibited_methods", []))
    missing_prohibitions = sorted(REQUIRED_PROHIBITED_METHODS - prohibited)
    for item in missing_prohibitions:
        missing.append(f"prohibited_method:{item}")

    flags = set(plan.get("red_flags", []))
    blocking = sorted(flags & BLOCKING_FLAGS)
    high_risk = sorted(flags & HIGH_RISK_FLAGS)
    conflicts.extend(blocking)
    conflicts.extend(high_risk)

    if "any tools" in plan.get("allowed_methods", []):
        conflicts.append("allowed methods are too vague")

    if set(plan.get("in_scope_assets", [])) & set(plan.get("out_of_scope_assets", [])):
        conflicts.append("asset appears in both in-scope and out-of-scope lists")

    max_checks = 20
    completeness_score = max(0, int(((max_checks - min(len(missing), max_checks)) / max_checks) * 100))

    if blocking or plan.get("written_authorization") is not True:
        status = "blocked"
    elif missing or high_risk or conflicts:
        status = "needs_revision_before_testing"
    else:
        status = "ready_for_authorized_testing"

    actions = []
    for item in missing:
        actions.append(f"define or correct: {item}")
    for item in conflicts:
        actions.append(f"resolve conflict or risk: {item}")
    if status == "ready_for_authorized_testing":
        actions.append("schedule kickoff, publish rules of engagement, and confirm stop procedure before testing begins")

    return PlanDecision(
        plan_id=plan["plan_id"],
        title=plan["title"],
        status=status,
        completeness_score=completeness_score,
        missing_items=missing,
        conflicts=conflicts,
        required_next_actions=actions,
    )


def main() -> None:
    decisions = [evaluate_plan(plan).__dict__ for plan in load_plans()]
    print(json.dumps(decisions, indent=2))


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

Step 4: Optional tests

Save this as test_roe_plan_validator.py if you want to check the logic with pytest.

from roe_plan_validator import evaluate_plan, load_plans


def by_id(plan_id):
    return next(plan for plan in load_plans() if plan["plan_id"] == plan_id)


def test_training_portal_plan_is_ready():
    decision = evaluate_plan(by_id("ROE-1302-001"))
    assert decision.status == "ready_for_authorized_testing"
    assert decision.completeness_score == 100


def test_unbounded_internet_plan_is_blocked():
    decision = evaluate_plan(by_id("ROE-1302-002"))
    assert decision.status == "blocked"
    assert "unbounded internet scope" in decision.conflicts
    assert "written_authorization" in decision.missing_items


def test_change_freeze_conflict_requires_revision():
    decision = evaluate_plan(by_id("ROE-1302-003"))
    assert decision.status == "needs_revision_before_testing"
    assert "change freeze conflict" in decision.conflicts


def test_cloud_review_keeps_production_out_of_scope():
    plan = by_id("ROE-1302-004")
    decision = evaluate_plan(plan)
    assert decision.status == "ready_for_authorized_testing"
    assert "production-cloud-account" in plan["out_of_scope_assets"]
Why this lab matters: In real work, blocking a weak assessment plan is often more valuable than running a tool. It prevents harm and forces the organization to define the risk question clearly.

20. Self-Assessment

  1. Why is scope a security control rather than an administrative detail?
  2. What is the difference between in-scope assets and allowed methods?
  3. Why should out-of-scope assets be named explicitly?
  4. What stop conditions would you require for production web testing?
  5. Why are real employee credentials risky in an assessment?
  6. What evidence proves an access-control weakness without exposing customer data?
  7. How should the plan change if a third-party SaaS provider is involved?
  8. What communication rules prevent SOC confusion during testing?
  9. Why is “use any tools” an unacceptable rule of engagement?
  10. What deliverables make a test useful to engineers and defenders?
