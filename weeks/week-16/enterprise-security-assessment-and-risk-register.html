Week 16 · Lesson 16.2

Enterprise Security Assessment and Risk Register

The capstone assessment is not a hunt for impressive findings. It is a disciplined conversion of scoped evidence into decision-ready risk records: assets, business impact, threats, weaknesses, control evidence, likelihood, impact, uncertainty, ownership, treatment, and verification criteria.

Estimated reading time95–130 minutes
Difficulty levelMS capstone integration
Required backgroundLessons 14.1–14.5 and Lesson 16.1 recommended
Lab modeSafe local risk-register generation using fictional JSON
Boundary: This lesson uses only fictional, course-provided records. It does not authorize live scanning, web requests, vulnerability testing, credential use, vendor contact, real asset discovery, legal conclusions, or assessments of real organizations.

1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Turn a capstone charter into an assessment plan with clear assessment objects and evidence sources.
  • Map assets, data, dependencies, owners, and business impact before writing risk statements.
  • Evaluate control evidence for design, implementation, operating effectiveness, freshness, and relevance.
  • Write risk statements that connect asset, threat/event, weakness, impact path, owner, evidence, uncertainty, and treatment.
  • Prioritize risk using likelihood, impact, confidence, business criticality, control status, and escalation flags.
  • Distinguish findings, risks, control gaps, assumptions, and recommendations.
  • Generate a safe local risk register from fictional data and identify evidence-quality warnings.

2. Research Basis

This lesson is grounded in current risk-management and assessment references. NIST SP 800-30 Rev. 1 provides guidance for conducting risk assessments and emphasizes that risk assessment supports senior leadership decisions about courses of action. NIST SP 800-37 Rev. 2 defines the RMF lifecycle, including categorization, control selection, implementation, assessment, authorization, and continuous monitoring. NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 supplies a flexible catalog of security and privacy controls, while NIST SP 800-53A Rev. 5 provides assessment methodology and customizable procedures. NIST IR 8286 Rev. 1 connects cybersecurity risk registers to enterprise risk management, and NIST SP 800-55 Volumes 1 and 2 support measurement selection and measurement-program design. CISA Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals provide practical baseline outcomes for prioritizing security improvements.

Research implication: A risk register should not be a list of vulnerabilities. It should be an evidence-backed management artifact that lets accountable people decide whether to mitigate, transfer, avoid, accept, monitor, or investigate a risk.

3. Assessment Model

In the capstone, an enterprise security assessment has four layers:

  1. Context: mission, services, stakeholders, data, dependencies, constraints, and risk appetite.
  2. Evidence: inventories, diagrams, policies, control summaries, alert samples, synthetic incident notes, and governance records.
  3. Analysis: asset criticality, control effectiveness, exposure, likelihood, impact, assumptions, and uncertainty.
  4. Decision support: risk register, remediation roadmap, executive rollup, and verification plan.

The common failure is starting at layer three. Good assessment starts with context. A missing asset owner or outdated service map can invalidate a technically correct finding because the recommendation may be impossible to fund, schedule, or verify.

4. Assessment Workflow

The workflow below keeps assessment work traceable from charter to decision.

flowchart TD
  A[Capstone charter] --> B[Asset, data, dependency map]
  B --> C[Control evidence review]
  C --> D[Observations and gaps]
  D --> E[Risk statements]
  E --> F[Likelihood, impact, confidence]
  F --> G[Risk register]
  G --> H[Remediation roadmap]
  H --> I[Executive decision brief]
  G --> J[Evidence notebook]
  J --> K[Reviewer validation]

Every arrow should have evidence. If you cannot show how a recommendation came from a scoped source, it should not appear in the final capstone.

5. Required Capstone Inputs

Lesson 16.2 assumes the following inputs are fictional and provided by the course:

  • Capstone charter and scope exclusions from Lesson 16.1.
  • Asset and service inventory with owners and criticality ratings.
  • Data classification notes and dependency summaries.
  • Policy excerpts, control-evidence summaries, and exception records.
  • Synthetic alert, incident, backup, identity, and vendor notes.
  • Business impact notes and executive constraints.

Do not fill gaps by collecting real-world evidence. Instead, write the gap as an uncertainty or required stakeholder confirmation.

6. Asset, Data, and Dependency Mapping

A risk register is weak when it says “the system is vulnerable” without naming the system, owner, data, business process, and dependency path. The capstone asset map should include:

FieldWhy it mattersBad shortcut
Asset or service nameDefines what is affected“Cloud app”
OwnerIdentifies who can authorize change“IT”
Business functionConnects technical conditions to mission impact“Important”
Data typesTriggers privacy, legal, and protection requirements“User data”
DependenciesShows failure propagation and inherited riskIgnoring SaaS, identity, vendors, backups
CriticalitySupports prioritization and recovery planningAll systems marked high

7. Control Evidence Review

A control can fail in several ways. A policy may exist but not be implemented. A configuration may be implemented but not monitored. A process may work for one team but not another. Evidence review should therefore separate design effectiveness from operating effectiveness.

Design

Does the control address the risk scenario in principle? Example: a privileged MFA policy exists and covers administrative accounts.

Implementation

Is the control actually deployed where expected? Example: identity-provider settings show MFA enabled for most privileged roles.

Operation

Is the control working over time? Example: exception reviews occur monthly and expired exceptions are removed.

Evidence quality

Is the evidence current, relevant, complete, and attributable to the scoped system?

Do not treat “control exists” as “risk is managed.” Control evidence must support the specific claim being made.

8. Observations Are Not Yet Risks

An observation is something noticed in the evidence. A risk is a potential event that could affect objectives. A finding is a documented condition supported by evidence. These are related but not identical.

ArtifactExampleWhat is missing?
ObservationTwo privileged accounts have MFA exceptions.Impact path, likelihood, owner, treatment.
FindingPrivileged MFA exception evidence shows two accounts lack expiry dates.Risk decision and response plan.
RiskUnauthorized privileged access could bypass expected identity controls and affect regulated systems.Owner decision, treatment, verification criteria.

9. Risk Statement Anatomy

A useful risk statement is specific enough for action. Use this pattern:

Because [weakness/control gap] affects [asset/service/data], [threat/event] could cause [business/security/privacy impact]. Evidence includes [source references]. Current uncertainty is [unknowns]. The accountable owner is [role]. Recommended treatment is [mitigate/transfer/avoid/accept/monitor/investigate].

Weak statement: “MFA is risky.” Strong statement: “Because privileged MFA exceptions lack current expiry dates for the identity provider, unauthorized privileged access could bypass expected access controls for systems supporting the Patient Portal and Telehealth Scheduling API. Evidence includes the MFA policy summary and exception list. Confirm whether compensating monitoring exists. Owner: Security Operations Manager. Treatment: mitigate by expiring or approving exceptions with compensating controls.”

10. Scoring Without False Precision

Risk scoring is a communication aid, not a mathematical truth. A simple 1–5 likelihood and 1–5 impact model is acceptable for the capstone if every score has a rationale. The score should not hide uncertainty.

ScoreLikelihood cueImpact cue
1Rare or highly constrainedMinor operational inconvenience
2Unlikely but plausibleLimited team-level disruption
3Plausible under normal conditionsService degradation or moderate data exposure
4Likely given current gaps or exposureMajor service, privacy, legal, or security impact
5Expected or already observed patternCritical mission, safety, regulatory, or enterprise impact

Add confidence separately. A high score with low confidence may require evidence improvement before a final treatment decision.

11. Inherent, Current, Residual, and Target Risk

Risk language becomes confusing when teams mix time states. Use these definitions consistently:

  • Inherent risk: risk before considering current controls.
  • Current risk: risk considering controls and evidence currently available.
  • Residual risk: risk expected after selected treatment is completed.
  • Target risk: desired risk level aligned with risk appetite or tolerance.

The capstone should focus on current and residual risk because those support decisions. Inherent risk is useful for context, but it is often overused when control evidence is incomplete.

12. Mapping Controls to Risk

Map controls to risk only when the control actually changes the likelihood, impact, detection, containment, or recovery path for the scenario. A firewall rule does not automatically reduce identity risk. A backup does not reduce initial compromise likelihood, but it can reduce recovery impact.

Reviewer test: For every mapped control, ask: “What part of the risk statement does this control change?” If the answer is unclear, the mapping is probably decorative.

13. Required Risk Register Fields

Each capstone risk record should include at minimum:

  • Risk ID and short title.
  • Asset/service and asset owner.
  • Threat/event or failure scenario.
  • Weakness, control gap, or evidence gap.
  • Business/security/privacy impact path.
  • Likelihood, impact, score, confidence, and rationale.
  • Evidence references and freshness notes.
  • Existing controls and control-effectiveness summary.
  • Treatment decision, owner, due date or dependency, and verification method.
  • Residual risk and uncertainty statement.

Do not submit a risk register with ownerless risks. An ownerless risk is not decision-ready.

14. Prioritization Beyond the Heat Map

A heat map can help, but it is not enough. Prioritize by combining score with operational realities:

  1. Does the risk affect a critical service or regulated/sensitive data?
  2. Is the control gap active, stale, or only suspected?
  3. Does the issue affect identity, recovery, monitoring, or third-party access?
  4. Can the organization remediate quickly, or is there a dependency?
  5. Does the finding require privacy/legal/compliance escalation?
  6. Would delaying action create recovery, evidence, or notification problems?

A medium-scored backup validation gap may deserve faster action than a higher-scored cosmetic issue because recovery failure changes incident outcomes.

15. Treatment Decisions

Risk treatment should be explicit:

Mitigate

Change controls, processes, architecture, or ownership to reduce likelihood or impact.

Transfer

Shift part of the risk through contract, insurance, or service-provider responsibility while retaining oversight.

Avoid

Stop the activity or remove the exposure when the risk is not worth the benefit.

Accept

Document a conscious business decision by an authorized risk owner.

Monitor

Continue observation when risk is tolerable but needs trend visibility.

Investigate

Improve evidence before making a final treatment decision.

16. Evidence Notebook

Your evidence notebook should separate:

  • Facts: Directly supported by provided records.
  • Inferences: Reasonable conclusions from facts.
  • Assumptions: Working beliefs that need confirmation.
  • Unknowns: Missing information that may affect scoring or treatment.
  • Reviewer notes: Why a score or decision changed during analysis.

This distinction is a major grading criterion. It prevents confidence theater and makes the final report more professional.

17. Executive Risk Rollup

Executives rarely need every technical detail. They need decisions, trade-offs, dependencies, and residual risk. A useful rollup groups risks into themes:

  • Identity and privileged access risk.
  • Logging and investigation-readiness risk.
  • Recovery and continuity risk.
  • Vendor and third-party access risk.
  • Privacy/legal escalation risk.

For each theme, state the decision needed: funding, policy exception approval, remediation priority, acceptance, escalation, or additional evidence collection.

18. Lesson 16.2 Deliverables

By the end of this lesson, your capstone package should contain:

  • Enterprise assessment summary.
  • Asset, data, dependency, and owner map.
  • Control-evidence review table.

  • Prioritized remediation roadmap.

Step 2 — Create risk_register_builder.py

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
Safe local lab for Lesson 16.2.
Builds a fictional enterprise assessment summary and risk register from provided JSON.
It performs no scanning, web requests, authentication, exploitation, cloud access, or real assessment.
"""
import json
from pathlib import Path
from datetime import date

RISK_BANDS = [
    (20, "critical"),
    (12, "high"),
    (6, "medium"),
    (1, "low"),
]
REQUIRED_SCOPE_WARNINGS = ["fictional", "no live scanning", "no live", "course-provided"]
REQUIRED_RISK_FIELDS = ["asset", "event", "weakness", "impact", "owner", "evidence", "treatment", "uncertainty"]


def load_package(path="capstone_assessment_package.json"):
    return json.loads(Path(path).read_text(encoding="utf-8"))


def risk_band(score):
    for threshold, band in RISK_BANDS:
        if score >= threshold:
            return band
    return "low"


def treatment_for(score, confidence, privacy_or_legal_flag):
    if privacy_or_legal_flag:
        return "escalate to privacy/legal owner and define remediation owner"
    if score >= 20:
        return "mitigate urgently and request executive decision on resources"
    if score >= 12:
        return "mitigate with named owner and target date"
    if confidence == "low":
        return "investigate; improve evidence before final decision"
    return "monitor or accept only with documented owner approval"


def check_scope(data):
    scope = data.get("scope_boundary", "").lower()
    warnings = []
    if "fictional" not in scope or "course-provided" not in scope:
        warnings.append("Scope boundary should clearly limit work to fictional course-provided records.")
    if "no live scanning" not in scope and "no live" not in scope:
        warnings.append("Scope boundary should explicitly prohibit live testing or scanning.")
    return warnings


def index_assets(data):
    return {item["id"]: item for item in data.get("assets", [])}


def index_controls(data):
    return {item["id"]: item for item in data.get("controls", [])}


def control_warnings(control):
    warnings = []
    if control.get("operating_status") in {"partial", "not_evidenced"}:
        warnings.append(f"{control['id']} {control['name']}: operating effectiveness is {control.get('operating_status')}.")
    age = control.get("evidence_age_days")
    if age is None:
        warnings.append(f"{control['id']} {control['name']}: no evidence age available.")
    elif age > 90:
        warnings.append(f"{control['id']} {control['name']}: evidence is older than 90 days.")
    if not control.get("owner"):
        warnings.append(f"{control['id']} {control['name']}: missing control owner.")
    return warnings


def build_risk_record(observation, assets, controls):
    asset = assets.get(observation["asset_id"], {"name": "Unknown", "owner": "Unknown", "criticality": "unknown"})
    linked_controls = [controls[cid] for cid in observation.get("linked_controls", []) if cid in controls]
    score = int(observation["likelihood"]) * int(observation["impact"])
    control_names = ", ".join(c["name"] for c in linked_controls) or "No linked control"
    record = {
        "risk_id": observation["id"].replace("O-", "R-"),
        "asset": asset["name"],
        "asset_owner": asset.get("owner", "Unknown"),
        "asset_criticality": asset.get("criticality", "unknown"),
        "event": observation["observation"],
        "weakness": f"Control gap or evidence weakness in: {control_names}",
        "likelihood": observation["likelihood"],
        "impact": observation["impact"],
        "score": score,
        "band": risk_band(score),
        "confidence": observation.get("confidence", "unknown"),
        "evidence": observation.get("evidence_refs", []),
        "owner": asset.get("owner", "Unknown"),
        "treatment": treatment_for(score, observation.get("confidence"), observation.get("privacy_or_legal_flag", False)),
        "uncertainty": "Confirm business impact, control operation, compensating controls, and target-state feasibility.",
        "privacy_or_legal_flag": observation.get("privacy_or_legal_flag", False),
    }
    return record


def validate_risk_record(record):
    missing = []
    for field in REQUIRED_RISK_FIELDS:
        if field not in record or record[field] in (None, "", []):
            missing.append(field)
    if record.get("score", 0) >= 12 and "owner" not in record:
        missing.append("owner")
    return missing


def main():
    data = load_package()
    warnings = []
    warnings.extend(check_scope(data))
    assets = index_assets(data)
    controls = index_controls(data)
    for control in data.get("controls", []):
        warnings.extend(control_warnings(control))
    risks = [build_risk_record(obs, assets, controls) for obs in data.get("observations", [])]
    risks.sort(key=lambda item: item["score"], reverse=True)
    for risk in risks:
        missing = validate_risk_record(risk)
        if missing:
            warnings.append(f"{risk.get('risk_id', 'unknown')}: missing required risk fields: {', '.join(missing)}")

    output = {
        "generated_on": str(date.today()),
        "organization": data.get("organization"),
        "assessment_id": data.get("assessment_id"),
        "risk_register": risks,
        "control_warnings": [w for w in warnings if w.startswith("C-")],
        "readiness_warnings": [w for w in warnings if not w.startswith("C-")],
    }
    Path("risk_register_output.json").write_text(json.dumps(output, indent=2), encoding="utf-8")

    print("Enterprise assessment and risk register review")
    print("Organization:", output["organization"])
    print("Assessment:", output["assessment_id"])
    print("Assets:", len(data.get("assets", [])))
    print("Controls:", len(data.get("controls", [])))
    print("Risk records:", len(risks))
    print()
    print("Top risks:")
    for risk in risks[:3]:
        print(f"- {risk['risk_id']} | {risk['band'].upper()} | score={risk['score']} | owner={risk['owner']} | {risk['asset']}")
    print()
    if warnings:
        print("Warnings to address before final submission:")
        for warning in warnings:
            print("-", warning)
    else:
        print("No warnings found. Risk register is ready for reviewer analysis.")
    print("\nWrote risk_register_output.json")


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

Step 3 — Run the lab locally

Expected result: the script prints asset, control, and risk counts, lists the top risks, writes risk_register_output.json, and flags evidence or ownership warnings that should be addressed before final submission. The warnings are intentional: professional assessment means naming uncertainty, not hiding it.

20. Self-Assessment

  • Can you explain the difference between an observation, a finding, and a risk?
  • Can every risk record name an asset, owner, impact path, evidence source, and treatment decision?
  • Can you justify likelihood and impact without pretending the score is exact science?
  • Can you identify which control gaps affect likelihood, impact, detection, containment, or recovery?
  • Can you separate facts, inferences, assumptions, and unknowns in your evidence notebook?
  • Can you summarize the top three risk themes for an executive audience?
