Week 16 · Lesson 16.1

Capstone Scenario, Charter, and Success Criteria

A capstone fails early when the scope is vague. This lesson turns the final project into a controlled professional engagement: a fictional scenario, a clear authorization boundary, named stakeholders, defined success criteria, evidence rules, exclusions, and a grading rubric that rewards defensible judgment rather than volume.

Estimated reading time90–120 minutes
Difficulty levelMS capstone integration
Required backgroundWeeks 0–15 recommended
Lab modeSafe local charter validation using fictional JSON
Boundary: This lesson is about capstone design, authorization, evidence quality, professional communication, and assessment criteria. It does not authorize scanning, phishing, exploitation, credential collection, cloud access, real incident response, legal conclusions, or testing against real systems.

1. Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define a capstone scenario that is realistic enough to require trade-offs but safe enough for a classroom portfolio.
  • Write an authorization and scope boundary that excludes real-world testing and uncontrolled data collection.
  • Identify stakeholders, decision rights, escalation paths, and evidence owners before analysis begins.
  • Turn high-level course outcomes into measurable success criteria.
  • Build a grading rubric that rewards evidence quality, reasoning, ethics, and communication.
  • Separate project facts, assumptions, inferences, unknowns, and constraints from recommendations.
  • Prepare the foundation for Lesson 16.2’s risk register and enterprise assessment package.

2. Research Basis

This lesson is grounded in current and authoritative cybersecurity practice references. NIST CSF 2.0 organizes outcomes through Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover, which gives the capstone a complete lifecycle structure. NIST SP 800-37 Rev. 2 frames cybersecurity and privacy risk management as a disciplined lifecycle from preparation through monitoring. NIST SP 800-53A Rev. 5 emphasizes assessment procedures and evidence, making it a strong basis for capstone grading beyond yes/no checklists. NIST SP 800-115 supports structured technical assessment planning and reporting. NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 3 connects incident response to CSF 2.0 risk-management activities. CISA Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals provide practical baseline practices, and the NICE Framework provides language for mapping capstone work to cybersecurity tasks and skills.

Research implication: The capstone should not be a single penetration test, a compliance checklist, or a long essay. It should be a portfolio of scoped, evidence-based, role-aware artifacts that support risk decisions.

3. Why the Charter Comes First

The charter is not paperwork. It is the control that prevents a capstone from becoming unsafe, ungradable, or incoherent. Without a charter, learners may collect irrelevant evidence, overstate authority, ignore privacy boundaries, or write recommendations that no real decision-maker could approve.

A useful charter answers seven questions before any analysis begins:

  1. What fictional organization or provided environment is being assessed?
  2. What is explicitly in scope?
  3. What is explicitly out of scope?
  4. Who owns decisions?
  5. What evidence is allowed?
  6. What success criteria will be used?
  7. What constraints, assumptions, and unknowns must be tracked?

4. Scenario Design Principles

A strong scenario has enough complexity to force prioritization, but it does not require learners to invent facts. The course should provide architecture notes, sample inventories, fictional logs, synthetic incident injects, policy excerpts, vendor summaries, and governance notes. Learners should interpret these materials, not go hunting on the public internet for real targets.

Realistic

Includes cloud, identity, endpoints, SaaS, vendors, logging, data sensitivity, business constraints, and leadership pressure.

Bounded

All evidence comes from intentionally provided materials or safe local files. No live third-party systems are needed.

Ambiguous

Contains incomplete telemetry and conflicting priorities so learners must document uncertainty.

Measurable

Success criteria are explicit enough that a reviewer can assess quality without guessing the learner's intent.

5. Fictional Organization: Northstar Community Health Alliance

The capstone scenario uses a fictional regional healthcare-adjacent organization called Northstar Community Health Alliance. The organization provides outpatient scheduling, telehealth coordination, billing support, and community wellness services. This setting is useful because it forces learners to handle identity, privacy, availability, third-party vendors, cloud systems, endpoint telemetry, incident response, and executive communication together.

This organization is fictional. Do not substitute a real clinic, hospital, employer, university, customer, vendor, or public website. If a learner uses a real organization, the project creates privacy, legal, authorization, and safety risks that the course cannot control.

6. Scope Statement

The scope statement should be short enough to be understood and specific enough to constrain behavior. A strong version is:

The Week 16 capstone is limited to fictional records provided by the course: architecture notes, asset inventories, control evidence summaries, alert samples, incident injects, governance notes, and synthetic datasets. Learners may analyze, transform, document, and report on these records locally. Learners may not scan, access, test, investigate, contact, enumerate, or collect evidence from real systems, real people, or third-party services.

Notice that this statement names what is allowed and what is prohibited. Ambiguity is not a professional virtue in assessment work.

7. Authorization Boundary

Authorization is more than permission to “do cybersecurity.” It is permission for a specific activity, against a specific environment, using specific methods, during a specific period, with specific reporting and escalation rules. In this capstone, the authorization boundary is educational and fictional. The learner is authorized to analyze the supplied capstone package only.

AreaAllowedNot allowed
DataFictional course records and learner-created local notesReal personal data, customer records, credentials, production logs
TestingOffline analysis, local scripts over provided files, tabletop reasoningLive scanning, phishing, exploitation, password testing, cloud probing
EvidenceSynthetic alerts, inventories, diagrams, policies, and lab outputsEvidence collected from real third-party systems
ReportingEducational findings and simulated executive briefingsLegal conclusions or real breach-notification advice

8. Stakeholders and Decision Rights

The stakeholder map prevents the final report from pretending every recommendation belongs to the security team. Some decisions are technical, some are operational, some are financial, and some require legal/privacy review.

StakeholderDecision rightsCapstone evidence they need
Executive SponsorRisk acceptance, budget, business-priority trade-offsExecutive summary, top risks, decision requests, residual risk
Security Operations ManagerTriage process, detection improvements, incident-response workflowAlert logic, timelines, evidence sources, response gaps
Digital Services DirectorApplication remediation, release scheduling, system ownershipAsset impact, remediation roadmap, dependencies, verification criteria
Privacy Officer / Legal EscalationPrivacy-risk escalation, regulated-data handling, notification reviewData types, affected records, uncertainty, jurisdictional triggers
Third-Party/Vendor OwnerSupplier follow-up, contract action, assurance evidenceVendor dependency map, evidence gaps, shared-responsibility notes

9. Success Criteria

Success criteria should be observable. “Good analysis” is not observable. “Every risk statement includes an asset, threat/event, weakness/control gap, impact path, owner, evidence, uncertainty, and treatment decision” is observable.

  • The charter states scope, authorization boundary, exclusions, assumptions, and evidence rules.
  • The architecture/context package identifies assets, identities, data types, dependencies, trust boundaries, and business-critical services.
  • The risk register contains clear risk statements with owner, existing controls, evidence, uncertainty, treatment option, and verification method.
  • The incident-response packet separates fact, inference, assumption, and unknown while preserving evidence provenance.
  • The governance brief asks leadership for specific decisions and states residual risk.
  • The portfolio is sanitized and contains no real credentials, secrets, personal data, or private organization information.

10. Evidence Rules

Evidence quality matters more than artifact volume. A screenshot, log snippet, note, or diagram is useful only when a reviewer can understand its source, timestamp, relevance, limitation, and connection to a claim.

Evidence propertyRequired questionWeak answerStrong answer
SourceWhere did it come from?“A log file.”“Synthetic SIEM sample identity_alerts.jsonl, record IDs 14–19.”
TimeWhen did it occur?“Yesterday.”“2026-06-15 09:42–10:08 UTC in the fictional scenario timeline.”
RelevanceWhat claim does it support?“Shows suspicious activity.”“Supports inference that password reset and impossible-travel alerts involve the same user identity.”
LimitationWhat does it not prove?“No limitation.”“Does not prove data access because application audit logs are missing.”

11. Facts, Inferences, Assumptions, and Unknowns

The capstone must distinguish evidence from interpretation. A fact is directly supported by supplied evidence. An inference is a reasoned conclusion from facts. An assumption is a temporary belief used to proceed. An unknown is a missing piece that could change the conclusion.

Professional standard: A precise unknown is better than an invented fact. Good analysts do not hide uncertainty; they manage it.
  • Fact: The fictional alert record shows three failed logins followed by a successful login from a new country.
  • Inference: The sequence is consistent with possible credential misuse.
  • Assumption: The geo-location label in the synthetic dataset is accurate unless corrected by later evidence.
  • Unknown: The dataset does not show whether sensitive records were viewed after login.

12. Capstone Architecture

flowchart TD Charter[Charter and authorization boundary] --> Context[System context and asset map] Context --> Evidence[Evidence notebook] Evidence --> Risks[Risk register] Evidence --> IR[Detection, IR, and forensic drill] Risks --> Roadmap[Remediation and verification roadmap] IR --> Lessons[Lessons learned and recovery improvements] Roadmap --> Brief[Governance and executive brief] Lessons --> Brief Brief --> Portfolio[Final portfolio and defense] Charter --> Ethics[Ethics, privacy, and legal escalation rules] Ethics --> Evidence Ethics --> Brief

The charter feeds every later deliverable. If scope, evidence rules, and stakeholder decisions are unclear, later findings become harder to defend.

13. Grading Rubric

AreaStrong workWeak work
Scope and authorizationClear boundary, exclusions, safe methods, and escalation rulesVague permission or implied access to real systems
Scenario understandingBusiness mission, services, assets, data, identities, vendors, and dependencies are connectedTechnical list with no business context
Evidence disciplineClaims are traceable to supplied evidence and limitations are documentedUnsupported conclusions and copied artifacts
Risk reasoningImpact, likelihood, exposure, control strength, and uncertainty are explainedSeverity labels without reasoning
CommunicationExecutive, technical, and governance audiences receive appropriate detailOne generic report for all audiences
Ethics and privacyMinimal data handling, sanitized portfolio, and legal/privacy escalation flagsUnnecessary data collection or legal overclaiming

14. Charter Deliverable Template

capstone-charter.md

1. Scenario name
2. Organization mission and business context
3. Authorization statement
4. In-scope materials
5. Out-of-scope activities
6. Stakeholders and decision rights
7. Evidence handling rules
8. Assumptions and constraints
9. Success criteria
10. Review and escalation process
11. Portfolio sanitization statement

The final charter should be short, explicit, and reviewable. It should not bury authorization and exclusions inside a long narrative.

15. Common Failure Points

  • Scope creep: adding real systems because the fictional scenario feels incomplete.
  • Artifact dumping: including many tables and screenshots without claims or interpretation.
  • Severity theater: assigning “critical” labels without impact path, ownership, or evidence.
  • Compliance overclaiming: saying the organization violates a law instead of identifying escalation triggers.
  • Privacy neglect: collecting or displaying unnecessary identifiers in a portfolio.
  • No decision request: giving leadership findings without asking for approval, funding, acceptance, or prioritization.

16. Portfolio Sanitization Rules

The capstone should become a professional portfolio artifact, but only after sanitization. A portfolio should never contain real secrets, API keys, tokens, credentials, customer records, personal data, employer documents, private screenshots, proprietary diagrams, private incident details, or vendor-confidential materials.

For this course, use only the fictional organization name and fictional datasets. If you later adapt the structure for real work, replace live details with summaries, mock records, generalized architecture, and explicit permission from the relevant owner.

17. Starting Capstone Package

Lesson 16.1 starts the portfolio with three artifacts:

  • Scenario file: a fictional JSON record describing the organization, scope, stakeholders, services, and success criteria.
  • Charter file: a short markdown document summarizing authorization, scope, exclusions, assumptions, and evidence rules.
  • Readiness review: a safe local script that checks whether the scenario has common charter elements before deeper analysis begins.

18. Review Questions

  1. What exact activity is authorized in the capstone?
  2. What activities are explicitly out of scope?
  3. Who can accept residual risk in the fictional scenario?
  4. What evidence can support a finding, and what evidence is prohibited?
  5. How will you distinguish fact from inference in the final portfolio?
  6. What would make a recommendation decision-ready for leadership?
  7. Which privacy or legal questions should be escalated instead of answered by the analyst?

Step 2 — Create capstone_charter_check.py

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
Safe local lab for Lesson 16.1.
Validates a fictional capstone charter and scenario package.
It performs no network activity, scanning, authentication, cloud access, exploitation, or real assessment.
"""
import json
from pathlib import Path

REQUIRED_TOP_LEVEL = [
    "organization", "scenario_id", "mission", "scope_statement", "out_of_scope",
    "critical_services", "stakeholders", "success_criteria"
]
REQUIRED_EXCLUSIONS = {
    "real scanning", "real phishing", "credential collection", "production-system access",
    "third-party testing", "real patient/customer data", "legal conclusions"
}
REQUIRED_STAKEHOLDER_ROLES = {
    "Executive Sponsor", "Security Owner", "System Owner", "Privacy/Legal Escalation"
}
MIN_SUCCESS_CRITERIA = 5


def load_scenario(path="capstone_scenario.json"):
    return json.loads(Path(path).read_text(encoding="utf-8"))


def check_required_fields(data):
    return [field for field in REQUIRED_TOP_LEVEL if field not in data or data[field] in (None, "", [])]


def check_scope_boundary(data):
    scope = data.get("scope_statement", "").lower()
    warnings = []
    if "fictional" not in scope and "provided" not in scope:
        warnings.append("Scope statement should explicitly limit work to fictional/provided course records.")
    exclusions = {str(item).lower() for item in data.get("out_of_scope", [])}
    missing = sorted(REQUIRED_EXCLUSIONS - exclusions)
    if missing:
        warnings.append("Missing out-of-scope exclusions: " + ", ".join(missing))
    return warnings


def check_services(data):
    warnings = []
    for service in data.get("critical_services", []):
        name = service.get("name", "Unnamed service")
        for field in ["business_impact", "data_types", "dependencies", "recovery_target"]:
            if not service.get(field):
                warnings.append(f"{name}: missing {field}")
        if "confirm" not in str(service.get("recovery_target", "")).lower() and "manual workaround" not in str(service.get("recovery_target", "")).lower():
            warnings.append(f"{name}: recovery target should identify validation or business confirmation need.")
    return warnings


def check_stakeholders(data):
    roles = {item.get("role") for item in data.get("stakeholders", [])}
    missing = sorted(REQUIRED_STAKEHOLDER_ROLES - roles)
    warnings = []
    if missing:
        warnings.append("Missing stakeholder role(s): " + ", ".join(missing))
    for item in data.get("stakeholders", []):
        if not item.get("decision_rights"):
            warnings.append(f"{item.get('role', 'Unknown role')}: decision_rights are missing")
    return warnings


def check_success_criteria(data):
    criteria = data.get("success_criteria", [])
    warnings = []
    if len(criteria) < MIN_SUCCESS_CRITERIA:
        warnings.append(f"Only {len(criteria)} success criteria found; expected at least {MIN_SUCCESS_CRITERIA}.")
    joined = " ".join(criteria).lower()
    for keyword in ["authorization", "risk", "evidence", "owner", "executive", "sanitized"]:
        if keyword not in joined:
            warnings.append(f"Success criteria should include the concept: {keyword}")
    return warnings


def main():
    data = load_scenario()
    findings = []
    missing = check_required_fields(data)
    if missing:
        findings.append("Missing required top-level fields: " + ", ".join(missing))
    findings.extend(check_scope_boundary(data))
    findings.extend(check_services(data))
    findings.extend(check_stakeholders(data))
    findings.extend(check_success_criteria(data))

    print("Capstone charter readiness review")
    print("Organization:", data.get("organization", "unknown"))
    print("Scenario:", data.get("scenario_id", "unknown"))
    print("Critical services:", len(data.get("critical_services", [])))
    print("Stakeholders:", len(data.get("stakeholders", [])))
    print("Success criteria:", len(data.get("success_criteria", [])))
    print()

    if findings:
        print("Warnings / items to fix:")
        for item in findings:
            print("-", item)
    else:
        print("No blocking charter warnings found. Next: build the risk register and evidence notebook.")


if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()

Step 3 — Run the lab locally

python3 capstone_charter_check.py

Expected result: the script should report the organization, scenario ID, service count, stakeholder count, success-criteria count, and no blocking charter warnings. If you intentionally remove an exclusion, stakeholder, or success criterion, the script should warn you. That is the point: the capstone should fail early when its authorization boundary is weak.

20. Self-Assessment

  • Can you explain the capstone authorization boundary in one sentence?
  • Can you list at least five out-of-scope activities without looking?
  • Can you name which stakeholder owns risk acceptance, remediation scheduling, detection improvement, and privacy/legal escalation?
  • Can you write one success criterion that a reviewer can objectively check?
  • Can you identify one assumption and one unknown that could change your later risk register?
